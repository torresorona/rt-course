# Acid Base Regulation — Complete Study Guide
## Pulmonary Anatomy & Physiology

---

## Topic 1: The Hydrolysis Equation

All of acid-base physiology in the respiratory system begins with a single reversible chemical reaction known as the hydrolysis equation. It looks like this: carbon dioxide plus water reversibly forms carbonic acid, which then reversibly dissociates into bicarbonate and a hydrogen ion.

This equation is the foundation for understanding how the lungs and kidneys work together to maintain acid-base balance. Every time you exhale CO₂, you are pulling this equation to the left. Every time your cells produce CO₂ as a metabolic waste product, you are pushing it to the right. The balance of this reaction determines the pH of your blood.

---

## Topic 2: Understanding pH

pH stands for "potential of hydrogen." It is a logarithmic scale used to measure the concentration of hydrogen ions in a solution. The normal pH of human blood is tightly maintained between 7.35 and 7.45. This corresponds to an incredibly small hydrogen ion concentration of approximately 0.00004 milliequivalents per liter.

When pH drops below 7.35, the condition is called acidosis, meaning there is an excess of hydrogen ions in the blood. When pH rises above 7.45, the condition is called alkalosis, meaning there is a deficit of hydrogen ions. Even small deviations from this narrow range can have serious consequences for enzyme function, nerve conduction, and overall cellular activity.

The body maintains multiple systems to regulate H⁺ concentration, but the two most important organs for this task are the lungs and the kidneys. Each handles pH regulation on a different time scale.

---

## Topic 3: CO₂ Regulation and the Respiratory Response

Carbon dioxide plays a starring role in acid-base balance because of its position in the hydrolysis equation. When CO₂ levels rise, the equation shifts to the right. More carbonic acid forms, which dissociates into more bicarbonate and more hydrogen ions. The extra hydrogen ions lower the pH, producing acidosis.

When CO₂ levels fall, the opposite happens. The equation shifts to the left, consuming hydrogen ions and raising the pH, producing alkalosis.

The body regulates CO₂ primarily through exhalation. The pulmonary system can adjust CO₂ elimination within seconds to minutes, making it the rapid-response arm of acid-base regulation. If you are becoming acidotic, you breathe faster and deeper to blow off more CO₂, shifting the equation to the left and raising pH. If you are becoming alkalotic, you breathe more slowly and shallowly to retain CO₂, shifting the equation to the right and lowering pH. This is called respiratory compensation.

---

## Topic 4: Bicarbonate Regulation and the Renal Response

Bicarbonate, or HCO₃⁻, is the base component of the carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffer system. It is regulated primarily by the kidneys. Unlike the rapid pulmonary response, renal compensation takes hours to days.

The kidneys adjust bicarbonate and hydrogen ion levels based on the body's needs. When there is too much hydrogen ion in the blood (acidosis), the kidneys respond by excreting more hydrogen ions into the urine and reabsorbing more bicarbonate back into the blood. This helps raise the pH back toward normal.

When there is too little hydrogen ion in the blood (alkalosis), the kidneys do the opposite. They retain hydrogen ions and excrete more bicarbonate in the urine, which helps lower the pH back toward normal.

This renal mechanism is slower but more powerful and sustained than the respiratory response. It allows the body to fine-tune its acid-base status over the long term.

## Topic 5: Protein Buffer Systems

Buffer systems are chemicals that react with hydrogen ions to remove them from solution, reducing their impact on pH. They act as a chemical sponge for excess H⁺.

The protein buffer system is one of the most important buffer systems in the body. The two key proteins involved are hemoglobin and albumin. Both of these proteins have the ability to reversibly bind hydrogen ions. This means they can pick up excess H⁺ when there is too much, and release H⁺ when there is too little.

Hemoglobin is particularly important as a buffer. Red blood cells contain very high concentrations of hemoglobin, and as blood circulates through the tissues, hemoglobin picks up the H⁺ that is produced when CO₂ dissociates in the red blood cell. This buffering action allows the blood to carry large amounts of CO₂ without dramatically changing pH. When the blood reaches the lungs, hemoglobin releases the H⁺, allowing the reaction to reverse and CO₂ to be exhaled.

---

## Topic 6: Carbonic Acid-Bicarbonate Buffer System

The carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffer system is the most important buffer system in the extracellular fluid. It is based entirely on the hydrolysis equation and works in close partnership with the lungs and kidneys.

The system works because carbonic acid is a weak acid. It does not fully dissociate in solution, which means it can exist in equilibrium with both its reactants (CO₂ and H₂O) and its products (HCO₃⁻ and H⁺). This equilibrium can be shifted in either direction depending on the body's needs.

One of the key benefits of this system is that the body can reverse the reaction near lung tissue. Carbonic acid is converted back into CO₂ and water, and the CO₂ is exhaled. This gives the body a way to directly eliminate acid in the form of a gas.

The second major benefit is that the kidneys can handle the base product of the reaction. If the body needs more alkalinity, the kidneys reabsorb bicarbonate. If the body needs more acidity, the kidneys excrete bicarbonate. This dual control — rapid respiratory elimination of CO₂ plus slower renal regulation of HCO₃⁻ — gives the body a remarkably flexible system for maintaining pH within its narrow normal range.

## Key Terms to Remember

- **Hydrolysis Equation**: CO₂ + H₂O ↔ H₂CO₃ ↔ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺
- **pH**: Potential of hydrogen; normal range 7.35–7.45 (≈0.00004 mEq/L H⁺)
- **Acidosis**: pH < 7.35, excess H⁺
- **Alkalosis**: pH > 7.45, deficit of H⁺
- **CO₂ Regulation**: Increased CO₂ → more H⁺ → acidosis. Decreased CO₂ → less H⁺ → alkalosis. Rapidly adjusted via exhalation.
- **HCO₃⁻ Regulation**: Renal — slow but powerful. Excess H⁺ → kidneys excrete H⁺ and retain HCO₃⁻. Deficit H⁺ → kidneys retain H⁺ and excrete HCO₃⁻.
- **Protein Buffer System**: Hemoglobin and albumin reversibly bind H⁺
- **Carbonic Acid-Bicarbonate Buffer System**: Based on hydrolysis equation; carries CO₂ as HCO₃⁻; weak acid H₂CO₃ can dissociate to CO₂ + H₂O for exhalation
- **Respiratory Compensation**: Rapid adjustment via changing ventilation rate
- **Renal Compensation**: Slow adjustment via H⁺ excretion and HCO₃⁻ reabsorption
