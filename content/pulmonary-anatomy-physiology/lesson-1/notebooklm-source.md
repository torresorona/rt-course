# Structure and Functional Dynamics of the Respiratory System — Complete Study Guide

## Pulmonary Anatomy & Physiology

---

## Topic 1: Structural and Functional Divisions

Let's start with the big picture. The respiratory system can be divided two ways: structurally and functionally.

Structurally, we separate it into the upper respiratory tract and the lower respiratory tract. The upper tract includes the nose, nasal cavity, mouth, and pharynx. The lower tract includes the larynx, trachea, bronchi, and alveoli.

Functionally, we think about it differently. The conducting zone includes everything from the nose down through the terminal bronchioles. These are passageways — they conduct air but don't participate in gas exchange. The respiratory zone is where the actual gas exchange happens: respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, alveolar sacs, and alveoli. Remember: no gas exchange occurs in the conducting zone. Only the respiratory zone.

## Topic 2: Epithelial Tissue Types

The airways are lined with different types of epithelium depending on location. We classify epithelial tissue by two features: the number of cell layers (simple = one layer, stratified = multiple layers) and the cell shape (cuboidal or columnar).

Here are the types you need to know:

Simple squamous epithelium looks like fried eggs under the microscope. It's found in the alveoli and capillary bed where gas exchange needs to happen — thin is fast.

Simple cuboidal epithelium is cube-shaped and found in the respiratory bronchioles.

Simple columnar epithelium lines the bronchioles.

Stratified epithelium has multiple layers and is found in the oral cavity where protection matters more than diffusion.

Now here's the most important one for the airways: pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium, or PCCE. It's called pseudostratified because it looks like it has multiple layers, but it really doesn't — all cells touch the basement membrane. It's the primary airway epithelium lining the nasopharynx, larynx, trachea, and bronchi.

PCCE has two key features: cilia and goblet cells. Cilia are tiny hair-like projections — some are motile, some are not. Goblet cells secrete mucus. Together they form the mucociliary escalator. Here's how it works: the gel layer sits on top and traps inhaled particles. Below it is the sol layer, a more watery layer that allows the cilia to beat. The cilia sweep everything toward the mouth, and each cell has about 200 cilia working together. This is one of the lungs' primary defense mechanisms.

## Topic 3: Upper Respiratory Anatomy

The external nose is supported by bone and hyaline cartilage. The nostrils are called nares.

The nasal cavity is lined with PCCE and has three important jobs: warming, moistening, and filtering inspired air.

Inside the nasal cavity you'll find the conchae — three bony shelves that increase surface area and create air turbulence. The spaces underneath each concha are called meatuses.

The sinuses are air-filled spaces in the skull bones: maxillary, frontal, ethmoid, and sphenoid. They reduce skull weight and modify voice quality. That's why your voice sounds different when you have a sinus infection.

The oral cavity contains the hard palate (bony front part of the roof of the mouth), the soft palate (muscular back part), and the uvula (that dangling thing at the back of your throat).

The pharynx has three divisions. The nasopharynx is the uppermost part behind the nasal cavity — it's lined with PCCE and contains the opening of the Eustachian tube. The oropharynx is behind the mouth — it's lined with stratified squamous epithelium and is a common passage for both air and food. The laryngopharynx is the lowest part, also lined with stratified squamous epithelium.

## Topic 4: Lower Respiratory Anatomy

The larynx contains nine cartilages. The thyroid cartilage is the largest — it's the Adam's apple. The epiglottis is leaf-shaped and covers the glottis during swallowing to prevent food from entering the airway. The cricoid cartilage is a complete ring — it's the landmark for tracheotomy because it's the only complete cartilage ring in the airway. The arytenoids are triangular and attach to the vocal cords, controlling their movement. The corniculate cartilages are horn-shaped, and the cuneiform cartilages are club-shaped.

The vallecula is the space between the epiglottis and the base of the tongue — it's an important landmark for endotracheal intubation. The glottis contains the vocal cords and mediates the cough reflex.

The trachea runs anterior to the esophagus from the larynx down to the level of T5. It's about 12 centimeters long with 16 to 20 C-shaped cartilage rings that give it a D-shaped cross-section. The C-shape is important because the posterior wall needs to be flexible to allow the esophagus to expand during swallowing.

The carina is where the trachea bifurcates into the left and right main bronchi. It's packed with cough receptors — touching it triggers an intense cough reflex. It's also a key landmark for ET tube placement.

The right primary bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left. That's why aspirated objects tend to end up in the right lung. Each lung has one primary bronchus. The secondary or lobar bronchi branch into the lung lobes: three on the right, two on the left. The tertiary or segmental bronchi further subdivide: ten on each side.

As the airways branch, cartilage changes from C-shaped rings to irregular plates and then disappears entirely in the bronchioles. Bronchioles are lined with ciliated simple columnar epithelium and have smooth muscle in their walls. Terminal bronchioles are the last part of the conducting zone — they're nonciliated and contain Clara cells, which secrete a protective substance.

Collateral ventilation is possible through accessory pathways: Channels of Martin connect terminal bronchioles to respiratory bronchioles, Lambert channels connect terminal bronchioles to alveoli, and Pores of Kohn connect adjacent alveoli directly.

## Topic 5: Thoracic Cavity and Lungs

Ribs 1 through 7 are true ribs — they attach directly to the sternum. Ribs 8 through 12 are false ribs — they attach indirectly or not at all. Ribs 11 and 12 are floating ribs — they don't attach to the sternum at all.

The mediastinum is the space between the two pleural cavities. It contains the heart, great vessels, trachea, esophagus, and other structures.

The left lung has a cardiac notch to accommodate the heart and is about 10 percent smaller than the right lung. The right lung is shorter because the liver pushes up on it from below. Each lung has an apex (top), a base (bottom), and a hilum (where structures enter and exit).

The pleural membrane has two layers. The parietal pleura lines the thoracic cavity and is pain-sensitive — this is what hurts in pleurisy. The visceral pleura surrounds the lung itself and is pain-insensitive. Between them is the pleural cavity containing about 18 milliliters of serous fluid that reduces friction. The costophrenic angle is the recess where the diaphragm meets the ribs — it's the lowest point in the pleural cavity. Thoracentesis is the procedure of draining fluid from this space.

Fissures divide the lungs into lobes. The right lung has an oblique fissure and a horizontal fissure, creating three lobes: superior, middle, and inferior. The left lung has only an oblique fissure, creating two lobes: superior and inferior. Each lung has ten bronchopulmonary segments, each with its own segmental bronchus and blood supply.

## Topic 6: Respiratory Zone and Pulmonary Blood Flow

The respiratory zone is where gas exchange happens. Respiratory bronchioles start with simple cuboidal epithelium and transition to simple squamous as they progress. Alveolar ducts are lined with simple squamous epithelium and lead to alveolar sacs, which are clusters of alveoli.

The alveoli contain three cell types. Type I cells make up 90 to 95 percent of the alveolar surface — they're extremely thin simple squamous cells designed for gas exchange. Type II cells make up the remaining 5 to 10 percent and secrete surfactant, a phospholipid-protein mixture that reduces surface tension. Type III cells are alveolar macrophages that act as phagocytes, cleaning up debris and pathogens.

Surfactant is critical. It reduces surface tension, which increases lung compliance (makes the lungs easier to inflate), decreases elastic recoil, and helps regulate alveolar size by stabilizing smaller alveoli. Without enough surfactant, alveoli collapse — this is called atelectasis. Surfactant deficiency is a major problem in premature infants (respiratory distress syndrome).

The respiratory membrane is the thin barrier between alveolar air and capillary blood. It's only 0.5 microns thick, and its total surface area is 70 to 100 square meters — about the size of a tennis court.

The pulmonary circulation is unique. Deoxygenated blood goes from the right heart through the pulmonary arteries to the lungs, gets oxygenated, and returns through the pulmonary veins to the left heart. The bronchial arteries branch from the aorta and supply oxygenated blood to the lung tissue itself. And here's a key difference from systemic circulation: hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction. In the systemic circulation, hypoxia causes vasodilation (to deliver more oxygen). In the pulmonary circulation, hypoxia causes vasoconstriction — the lungs shunt blood away from poorly ventilated areas to match ventilation and perfusion.

## Topic 7: Pulmonary Ventilation

Air moves from areas of higher pressure to areas of lower pressure — it's that simple.

Inhalation is an active process. The diaphragm contracts and descends — about one centimeter during quiet breathing and up to ten centimeters during strenuous breathing. It's responsible for about 75 percent of air movement. The external intercostals lift the rib cage, contributing the other 25 percent. During forced inhalation, accessory muscles kick in: the sternocleidomastoid, scalenes, and pectoralis minor.

Exhalation during quiet breathing is completely passive, driven by elastic recoil. The lungs contain elastic fibers, and surface tension also pulls the alveoli inward. When the diaphragm relaxes, it moves upward and air flows out. During exercise, exhalation becomes active — the abdominal muscles and internal intercostals contract to push air out faster.

Intrapleural pressure is normally negative (about 756 mmHg relative to atmospheric). During inhalation, it becomes even more negative (about 754 mmHg). If the pleural cavity is opened to the atmosphere, intrapleural pressure equalizes and the lung collapses — this is a pneumothorax.

LaPlace's Law says that pressure inside a sphere is proportional to wall tension divided by radius. For alveoli, this means smaller alveoli would tend to collapse into larger ones without surfactant to stabilize them.

Compliance is how easily the lungs expand. High compliance means the lungs inflate easily — think emphysema, where elastic tissue is destroyed. Low compliance means stiff lungs — think ARDS, pulmonary fibrosis, pulmonary edema, or surfactant deficiency. Restrictive lung diseases are characterized by decreased compliance and increased elasticity. Obstructive lung diseases show increased compliance and decreased elasticity.

Airway resistance, or Raw, comes mostly from the airways (80 percent) with the remaining 20 percent from lung tissue. Poiseuille's Law says resistance equals 8 times the viscosity times length, divided by pi times the radius to the fourth power. The key clinical point: if you halve the radius, resistance increases 16 times. This is why bronchoconstriction is so dangerous. Resistance decreases during inhalation (airways are pulled open) and increases during exhalation. Sympathetic stimulation causes bronchodilation, which is why beta-agonists are such effective asthma medications. Most airway resistance is located in the first five generations of airways — the medium-sized bronchi, not the tiny ones.

## Topic 8: Lung Volumes and Capacities

Tidal volume (Vt) is the volume of air moved in or out with each breath during quiet breathing. Minute ventilation is the respiratory rate times tidal volume. Anatomic dead space is roughly one milliliter per pound of ideal body weight, which works out to about 30 percent of tidal volume. Alveolar ventilation is tidal volume minus dead space — this is the air that actually participates in gas exchange.

A spirometer can measure most, but not all, lung volumes. The four lung volumes are: tidal volume (Vt), inspiratory reserve volume (IRV — the extra air you can inhale above a normal breath), expiratory reserve volume (ERV — the extra air you can forcefully exhale), and residual volume (RV — air remaining in the lungs after maximal exhalation, which cannot be measured by spirometry).

Lung capacities are combinations of two or more lung volumes. Inspiratory capacity (IC) is tidal volume plus inspiratory reserve volume — it's the maximum you can inhale from a normal resting exhalation. Functional residual capacity (FRC) is expiratory reserve volume plus residual volume — it's the amount of air left in the lungs at the end of a normal breath. Vital capacity (VC) is inspiratory reserve volume plus tidal volume plus expiratory reserve volume — the maximum amount you can exhale after a maximum inhalation. Total lung capacity (TLC) is the sum of all four volumes.

Normal values depend on age, height, sex, and race. This is why pulmonary function tests use reference equations that account for these factors.
