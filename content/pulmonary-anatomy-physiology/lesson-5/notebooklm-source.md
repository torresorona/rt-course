## Topic 1: Structure and Functional Dynamics of the Respiratory System (Unit I)

The respiratory system is divided structurally into upper respiratory (nose, nasal cavity, mouth, pharynx) and lower respiratory (larynx, trachea, bronchi, alveoli). Functionally, it is divided into the conducting zone (nose through terminal bronchioles) and the respiratory zone (respiratory bronchioles through alveoli), where gas exchange occurs.

The primary airway epithelium is Pseudostratified Ciliated Columnar Epithelium (PCCE), found in the nasopharynx, larynx, trachea, and bronchi. PCCE features cilia (motile and non-motile) and goblet cells that secrete mucus. The mucociliary escalator traps particles in the gel layer while cilia sweep through the sol layer toward the mouth.

The upper respiratory system includes the nasal cavity (warms, moistens, filters air), conchae and meatuses that create turbulence for maximum mucosal exposure, sinuses that reduce skull weight and modify voice, and the pharynx divided into nasopharynx (PCCE, Eustachian tubes), oropharynx (stratified squamous), and laryngopharynx (stratified squamous).

The lower respiratory system includes the larynx with 9 cartilages: thyroid (Adam's apple), epiglottis (covers glottis during swallowing), cricoid (complete ring, tracheotomy landmark), and paired arytenoids, corniculate, and cuneiform cartilages. The trachea has 16-20 C-shaped rings, and the carina is the bifurcation point that triggers a reflex cough. The right mainstem bronchus is wider, shorter, and more vertical than the left. Cartilage transitions from C-shaped rings to plates to none in the bronchioles. Clara cells in terminal bronchioles protect against toxins. Pores of Kohn provide collateral ventilation between alveoli.

The thoracic cavity houses the lungs and mediastinum. The left lung has a cardiac notch and is 10% smaller; the right lung is shorter due to the liver. The pleural membrane has a pain-sensitive parietal layer lining the chest wall and an insensitive visceral layer surrounding the lungs, with ~18 mL of serous fluid between them. The right lung has 3 lobes (upper, middle, lower) divided by oblique and horizontal fissures; the left lung has 2 lobes divided by the oblique fissure. Each lung has 10 bronchopulmonary segments.

The respiratory zone contains respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, and alveolar sacs. Three alveolar cell types: Type I (90-95%, gas exchange surface), Type II (5-10%, secrete surfactant), and Type III macrophages (phagocytes). Surfactant reduces surface tension, increases compliance, and decreases elastic recoil. Surfactant deficiency causes atelectasis. The respiratory membrane is 0.5 microns thick with ~70-100 m² surface area.

Pulmonary circulation: deoxygenated blood from the right ventricle flows through pulmonary arteries to the capillary bed, and oxygenated blood returns via pulmonary veins to the left atrium. Bronchial arteries from the aorta supply the lung tissue. Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction diverts blood from poorly ventilated areas.

Ventilation occurs via pressure gradients. Inhalation is active: the diaphragm (innervated by phrenic nerve C3-C5) descends ~1 cm during quiet breathing, providing 75% of air movement; external intercostals provide 25%; accessory muscles engage during forced breathing. Quiet exhalation is passive via elastic recoil. During exercise, exhalation becomes active using abdominal and internal intercostal muscles. Intrapleural pressure is subatmospheric (~756 mmHg). Pneumothorax is air in the pleural space.

Three factors influence ventilation: alveolar surface tension (described by LaPlace's Law), compliance (high in emphysema, low in ARDS/fibrosis/edema), and airway resistance (described by Poiseuille's Law, where halving radius increases resistance 16-fold). Sympathetic stimulation causes bronchodilation. Most resistance is in the first 5 generations of the tracheobronchial tree.

Lung volumes: Tidal Volume (Vt), Inspiratory Reserve Volume (IRV), Expiratory Reserve Volume (ERV), and Residual Volume (RV). Lung capacities: Inspiratory Capacity (IC = Vt + IRV), Functional Residual Capacity (FRC = ERV + RV), Vital Capacity (VC = IRV + Vt + ERV), Total Lung Capacity (TLC = sum of all four). Minute ventilation = RR × Vt. Anatomic dead space = 1 mL/lb IBW. Alveolar ventilation = Vt - dead space.

## Topic 2: Gas Exchange (Unit II)

Kinetic energy drives gas movement. Temperature scales: Fahrenheit, Celsius, and Kelvin (used in gas laws). Conversions: C = 5/9(F-32), K = C+273. STPD: 0°C, 760 mmHg, 22.4 L/mol. BTPS: 37°C, 760 mmHg, 27.11 L/mol.

All gases exert pressure. Pascal's Principle: P = F/A. Barometric pressure is measured with a barometer; standard is 760 mmHg (1 mmHg = 1 Torr). Archimedes' Principle explains buoyancy in gases. Poiseuille's Law: ΔP = 8ηL/πr⁴, where driving pressure increases with viscosity, tube length, and flow, and dramatically with decreased radius. Laminar flow requires less pressure; turbulent flow creates irregular eddy currents requiring more pressure.

Avogadro's Law: equal volumes of gases at same T/P contain equal molecules. At STPD, 1 mol of any gas = 22.4 L. Density = mass/volume. O₂ density = 1.43 g/L, N₂ density = 1.25 g/L at STPD. Atmospheric air density = 1.29 g/L. Heliox therapy uses a helium-oxygen blend to reduce density and improve flow.

Ideal Gas Law: PV = nRT. Boyle's Law (P₁V₁ = P₂V₂): pressure and volume inversely related, applies to compliance measurements. Charles' Law (V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂): volume and temperature directly related. Gay-Lussac's Law (P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂): pressure and temperature directly related. Combined Gas Law: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂.

Red blood cells are biconcave discs without nuclei or mitochondria, maximizing gas transport. Hemoglobin consists of globin (4 polypeptide chains) and heme (iron Fe²⁺ that binds O₂). Each Hb binds 4 O₂ molecules. O₂ transport: 98.5% as oxyhemoglobin, 1.5% dissolved. CO₂ transport: 70% as bicarbonate, 23% as carbaminohemoglobin, 7% dissolved. CO has 200× greater affinity for hemoglobin than O₂. Methemoglobin (Fe³⁺) cannot bind oxygen.

Graham's Law: diffusion rate is inversely proportional to the square root of molecular weight. Dalton's Law: total pressure equals sum of partial pressures. Pgas = Gas% × pB. The oxygen cascade describes O₂ movement from atmosphere to tissues. Henry's Law: gas dissolved in liquid = solubility coefficient × partial pressure. CO₂ is more soluble than O₂ (0.510 vs 0.023 mL/dL). Temperature affects solubility: high T decreases, low T increases.

External respiration exchanges O₂ and CO₂ across the alveolar-capillary membrane. Internal respiration exchanges gases between systemic capillaries and tissues. Four factors affecting gas exchange rate: partial pressure gradient, surface area, diffusion distance, and molecular weight/solubility. Hypoxia types: hypoxic (low PaO₂), anemic (low hemoglobin), ischemic (reduced blood flow), and histotoxic (toxicity affecting cellular utilization).

## Topic 3: Regulation of Breathing (Unit III)

The respiratory center is in the medulla oblongata and pons. The medullary rhythmicity center controls basic rhythm: the inspiratory area sends signals for ~2 seconds to the diaphragm (via phrenic nerve C3-C5) and external intercostals, then stops for passive exhalation. The expiratory area activates only during forceful breathing, engaging internal intercostals and abdominal muscles.

The pons modifies medullary output. The pneumotaxic center (upper pons) helps turn off inspiration to prevent overinflation, shortening inhalation and increasing rate when active. The apneustic center prolongs inhalation for deep breaths, but the pneumotaxic center can override it.

The cerebral cortex allows voluntary breathing control. Breath holding is limited by CO₂ and H⁺ buildup, which eventually overrides voluntary control.

Central chemoreceptors in/near the medulla respond to H⁺ and PCO₂ changes in CSF. Peripheral chemoreceptors in the aortic arch and carotid bodies respond to PO₂, PCO₂, and H⁺ in blood, increasing ventilation in response to hypoxia.

Proprioceptors in muscles, tendons, joints, and the inner ear stimulate increased breathing rate and depth at the onset of exercise, even before chemoreceptor changes occur.

## Topic 4: Acid Base Regulation (Unit IV)

The hydrolysis equation: CO₂ + H₂O ↔ H₂CO₃ ↔ HCO₃⁻ + H⁺. pH measures hydrogen ion concentration. Normal range: 7.35-7.45. Acidosis = low pH (high H⁺), alkalosis = high pH (low H⁺). The lungs and kidneys regulate pH.

CO₂ regulation: increased CO₂ shifts the equation right, producing more H⁺ (acidosis). Decreased CO₂ shifts left, reducing H⁺ (alkalosis). CO₂ is rapidly regulated through exhalation by the lungs.

HCO₃⁻ regulation is slower, handled by the kidneys. Excess H⁺ triggers renal excretion of H⁺ and retention of HCO₃⁻. Insufficient H⁺ triggers H⁺ retention and HCO₃⁻ excretion.

The protein buffer system uses hemoglobin and albumin to reversibly bind H⁺, buffering pH changes. The carbonic acid-bicarbonate buffer system, based on the hydrolysis equation, allows the body to carry CO₂ as bicarbonate and to excrete CO₂ through the lungs or H⁺ through the kidneys.
