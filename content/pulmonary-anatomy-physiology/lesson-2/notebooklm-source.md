## Topic 1: Energy and Temperature

Let's start with the basics of how gases behave. Gases have really weak molecular forces, which means their molecules are in constant random motion. They don't have a fixed volume or shape — they'll expand to fill whatever container they're in. Think about the air around you right now: it's a gas, and it's everywhere.

Internal energy comes in two forms: potential energy (based on position) and kinetic energy (based on motion). For gases, kinetic energy is what we focus on, because gas molecules are always bouncing around. Temperature is basically a measurement of how much of that energy is being released as heat.

You'll run into three temperature scales in medicine: Fahrenheit, Celsius, and Kelvin. Here's the critical point: **Kelvin is used in ALL gas laws.** If you try to use Celsius in a gas law equation, your answer will be wrong. The conversions you need to know are: Celsius = 5/9(F − 32), Kelvin = Celsius + 273, and Fahrenheit = (9/5 × Celsius) + 32.

Two important abbreviations you'll see: **STPD** stands for Standard Temperature Pressure Dry — that's 0°C and 760 mmHg, where one mole of gas occupies 22.4 liters. **BTPS** stands for Body Temperature Pressure Saturated — that's 37°C and 760 mmHg, where one mole occupies 27.11 liters. BTPS is more relevant clinically because it reflects conditions inside the human body.

## Topic 2: Gas Pressure

Here's a fundamental concept: every gas exerts pressure. Pascal's Principle tells us that pressure equals force divided by area (P = F/A), and that a gas or liquid exerts pressure equally in all directions within a container. When we talk about the pressure of dissolved gases — like oxygen dissolved in blood — we use the term "tension" instead of pressure.

You'll see pressure measured in several units: Pascals (Pa), pounds per square inch (psi), millimeters of mercury (mmHg), atmospheres (atm), and Torr. Here's an easy one: 1 mmHg equals 1 Torr — they're the same thing. A barometer measures atmospheric pressure, and standard atmospheric pressure is 760 mmHg. That's your baseline.

Archimedes' Principle says buoyancy applies in both liquids AND gases — this is important for understanding how aerosol generators work. Then there's Poiseuille's Law, which is huge for respiratory therapy: ΔP = 8ηL/πr⁴. This tells you the driving pressure gradient needed for gas flow. The key clinical takeaway: if you halve the radius of a tube, you need 16 times more pressure to maintain the same flow. That's why a small amount of mucus swelling or bronchoconstriction can dramatically impact breathing.

Viscosity is the force that opposes flow. When flow is **laminar**, the gas moves in discrete streamlines and less pressure is needed. When flow is **turbulent**, you get irregular eddy currents that require much more pressure. Whether flow is laminar or turbulent depends on the gas's density, viscosity, velocity, and the radius of the tube.

## Topic 3: Volume and Weight

Avogadro's Law is straightforward: equal volumes of gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules. One mole contains 6.023 × 10²³ molecules — that's Avogadro's number. At STPD, one mole of any gas occupies 22.4 liters.

Density is mass divided by volume. Let's calculate: Oxygen has a gram molecular weight (GMW) of 32, so its density is 32 ÷ 22.4 = 1.43 g/L. Nitrogen has a GMW of 28, so its density is 28 ÷ 22.4 = 1.25 g/L.

What about atmospheric air? Air is 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, 0.9% argon, and 0.1% other gases. The density of air is: (1.43 × 0.21) + (1.25 × 0.78) = 0.30 + 0.99 = 1.29 g/L.

Here's where it gets clinically interesting: **Heliox therapy**. Heliox is a mixture of 30% oxygen and 70% helium. Helium is much lighter than nitrogen, so the resulting gas mixture has lower density. To calculate: O₂ density contribution is (32 ÷ 22.4) × 0.30, and He density contribution is (4 ÷ 22.4) × 0.70. The lower density means the gas flows more easily through constricted airways — that's why heliox is used in patients with severe airway obstruction.

## Topic 4: Ideal Gas Law

This is the big one: **PV = nRT**. R is the universal gas constant, 0.0821 L·atm/mol·K. This law applies when the number of moles (n) stays constant. From this equation, we get several specific gas laws you'll need to know.

**Boyle's Law**: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂. Pressure and volume are inversely related when temperature is constant. Think about it: if you squeeze a gas into a smaller volume, its pressure goes up. Clinically, this relates to **compliance** (ΔV/ΔP) and pulmonary function test measurements of lung volumes.

**Charles' Law**: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂. Volume and temperature are directly related when pressure is constant. This is why hot air balloons rise — heating the air increases its volume, making it less dense. Clinically, this law is used to convert gas volumes from ATPS (ambient conditions) to BTPS (body conditions).

**Gay-Lussac's Law**: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂. Pressure and temperature are directly related when volume is constant. If you heat a gas in a rigid container, its pressure increases.

You can combine all three into the **Combined Gas Law**: P₁V₁/T₁ = P₂V₂/T₂. This lets you calculate changes in any variable when the others change, as long as the amount of gas is constant.

## Topic 5: Gas Transportation

Blood is a liquid connective tissue made of plasma and cells. The red blood cells — erythrocytes — are incredible little machines. They're biconcave discs about 7 to 8 micrometers in diameter. They have no nucleus, which gives them more surface area for gas exchange. They also have no mitochondria, so they produce ATP anaerobically — this means they don't consume the oxygen they're carrying.

**Hemoglobin** is the protein that makes it all work. It has two parts: **globin**, which is made of four protein chains (two alpha and two beta), and **heme**, which has an iron ion (Fe²⁺) at its center. Each hemoglobin molecule can bind four oxygen molecules — one at each heme site.

How is oxygen transported? 98.5% is carried as **oxyhemoglobin** — oxygen bound to hemoglobin. Only 1.5% is dissolved directly in plasma. For carbon dioxide: 23% is carried as **carbaminohemoglobin** (bound to hemoglobin), 7% is dissolved in plasma, and 70% is carried as **bicarbonate** (HCO₃⁻). That bicarbonate conversion is a big deal — it's how most CO₂ gets from tissues to the lungs.

Hemoglobin also buffers hydrogen ions (H⁺), which helps maintain blood pH. There's a **cooperative interaction** in hemoglobin binding — once one oxygen molecule binds, it's easier for the next one to bind, and the same works in reverse for release.

Be aware of abnormal hemoglobin species: **Methemoglobin (metHB)** has iron in the Fe³⁺ state, which cannot bind oxygen. **Carboxyhemoglobin (HbCO)** forms when carbon monoxide binds to hemoglobin — CO has about 200 times greater affinity for hemoglobin than oxygen does. That's why CO poisoning is so dangerous.

## Topic 6: Gas Diffusion

Diffusion is the movement of molecules from an area of high concentration to low concentration — it's all driven by kinetic energy. **Graham's Law** says the rate of diffusion is inversely proportional to the square root of molecular weight. In plain language: lighter gases diffuse faster. Heating and agitation also speed up diffusion.

**Dalton's Law**: The total pressure of a gas mixture equals the sum of the partial pressures of each individual gas. Each gas exerts its own pressure independently, as if it were alone in the container. The partial pressure of a gas is calculated as: Pgas = Gas% × total barometric pressure (pB). This partial pressure is what determines whether a gas moves across a membrane — it moves from higher partial pressure to lower partial pressure.

The **oxygen cascade** describes the step-by-step movement of oxygen from the atmosphere, into the lungs, into the blood, and finally to the tissues. At each step, the partial pressure drops a little bit, creating the gradient that drives diffusion.

**Henry's Law**: The volume of gas dissolved in a liquid equals the solubility coefficient times the partial pressure. Different gases have different solubilities. At 37°C and 760 torr, oxygen's solubility coefficient in plasma is 0.023 mL per dL. Carbon dioxide's solubility coefficient is 0.510 mL per dL — that's more than 20 times higher than oxygen, which is why CO₂ dissolves so readily in blood. Higher temperature decreases solubility, lower temperature increases solubility.

## Topic 7: Gas Exchange

Let's put it all together with actual gas exchange. There are two types:

**External respiration** (pulmonary gas exchange) happens in the lungs — oxygen moves from the alveoli into the blood, and carbon dioxide moves from the blood into the alveoli.

**Internal respiration** (systemic gas exchange) happens in the tissues — oxygen moves from the blood into the tissues, and carbon dioxide moves from the tissues into the blood.

The numbers tell the story. For oxygen: alveolar PO₂ is about 100 mmHg, while deoxygenated blood entering the pulmonary capillaries has a PO₂ of about 40 mmHg — that big gradient drives oxygen into the blood. In the systemic capillaries, blood PO₂ is about 100 mmHg and tissue PO₂ is about 40 mmHg, so oxygen moves into the tissues.

For CO₂, the gradients work in reverse: pulmonary blood PCO₂ is about 45 mmHg, alveolar PCO₂ is about 40 mmHg, so CO₂ moves into the alveoli. Tissue PCO₂ is about 45 mmHg, arterial PCO₂ is about 40 mmHg, so CO₂ moves into the blood.

Know the difference between **hypoxia** (low oxygen at the cellular level) and **hypoxemia** (low oxygen in the blood). There are four types of hypoxia: **Hypoxic hypoxia** — low PaO₂, like in lung disease or at high altitude. **Anemic hypoxia** — low hemoglobin, so even if PaO₂ is normal, not enough oxygen is carried. **Ischemic hypoxia** — low blood flow, so oxygen doesn't reach tissues despite normal PaO₂ and hemoglobin. **Histotoxic hypoxia** — cellular toxins prevent cells from using oxygen, like in cyanide poisoning.

Finally, four factors determine the rate of gas exchange: (1) the partial pressure difference across the membrane, (2) the surface area available for exchange, (3) the distance the gas must diffuse, and (4) the molecular weight and solubility of the gas. Any change to these factors — like fluid in the alveoli increasing diffusion distance, or emphysema reducing surface area — will impair gas exchange.
