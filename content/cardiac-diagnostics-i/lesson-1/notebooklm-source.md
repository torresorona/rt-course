> 1. **ECG fundamentals** — the ECG records the electrical activity of large masses of atrial and ventricular cells as specific waveforms and complexes. A 12-LEAD ECG provides views of the heart in BOTH the FRONTAL AND HORIZONTAL planes and views the surfaces of the LEFT VENTRICLE from 12 different angles. Clinical uses: monitor heart rate; effects of disease or injury on heart function; pacemaker function; response to medications such as antiarrhythmics; baseline before, during, and after medical procedures. Activity is observed via ELECTRODES connected by cables to the ECG machine.
> 2. **Electrodes — count and types** — a 12-lead ECG uses 10 ELECTRODES total: 4 LIMB electrodes and 6 CHEST electrodes.
> 3. **Limb electrode placement (frontal leads)** — RIGHT ARM (RA), LEFT ARM (LA), LEFT LEG (LL), RIGHT LEG (RL). RIGHT LEG is used as the GROUND. Limb electrodes are usually placed on the WRISTS and ANKLES but may be positioned anywhere on the appropriate limb. To reduce muscle tension, make sure the patient's limbs are RESTING ON A SUPPORTIVE SURFACE. Do NOT apply electrodes over BONY AREAS, BROKEN SKIN, JOINTS, SCAR TISSUE, BURNS, or RASHES.
> 4. **Chest electrode placement (horizontal plane leads)** — six precise landmarks (memorize each): V1 = 4th INTERCOSTAL SPACE just to the RIGHT of the sternum; V2 = 4th intercostal space just to the LEFT of the sternum; V3 = HALFWAY between V2 and V4; V4 = 5th intercostal space in the MIDCLAVICULAR LINE; V5 = 5th intercostal space ANTERIOR AXILLARY LINE; V6 = 5th intercostal space MID-AXILLARY LINE.
> 5. **Performing an ECG — equipment prep** — ensure the machine has adequate ECG paper; gather all required equipment (gloves, gauze, electrodes, skin cleanser); connect the ECG cable to the machine; check for FRAYED or BROKEN cables or lead wires.
> 6. **Performing an ECG — patient prep** — bring patient into the room and introduce yourself; explain the procedure; have the patient remove garments from the WAIST UP; prep the skin to MINIMIZE DISTORTION of the ECG tracing — BRISKLY rub the skin with a DRY GAUZE pad and a SKIN CLEANSER (usually SOAPY WATER); SHAVE small amounts of chest hair if needed for good contact; remove the backing from each electrode and apply in the appropriate location; connect lead wires to the electrodes.
> 7. **Performing an ECG — acquisition** — coach the patient to relax; adjust the ECG SIZE if necessary; press PRINT, RHYTHM, or RECORD to obtain the tracing; ensure the ECG is FREE OF ARTIFACT before removing cables and electrodes.
> 8. **Artifact** — distortion of the ECG tracing by ELECTRICAL ACTIVITY THAT IS NON-CARDIAC IN ORIGIN. Critical clinical point: artifact CAN MIMIC ventricular fibrillation and other dysrhythmias — it is essential to EVALUATE THE PATIENT BEFORE INITIATING ANY MEDICAL INTERVENTION.
> 9. **ECG paper measurements** — exact numbers, memorize: HORIZONTAL AXIS = TIME — one small block (1 mm) = 0.04 SECONDS; one large block (5 mm) = 0.20 SECONDS. VERTICAL AXIS = AMPLITUDE / VOLTAGE — one small block (1 mm) = 0.1 mV; one large block (5 mm) = 0.5 mV.
> 10. **Waveform deflection from baseline** — three rules: if the wave of depolarization moves TOWARD the positive electrode, the waveform recorded is UPRIGHT; if it moves AWAY from the positive electrode, the waveform is INVERTED (upside down); if it moves PERPENDICULAR to the positive electrode, the waveform is BIPHASIC (partly positive, partly negative).
> 11. **P wave** — represents ATRIAL DEPOLARIZATION. The first half is recorded when the electrical impulse originates in the SA NODE and stimulates the right atrium, then reaches the AV node. SMOOTH and ROUNDED. NO GREATER THAN 2.5 mm / 0.25 mV TALL. NO GREATER THAN 0.11 SECONDS in duration. POSITIVE in leads I, II, aVF, and V2–V6.
> 12. **QRS complex** — represents VENTRICULAR DEPOLARIZATION. Because of its greater muscle mass, the QRS complex generally represents the electrical activity of the LEFT VENTRICLE. The Q WAVE represents depolarization of the INTERVENTRICULAR SEPTUM, which activates LEFT TO RIGHT, and begins with a DOWNWARD deflection. The R WAVE is a large, upright, triangular waveform — ALWAYS POSITIVE. The S WAVE is the negative waveform FOLLOWING the R wave. The R and S waves represent SIMULTANEOUS depolarization of the right and left ventricles. NORMAL QRS DURATION = 0.11 SECONDS OR LESS.
> 13. **T wave** — represents VENTRICULAR REPOLARIZATION. At the PEAK of the T wave, the RELATIVE REFRACTORY PERIOD (RRP) has begun — a STRONGER-THAN-NORMAL stimulus during the RRP may produce a VENTRICULAR DYSRHYTHMIA.
> 14. **Abnormal T wave** — three pairings: INVERTED or NEGATIVE T waves suggest MYOCARDIAL ISCHEMIA. TALL, POINTED (PEAKED) T waves are commonly seen in HYPERKALEMIA. LOW AMPLITUDE T waves may be seen in HYPOKALEMIA or HYPOMAGNESEMIA.
> 15. **U wave** — represents repolarization of the PURKINJE FIBERS. Small upright waveform that, when seen, FOLLOWS the T wave. Usually LESS THAN 0.1 mV. NEGATIVE U WAVES in leads V2–V5 may be seen in patients with ISCHEMIC HEART DISEASE or HYPERTENSION.
> 16. **PR segment** — the line between the END of the P wave and the ONSET of the QRS complex. Represents activation of the AV NODE, BUNDLE OF HIS, BUNDLE BRANCHES, and PURKINJE FIBERS.
> 17. **PR interval (PRI)** — begins with the ONSET of the P wave and ends with the ONSET of the QRS complex. NORMAL = 0.12 – 0.20 SECONDS. LONG PR > 0.20 seconds = impulse was DELAYED OR BLOCKED before reaching the AV node. SHORT PR < 0.12 seconds = impulse originates in an ECTOPIC PACEMAKER CLOSE TO the AV node.
> 18. **ST segment** — represents the EARLY PART of ventricular repolarization; the segment between the QRS complex and the T wave. Three patterns: ST DEPRESSION suggests MYOCARDIAL ISCHEMIA. ST elevation in the shape of a "FROWNY" face is often associated with an ACUTE INJURY pattern (MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION, PERICARDITIS). ST elevation in the shape of a "SMILEY" face is usually BENIGN. ST segment elevation varies and is dependent on the patient's age, sex, and ECG lead.
> 19. **J point** — the point where the QRS complex and the ST segment MEET.
> 20. **QT interval** — represents TOTAL VENTRICULAR ACTIVITY — time from ventricular DEPOLARIZATION (activation) to REPOLARIZATION (recovery). Varies according to AGE, SEX, and HEART RATE. Generally, the QT interval should be LESS THAN 50% OF THE R-R INTERVAL. Conditions associated with PROLONGED QT: certain drugs; HYPOCALCEMIA; marked BRADYARRHYTHMIAS; INTRACRANIAL HEMORRHAGE; MYOCARDITIS.
> 21. **Leads — definition** — a tracing of electrical activity between two electrodes; each lead records the AVERAGE CURRENT FLOW at a specific time in a portion of the heart.
> 22. **Standard & augmented leads (frontal plane)** — formed by leads I, II, III, aVL, aVF, and aVR. Allow viewing of the FRONTAL plane. Use EINTHOVEN'S TRIANGLE — RA, LA, LL, with RL as the GROUND.
> 23. **Standard limb leads (bipolar)** — three pairings: LEAD I = LA(+) / RA(−) — UPRIGHT complex; views the LATERAL surface of the left ventricle. LEAD II = LL(+) / RA(−) — UPRIGHT complex; views the INFERIOR surface of the left ventricle. LEAD II is the lead MOST COMMONLY USED for cardiac monitoring because its electrode positioning most closely resembles the normal pathway of current flow in the heart. LEAD III = LL(+) / LA(−) — UPRIGHT complex; views the INFERIOR surface of the left ventricle.
> 24. **Augmented limb leads (unipolar)** — single positive electrode. aVR — views the heart from the RIGHT SHOULDER and views the BASE of the heart (primarily the ATRIA and the GREAT VESSELS); generates an INVERTED complex. aVL — views the heart from the LEFT SHOULDER, oriented to the LATERAL wall of the left ventricle; UPRIGHT or BIPHASIC complex. aVF — views the heart from the LEFT FOOT and views the INFERIOR surface of the left ventricle; UPRIGHT complex.
> 25. **Horizontal plane leads (V1–V6)** — UNIPOLAR chest leads. View the heart as if the body were sliced in half horizontally. Directions in the horizontal plane are ANTERIOR, POSTERIOR, RIGHT, and LEFT. V1 = SEPTAL wall; V2 = SEPTAL wall; V3 = ANTERIOR wall; V4 = ANTERIOR wall; V5 = LATERAL wall; V6 = LATERAL wall.
> 26. **SALI mnemonic** — leads-to-walls mapping (memorize cold): SEPTAL = V1 and V2. ANTERIOR = V3 and V4. LATERAL = V5, V6, I, and aVL. INFERIOR = II, III, and aVF.
>
> **High-yield distinctions to reinforce:**
> - 10 electrodes total: 4 LIMB + 6 CHEST. RIGHT LEG = GROUND (not a lead).
> - Chest landmarks — V1/V2 sit at the 4TH ICS at the sternal borders; V4/V5/V6 ALL sit at the 5TH ICS at progressively lateral lines (midclavicular → anterior axillary → mid-axillary). V3 is the only one defined by a relationship ("halfway between V2 and V4").
> - ECG paper math: small block = 0.04 s / 0.1 mV; large block = 0.20 s / 0.5 mV. Time on the horizontal axis, voltage on the vertical.
> - Deflection rules: TOWARD = upright; AWAY = inverted; PERPENDICULAR = biphasic.
> - P wave limits: ≤ 2.5 mm tall, ≤ 0.11 s wide, positive in I, II, aVF, V2–V6.
> - QRS limit: ≤ 0.11 s. PR interval: 0.12–0.20 s.
> - T wave abnormalities — INVERTED → ischemia; PEAKED → hyperkalemia; LOW AMP → hypokalemia or hypomagnesemia.
> - U wave — small, upright, < 0.1 mV, follows the T wave; NEGATIVE U in V2–V5 → ischemic heart disease or hypertension.
> - ST segment patterns — DEPRESSION = ischemia; FROWNY ELEVATION = acute injury (MI, pericarditis); SMILEY ELEVATION = usually benign.
> - QT interval — should be LESS THAN 50% of the R-R interval. Prolonged by drugs, hypocalcemia, marked bradyarrhythmias, intracranial hemorrhage, and myocarditis.
> - LEAD II is the standard monitoring lead because it follows the normal pathway of current flow.
> - aVR is the ONLY frontal lead that produces an INVERTED complex (it views from the right shoulder).
> - SALI: SEPTAL (V1, V2) · ANTERIOR (V3, V4) · LATERAL (V5, V6, I, aVL) · INFERIOR (II, III, aVF).
> - Critical safety point: artifact can MIMIC ventricular fibrillation. Always EVALUATE THE PATIENT before treating a strip.

## Topic 1: ECG Fundamentals

The electrocardiogram (ECG) is a device used to **record and monitor the electrical activity of a large mass of the atrial and ventricular cells as specific waveforms and complexes**.

A **12-lead ECG** provides:

- Views of the heart in both the **frontal and horizontal planes**.
- Views the surfaces of the **left ventricle** from **12 different angles**.

ECGs may be used to monitor a patient's:

- Heart rate
- Effects of disease or injury on heart function
- Pacemaker function
- Response to medications (e.g., antiarrhythmics)
- Baseline recording before, during, and after medical procedures

The heart's electrical activity is observed by means of **electrodes** connected by cables to the ECG machine.

---

## Topic 2: Electrode Count

A 12-lead ECG utilizes **10 electrodes** on the surface of the skin:

- **4 limb electrodes**
- **6 chest electrodes**

---

## Topic 3: Limb Electrode Placement (Frontal Leads)

Utilizes **Right Arm (RA), Left Arm (LA), Left Leg (LL), and Right Leg (RL)**. **Right leg is used as a ground.**

- Limb electrodes are usually placed on the **wrists and ankles** but may be positioned anywhere on the appropriate limb.
- To reduce muscle tension, make sure the patient's limbs are **resting on a supportive surface**.
- **Do not apply electrodes over** bony areas, broken skin, joints, scar tissues, burns, or rashes.

---

## Topic 4: Chest Electrode Placement (Horizontal Plane Leads)

- **V1** — 4th intercostal space, just to the **right** of the sternum.
- **V2** — 4th intercostal space, just to the **left** of the sternum.
- **V3** — **halfway between V2 and V4**.
- **V4** — 5th intercostal space, **midclavicular line**.
- **V5** — 5th intercostal space, **anterior axillary line**.
- **V6** — 5th intercostal space, **mid-axillary line**.

---

## Topic 5: Performing an ECG — Equipment Prep

- Ensure the machine has adequate **ECG paper**.
- Gather all required equipment (gloves, gauze, electrodes, skin cleanser).
- Connect the ECG cable to the machine.
- Make sure there are no **frayed/broken cables or lead wires**.

---

## Topic 6: Performing an ECG — Patient Prep

- Bring the patient into the room and introduce yourself; explain the procedure.
- Have the patient **remove garments from the waist up**.
- Prep the patient's skin to minimize distortion of the ECG tracing:
  - **Briskly rub** the patient's skin with a **dry gauze pad and skin cleanser** (usually soapy water).
  - **Shave small amounts of chest hair** if needed before applying electrodes for good contact.
- Remove the backing from each electrode and apply to the patient in the appropriate placement.
- Connect the lead wires to the electrodes.

---

## Topic 7: Performing an ECG — Acquisition

- Coach the patient to **relax**.
- Adjust the **ECG size** if necessary.
- Select the **print, rhythm, or record** button to obtain a copy of the patient's ECG.
- Ensure the ECG is **free of artifact** before removing cables and electrodes.

---

## Topic 8: Artifact

**Artifact** is distortion of an ECG tracing by **electrical activity that is non-cardiac in origin**.

> Artifact can mimic various cardiac dysrhythmias, **including ventricular fibrillation**. It is essential to **evaluate the patient before initiating any medical intervention**.

---

## Topic 9: ECG Paper Measurements

- **Horizontal axis** represents **time**:
  - One small block = 1 mm = **0.04 seconds**
  - One large block = 5 mm = **0.20 seconds**
- **Vertical axis** represents **amplitude (voltage)**:
  - One small block = 1 mm = **0.1 mV**
  - One large block = 5 mm = **0.5 mV**

---

## Topic 10: Waveform Deflection from Baseline

- If the wave of depolarization moves **toward** the positive electrode → the waveform is **upright**.
- If the wave moves **away from** the positive electrode → the waveform is **inverted** (upside down).
- A **biphasic** waveform (partly positive, partly negative) is recorded when the wave moves **perpendicularly** to the positive electrode.

---

## Topic 11: P Wave

Represents **atrial depolarization**.

- First half is recorded when the electrical impulse originates in the **SA node** and stimulates the right atrium and then reaches the AV node.
- **Smooth and rounded.**
- No greater than **2.5 mm / 0.25 mV** tall.
- No greater than **0.11 seconds**.
- **Positive in leads I, II, aVF, and V2–V6.**

---

## Topic 12: QRS Complex

Represents **ventricular depolarization**. Because of its greater muscle mass, the QRS complex generally represents the electrical activity occurring in the **left ventricle**.

- **Q wave** — depolarization of the **interventricular septum** (activates left to right). Begins with a downward deflection.
- **R wave** — large, upright, triangular waveform; **always positive**.
- **S wave** — the negative waveform following the R wave.
- The R and S waves represent **simultaneous depolarization of the right and left ventricles**.

**Normal QRS duration = 0.11 seconds or less.**

---

## Topic 13: T Wave

Represents **ventricular repolarization**.

> At the **peak** of the T wave, the **Relative Refractory Period (RRP)** has begun. A stronger-than-normal stimulus during the RRP may produce a **ventricular dysrhythmia**.

---

## Topic 14: Abnormal T Wave

- **Inverted or negative T waves** suggest **myocardial ischemia**.
- **Tall, pointed (peaked) T waves** are commonly seen in **hyperkalemia**.
- **Low amplitude T waves** may be seen in **hypokalemia** or **hypomagnesemia**.

---

## Topic 15: U Wave

Represents **repolarization of the Purkinje fibers**.

- Small **upright** waveform that, when seen, **follows the T wave**.
- Usually **less than 0.1 mV**.
- **Negative U waves in leads V2–V5** may be seen in patients with **ischemic heart disease** or **hypertension**.

---

## Topic 16: PR Segment

The line between the **end of the P wave** and the **onset of the QRS complex**.

Represents activation of the **AV node, bundle of His, bundle branches, and Purkinje fibers**.

---

## Topic 17: PR Interval (PRI)

Begins with the **onset of the P wave** and ends with the **onset of the QRS complex**.

- **Normal:** 0.12 – 0.20 seconds.
- **Long PR interval > 0.20 seconds** — indicates the impulse was **delayed or blocked before reaching the AV node**.
- **Short PR interval < 0.12 seconds** — suggests the impulse originates in an **ectopic pacemaker close to the AV node**.

---

## Topic 18: ST Segment

Represents the **early part of ventricular repolarization**. Segment between the QRS complex and the T wave.

- **ST depression** → suggestive of **myocardial ischemia**.
- **ST elevation — "frowny" face** → often associated with an **acute injury pattern** (e.g., myocardial infarction, pericarditis).
- **ST elevation — "smiley" face** → usually **benign**.
- ST segment elevation **varies** and depends on patient's **age, sex, and ECG lead**.

---

## Topic 19: J Point

The point where the **QRS complex and the ST segment meet**.

---

## Topic 20: QT Interval

Represents **total ventricular activity** — time from ventricular **depolarization (activation)** to **repolarization (recovery)**.

- Varies according to **age, sex, and heart rate**.
- Generally, the QT interval should be **less than 50% of the R-R interval**.

**Conditions associated with a prolonged QT interval:**

- Certain types of drugs
- Hypocalcemia
- Marked bradyarrhythmias
- Intracranial hemorrhage
- Myocarditis

---

## Topic 21: Leads — Definition

A **lead** is a tracing of **electrical activity between two electrodes**.

Each lead records the **average current flow** at a specific time in a portion of the heart.

---

## Topic 22: Standard & Augmented Leads (Frontal Plane)

Formed by leads **I, II, III, aVL, aVF, and aVR**. Allow viewing of the **frontal planes** of the heart.

Use **Einthoven's Triangle** — RA, LA, LL, with **RL as the ground**.

- **Standard limb leads (bipolar):** I (RA, LA) · II (RA, LL) · III (LA, LL).
- **Augmented limb leads (unipolar):** aVL (LA) · aVF (LL) · aVR (RA).

---

## Topic 23: Standard Limb Leads (Bipolar)

- **Lead I** — Upright complex. Views the **lateral** surface of the left ventricle. Records the difference in electrical potential between **LA (+) and RA (−)**.
- **Lead II** — Upright complex. Views the **inferior** surface of the left ventricle. Records the difference between **LL (+) and RA (−)**. **Most commonly used for cardiac monitoring** because positioning of the positive and negative electrodes most closely resembles the normal pathway of current flow in the heart.
- **Lead III** — Upright complex. Views the **inferior** surface of the left ventricle. Records the difference between **LL (+) and LA (−)**.

---

## Topic 24: Augmented Limb Leads (Unipolar)

Each consists of a single positive electrode.

- **aVR** — **inverted** complex generated. Views the heart from the **right shoulder** and views the **base of the heart** (primarily the **atria and the great vessels**).
- **aVL** — **upright or biphasic** complex generated. Views the heart from the **left shoulder**, oriented to the **lateral wall** of the left ventricle.
- **aVF** — **upright** complex generated. Views the heart from the **left foot** and views the **inferior surface** of the left ventricle.

---

## Topic 25: Horizontal Plane Leads (V1–V6)

Unipolar chest leads. View the heart as if the body were sliced in half horizontally. Directions in the horizontal plane are **anterior, posterior, right, and left**.

- **V1** → **septal** wall.
- **V2** → **septal** wall.
- **V3** → **anterior** wall.
- **V4** → **anterior** wall.
- **V5** → **lateral** wall.
- **V6** → **lateral** wall.

---

## Topic 26: SALI Mnemonic

- **(S)eptal wall:** V1 and V2.
- **(A)nterior wall:** V3 and V4.
- **(L)ateral wall:** V5, V6, I, and aVL.
- **(I)nferior wall:** II, III, and aVF.

---

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **12-lead ECG = 10 electrodes:** 4 limb + 6 chest. **RL = GROUND.**
- **Chest electrode landmarks:** V1 = 4th ICS R sternum · V2 = 4th ICS L sternum · V3 = halfway between V2 and V4 · V4 = 5th ICS midclavicular · V5 = 5th ICS anterior axillary · V6 = 5th ICS mid-axillary.
- **ECG paper:** small block (1 mm) → time 0.04 s / voltage 0.1 mV; large block (5 mm) → time 0.20 s / voltage 0.5 mV.
- **Deflection rules:** toward (+) electrode = UPRIGHT · away = INVERTED · perpendicular = BIPHASIC.
- **P wave** = atrial depolarization · ≤ 2.5 mm / 0.25 mV tall · ≤ 0.11 s wide · positive in I, II, aVF, V2–V6.
- **QRS** = ventricular depolarization (mostly LV) · Q (septum, L→R, downward) · R (always +) · S (negative after R) · ≤ 0.11 s.
- **T wave** = ventricular repolarization. Peak = start of RRP — strong stimulus here can trigger a ventricular dysrhythmia.
- **Abnormal T:** inverted → ischemia · peaked → hyperkalemia · low amp → hypokalemia / hypomagnesemia.
- **U wave** = Purkinje repolarization · upright, follows T, < 0.1 mV. Negative U in V2–V5 → ischemic heart disease / HTN.
- **PR interval** 0.12–0.20 s · long (> 0.20) = AV delay/block · short (< 0.12) = ectopic pacemaker near AV node.
- **ST segment:** depression → ischemia · "frowny" elevation → acute injury (MI, pericarditis) · "smiley" elevation → usually benign.
- **J point** = junction of QRS and ST.
- **QT interval** = total ventricular activity · < 50 % of R-R · prolonged by drugs, hypocalcemia, marked bradyarrhythmias, intracranial hemorrhage, myocarditis.
- **Lead** = tracing between two electrodes; records average current flow.
- **Frontal plane (limb leads) — Einthoven's triangle:** I (LA+/RA−, lateral) · II (LL+/RA−, inferior, **standard monitoring lead**) · III (LL+/LA−, inferior).
- **Augmented:** aVR (RA, right shoulder, **inverted**) · aVL (LA, left shoulder, lateral) · aVF (LL, left foot, inferior).
- **Horizontal plane (V1–V6):** V1/V2 = septal · V3/V4 = anterior · V5/V6 = lateral.
- **SALI:** Septal V1, V2 · Anterior V3, V4 · Lateral V5, V6, I, aVL · Inferior II, III, aVF.
- **Artifact:** non-cardiac electrical activity that distorts the tracing — can mimic V-fib. Always **evaluate the patient first**.
