> 1. **Exercise stress test — what it is** — referred to as a GRADED EXERCISE TEST (GXT). One of the MOST FREQUENT NON-INVASIVE MODALITIES used to assess patients with suspected or proven cardiovascular disease. Exercise is a common physiologic stress used to obtain CARDIOVASCULAR ABNORMALITIES NOT PRESENT AT REST and to determine the ADEQUACY OF CARDIAC FUNCTION. The test is mainly used to estimate PROGNOSIS and determine FUNCTIONAL CAPACITY, the LIKELIHOOD AND EXTENT OF CORONARY ARTERY DISEASE (CAD), and the EFFECTS OF THERAPY.
> 2. **Indications — populations and syndromes** — most frequent indication is establishing the diagnosis of CORONARY ARTERY DISEASE. Provides DIAGNOSTIC AND PROGNOSTIC information. Estimating prognosis must be considered along with the patient's RISK STATUS. Populations: ASYMPTOMATIC POPULATION, SYMPTOMATIC PATIENTS, SILENT MYOCARDIAL ISCHEMIA, ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES, MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION, CONGESTIVE HEART FAILURE.
> 3. **Indications — arrhythmias and devices** — VENTRICULAR ARRHYTHMIAS, SUPRAVENTRICULAR ARRHYTHMIAS, ATRIAL FIBRILLATION, SINUS NODE DYSFUNCTION, ATRIOVENTRICULAR BLOCK, LEFT AND RIGHT BUNDLE BRANCH BLOCK, PRE-EXCITATION SYNDROME, CARDIAC PACEMAKERS AND IMPLANTABLE CARDIOVERTER-DEFIBRILLATOR DEVICES.
> 4. **Absolute contraindications** — RECENT SIGNIFICANT CHANGE IN THE REST ECG suggestive of significant ischemia or another acute cardiac event; ACUTE SYSTEMIC INFECTION accompanied by fever, body aches; ACUTE MI WITHIN 2 DAYS; HIGH-RISK UNSTABLE ANGINA; UNCONTROLLED CARDIAC ARRHYTHMIAS; SYMPTOMATIC SEVERE AORTIC STENOSIS; UNCONTROLLED SYMPTOMATIC HEART FAILURE; ACUTE PULMONARY EMBOLUS or PULMONARY INFARCTION; ACUTE MYOCARDITIS or PERICARDITIS.
> 5. **Etiology / physiology of exercise** — at fixed submaximal workloads BELOW ANAEROBIC THRESHOLD, STEADY-STATE CONDITIONS are usually reached AFTER THE SECOND MINUTE OF EXERCISE; after that, heart rate, cardiac output, blood pressure, and pulmonary ventilation are maintained at reasonably constant levels. As exercise progresses: SKELETAL MUSCLE BLOOD FLOW INCREASES; OXYGEN EXTRACTION INCREASES; TOTAL CALCULATED PERIPHERAL RESISTANCE DECREASES; SYSTOLIC BP, MEAN ARTERIAL PRESSURE INCREASE; PULSE PRESSURE INCREASES; DIASTOLIC BP DOES NOT CHANGE SIGNIFICANTLY; RESPIRATORY RATE INCREASES.
> 6. **Exercise protocols — overview** — approximately 8 TO 12 MINUTES of continuous progressive exercise during which myocardial oxygen demand is elevated to the patient's MAXIMAL LEVEL is OPTIMAL FOR DIAGNOSTIC AND PROGNOSTIC purposes. The protocol should include a suitable RECOVERY OR COOL-DOWN period. If the protocol is TOO STRENUOUS, the test must be TERMINATED EARLY and the test will be INCONCLUSIVE.
> 7. **Bruce protocol** — 3-MINUTE PERIODS to allow achievement of a steady state before workload is increased. (For HEALTHY individuals.) Utilized most commonly by HEALTHY individuals.
> 8. **Modified Bruce protocol** — modified by TWO 3-MINUTE WARM-UP STAGES at 1.7 MPH AND 0% GRADE and 1.7 MPH AND 5% GRADE. Utilized by OLDER INDIVIDUALS or those whose EXERCISE CAPACITY IS LIMITED BY CARDIAC DISEASE.
> 9. **Naughton and Weber protocols** — use 1 to 2 MINUTE STAGES with 1 MET INCREMENTS between stages. More suitable for patients with LIMITED EXERCISE TOLERANCE, such as patients with COMPENSATED CONGESTIVE HEART FAILURE.
> 10. **Treadmill technique** — protocol should be CONSISTENT with the patient's PHYSICAL CAPACITY and the PURPOSE of the test. It is important to encourage patients NOT TO GRASP THE HANDRAILS during exercise, particularly the FRONT handrails. FUNCTIONAL CAPACITY CAN BE OVERESTIMATED BY AS MUCH AS 20% in tests in which handrail support is permitted.
> 11. **GXT procedure — patient prep** — patients should be instructed NOT to eat, drink, or smoke. A BRIEF HISTORY AND PHYSICAL EXAMINATION should be performed; advise about RISKS AND BENEFITS. The test should NOT be performed on subjects who are MARKEDLY HYPERTENSIVE OR HYPOTENSIVE. A WRITTEN CONSENT FORM is required, signed by PATIENT, PHYSICIAN, AND TECHNICIAN. A standard 12-LEAD ECG is recorded with the patient in MULTIPLE POSITIONS. Patients should be instructed on HOW TO PERFORM THE TEST. A MINIMUM OF 3 LEADS should be displayed continuously throughout the test. HR, BP, and ECG will be recorded at the BEGINNING AND END OF EACH STAGE of exercise.
> 12. **During GXT — ECG observations** — QRS = period of ventricle systolic depolarization (cardiac muscle CONTRACTED). ST segment = period of early repolarization (cardiac muscle RELAXED). NON-SHORTENING of PR, QRS, & QT intervals with exercise. P-WAVE AMPLITUDE INCREASE. J-POINT DEPRESSION. ST SEGMENT DEPRESSION. ST SEGMENT DEPRESSION WITH INVERTED T WAVES. In some cases ST-segment displacement may ONLY BE SEEN DURING EXERCISE — patient should NOT LEAVE until ECG has RETURNED TO BASELINE.
> 13. **ST segment depression — thresholds** — ST at rest may occasionally be depressed. If depression is 0.1 mV / 1 mm (ISCHEMIA) OR GREATER, the exercise ECG becomes LESS SPECIFIC, and MYOCARDIAL IMAGING MODALITIES should be considered. Exercise-induced ST-segment depression DOES NOT LOCALIZE the site of myocardial ischemia nor does it INDICATE WHICH CORONARY ARTERY is involved. Also: 0.5 mm in leads V2 AND V3 also signify ischemia.
> 14. **Upsloping ST segments** — J-POINT DEPRESSION is a NORMAL FINDING during maximal exercise. RAPID UPSLOPING ST (more than 1 mV/sec) depressed LESS THAN 0.15 mV (1.5 mm) after the J point should be considered NORMAL. A SLOW UPSLOPING ST depressed 0.15 mV (1.5 mm) OR MORE at 80 MILLISECONDS after the J point should be considered ABNORMAL.
> 15. **ST-segment elevation and T wave changes** — development of 0.10 mV (1 mm) or more of J POINT ELEVATION is considered an ABNORMAL RESPONSE; occurs MORE FREQUENTLY in patients with ANTERIOR MI. T WAVE CHANGES — morphology can be influenced by several factors.
> 16. **Non-ECG observations — Maximal Work Capacity** — one of the MOST IMPORTANT PROGNOSTIC MEASUREMENTS obtained. In healthy individuals, influenced by FAMILIARIZATION with equipment, LEVEL OF TRAINING, and ENVIRONMENTAL CONDITIONS. In patients with known or suspected CAD, a LIMITED EXERCISE CAPACITY is associated with INCREASED RISK of fatal and nonfatal CV events such as ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES AND STROKE, regardless of age, sex, race, or abdominal adiposity.
> 17. **Submaximal exercise** — interpretation requires consideration of MAXIMAL WORK CAPACITY. When a patient is unable to complete moderate levels of exercise or reach AT LEAST 85% TO 90% OF AGE-PREDICTED MAXIMUM, the level performed may be INADEQUATE TO TEST CARDIAC RESERVE. NON-DIAGNOSTIC TEST results common in PERIPHERAL VASCULAR DISEASE, ORTHOPEDIC LIMITATION, NEUROLOGIC IMPAIRMENT, and POOR MOTIVATION. PHARMACOLOGIC STRESS IMAGING STUDIES should be considered — VASODILATOR NUCLEAR STRESS TESTING or INOTROPE STRESS ECHO.
> 18. **Chest discomfort** — can be helpful when diagnosing the patient's symptoms. The exercise level may exceed daily activities, causing ANGINA. Exercise-induced chest discomfort usually occurs AFTER THE ONSET of ischemic ST-segment abnormalities and may be associated with DIASTOLIC HYPERTENSION. In some patients, chest discomfort may be the ONLY SIGN that obstructive CAD is present. In CHRONIC STABLE ANGINA, exercise-induced chest discomfort/pain occurs LESS FREQUENTLY than ischemic ST-segment depression.
> 19. **Blood pressure responses** — NORMAL: increase systolic BP progressively with increasing workloads to a peak ranging from 160 – 200 mmHg. ABNORMAL: failure to increase systolic BP BEYOND 120 mmHg; sustained DECREASE GREATER THAN 10 mmHg repeatable within 15 SECONDS; FALL IN SYSTOLIC BP BELOW STANDING RESTING VALUES during progressive exercise.
> 20. **Heart rate responses** — NORMAL: sinus rate increases progressively with exercise; sometimes ANXIOUSNESS can cause an increase. Other inappropriate increases can result from A-FIB, PHYSICALLY DECONDITIONED, HYPOVOLEMIA, ANEMIC, LV FAILURE (marginal LV function). ABNORMAL: HR FAILS TO INCREASE APPROPRIATELY with exercise.
> 21. **Heart rate recovery (HRR)** — there is some controversy about optimal patient position in the recovery phase. Abnormal HRR refers to a relatively SLOW DECELERATION of heart rate following exercise cessation. HRR = HR PEAK − HR ONE MINUTE LATER. UPRIGHT cool-down position: 12 BPM OR LESS is abnormal. ANY OTHER POSITIONS: 18 BPM OR LESS is abnormal. HRR 2 MINUTES INTO RECOVERY: 22 BPM OR LESS is abnormal.
> 22. **Reasons to terminate the GXT** — DROP IN SYSTOLIC BP > 10 mmHg from baseline; MODERATE TO SEVERE ANGINA; ATAXIA, DIZZINESS, NEAR-SYNCOPE; CYANOSIS; TECHNICAL DIFFICULTIES in monitoring ECG or BP; SUBJECT'S DESIRE TO STOP; SUSTAINED VENTRICULAR TACHYCARDIA; ST ELEVATION ≥ 1.0 mm in leads WITHOUT diagnostic Q waves (other than V1 or aVR).
> 23. **Emergency equipment** — a CRASH CART AND DEFIBRILLATOR should be ALWAYS readily available. Appropriate medication for cardiac arrhythmias, AV BLOCK, HYPOTENSION, PERSISTENT CHEST PAIN. An IV LINE should be started in HIGH-RISK PATIENTS. Equipment and supplies in the cart should be checked DAILY.
> 24. **Holter monitor — definition** — also known as AMBULATORY ELECTROCARDIOGRAPHIC RECORDING. A SMALL, WEARABLE device that records heart rhythm while allowing patients to engage in NORMAL DAILY ACTIVITIES. CONTINUOUS electrocardiographic recorder. Records for a 24–72 HOUR TIME PERIOD specified in physician's orders.
> 25. **Holter indications** — SYNCOPE, DIZZINESS, PALPITATIONS, PHYSICAL EXAMINATION, ARRHYTHMIAS, PREOPERATIVE ARRHYTHMIA EVALUATION, VALVULAR HEART DISEASE, POST-MI PATIENTS.
> 26. **Holter — patient prep** — properly prep patient for best results. CLEAN THE SITE where the electrodes will be placed. SHAVE any necessary chest hair. Use GAUZE OR AN ABRASIVE PAD with a CLEANSER to remove OILS, LOTIONS, AND EXCESS SKIN CELLS. HOOK UP ELECTRODES according to the diagram. CONNECT CABLE INTO MONITOR. TURN ON the Holter monitor by INSERTING BATTERIES to get a BASELINE READING. ASSURE the monitor has an electrocardiogram BASELINE, IS ON, and DISPLAYS TODAY'S DATE before patient leaves.
> 27. **Event monitor — overview** — in many patients, 24–72 hour Holter is INCAPABLE of documenting the cause of the patient's symptoms; LONGER-TERM monitoring is necessary. Devices are about the SIZE OF A PAGER and are kept for UP TO 30 DAYS. Digital recordings can be made during symptomatic episodes and TRANSMITTED to a receiving station OVER STANDARD TELEPHONE LINES at the patient's convenience. HIGHLY EFFECTIVE in documenting INFREQUENT EVENTS, but the QUALITY of recordings is MORE VARIABLE than with Holter recorders.
> 28. **Loop event recorder mechanics** — record CONTINUOUSLY, but only a SMALL WINDOW OF TIME is present in memory at any moment; when the EVENT BUTTON is pressed, the current window is FROZEN while the device CONTINUES recording for ANOTHER 30 TO 60 SECONDS depending on configuration. Store MORE THAN 30 SECONDS of the ECG BEFORE the patient activates the recording. With some systems, the patient must press the event button to begin recording — IF SYNCOPE OCCURS WITHOUT WARNING and the patient is unable to actuate the device, IT CANNOT PROVIDE DIAGNOSTIC INFORMATION. With other systems, the device AUTOMATICALLY begins recording when heart rate falls outside PREDETERMINED PARAMETERS.
> 29. **Cell-phone-enabled event monitors** — some systems incorporate CELL PHONE TECHNOLOGY that AUTOMATICALLY NOTIFIES a CENTRAL MONITORING FACILITY when certain conditions are met (extreme bradycardia or tachycardia). Information is transmitted via INTERNET/REPORT to the physician for PROMPT DIAGNOSIS AND TREATMENT. Patient prep — same steps as Holter.
> 30. **Patient education — Do's and Don'ts** — patient SHOULD go about their NORMAL DAILY ACTIVITIES. Patient should NOT shower or bathe — DO NOT GET MONITOR WET in any way (SWEATING is an exception). Instruct patient NOT TO TAMPER with the recorder. DO NOT pull on electrode leads or disturb the electrodes — may cause ARTIFACT.
>

## Topic 1: Exercise Stress Test — Definition

The exercise stress test, also called a **Graded Exercise Test (GXT)**, is one of the most frequent non-invasive modalities used to assess patients with suspected or proven cardiovascular disease. Exercise is used as a physiologic stress to obtain:

- Cardiovascular abnormalities not present at rest
- Adequacy of cardiac function

Used to estimate **prognosis**, determine **functional capacity**, the **likelihood and extent of CAD**, and the **effects of therapy**.

---

## Topic 2: Indications

The most frequent indication is establishing the diagnosis of **Coronary Artery Disease (CAD)**. Provides diagnostic and prognostic information.

Populations:

- Asymptomatic
- Symptomatic
- Silent myocardial ischemia
- Acute coronary syndromes
- Myocardial infarction
- Congestive heart failure

Evaluation of arrhythmias and devices:

- Ventricular arrhythmias
- Supraventricular arrhythmias
- Atrial fibrillation
- Sinus node dysfunction
- AV block
- LBBB / RBBB
- Pre-excitation syndrome
- Pacemakers and ICDs

---

## Topic 3: Absolute Contraindications

- Recent significant change in the rest ECG suggestive of significant ischemia or acute cardiac event
- Acute systemic infection with fever, body aches
- Acute MI within 2 days
- High-risk unstable angina
- Uncontrolled cardiac arrhythmias
- Symptomatic severe aortic stenosis
- Uncontrolled symptomatic heart failure
- Acute pulmonary embolus or pulmonary infarction
- Acute myocarditis or pericarditis

---

## Topic 4: Etiology / Physiology

At fixed submaximal workloads below the anaerobic threshold, **steady-state conditions are usually reached after the second minute of exercise**. After that, HR, cardiac output, BP, and pulmonary ventilation are reasonably constant.

As exercise progresses:

- Skeletal muscle blood flow increases
- Oxygen extraction increases
- Total calculated peripheral resistance decreases
- Systolic BP and mean arterial pressure increase
- Pulse pressure increases
- Diastolic BP does not change significantly
- Respiratory rate increases

---

## Topic 5: Protocol Overview

Approximately **8 to 12 minutes** of continuous progressive exercise is **optimal** for diagnostic/prognostic purposes. The protocol must include a recovery / cool-down period. If too strenuous, the test must be terminated early and is **inconclusive**.

---

## Topic 6: Bruce Protocol

3-minute periods to allow steady state before workload is increased. **Utilized most commonly by healthy individuals.**

---

## Topic 7: Modified Bruce Protocol

Modified by **two 3-minute warm-up stages**: **1.7 mph at 0% grade**, then **1.7 mph at 5% grade**. Utilized by **older individuals** or those whose **exercise capacity is limited by cardiac disease**.

---

## Topic 8: Naughton and Weber Protocols

Use **1 to 2 minute stages** with **1 MET increments** between stages. More suitable for patients with **limited exercise tolerance**, such as patients with **compensated congestive heart failure**.

---

## Topic 9: Treadmill Technique

Protocol should be consistent with the patient's physical capacity and the purpose of the test.

> Encourage patients **NOT to grasp the handrails** during exercise, particularly the front handrails. Functional capacity can be **overestimated by as much as 20%** when handrail support is permitted.

---

## Topic 10: GXT Procedure — Patient Prep

- Instruct patient not to eat, drink, or smoke
- Brief history and physical
- Advise about risks and benefits
- Do not test if markedly hypertensive or hypotensive
- Written consent — signed by patient, physician, and technician
- Standard 12-lead ECG in multiple positions
- Instruct on how to perform the test
- Display **minimum 3 leads continuously**
- Record HR, BP, and ECG at the beginning and end of each stage

---

## Topic 11: During GXT — ECG Observations

- **QRS** — ventricular systolic depolarization (cardiac muscle contracted)
- **ST segment** — early repolarization (cardiac muscle relaxed)
- Non-shortening of PR, QRS, and QT with exercise
- P-wave amplitude increases
- J-point depression
- ST segment depression
- ST segment depression with inverted T waves

> ST-segment displacement may **only be seen during exercise**. The patient should not leave until the ECG has returned to baseline.

---

## Topic 12: ST Segment Depression — Thresholds

- ST at rest may occasionally be depressed
- **≥ 0.1 mV / 1 mm** (ischemia) — exercise ECG becomes less specific; consider myocardial imaging
- **≥ 0.5 mm in V2 and V3** also signifies ischemia
- Exercise-induced ST depression **does not localize** the site of ischemia nor identify the artery

---

## Topic 13: Upsloping ST Segments

- **J-point depression** is a normal finding during maximal exercise
- Rapid upsloping ST (> 1 mV/sec) depressed **< 0.15 mV (1.5 mm)** after the J point → **normal**
- Slow upsloping ST depressed **≥ 0.15 mV (1.5 mm)** at **80 ms** after the J point → **abnormal**

---

## Topic 14: ST-Segment Elevation & T Wave Changes

- **≥ 0.10 mV (1 mm) of J-point elevation** is an **abnormal response**; occurs more frequently in **anterior MI**
- T-wave morphology can be influenced by several factors

---

## Topic 15: Maximal Work Capacity

One of the most important prognostic measurements obtained.

- In healthy individuals — influenced by familiarization, level of training, environmental conditions
- In patients with known/suspected CAD — limited exercise capacity → increased risk of fatal and nonfatal CV events (ACS, stroke), regardless of age, sex, race, or abdominal adiposity

---

## Topic 16: Submaximal Exercise

Diagnostic interpretation requires consideration of maximal work capacity. Failing to reach **85–90% of age-predicted maximum** = inadequate test of cardiac reserve.

> **Non-diagnostic test** — common in **peripheral vascular disease, orthopedic limitation, neurologic impairment, poor motivation**. Consider **pharmacologic stress imaging** — vasodilator nuclear stress testing or inotrope stress echo.

---

## Topic 17: Chest Discomfort

- Helpful when diagnosing symptoms
- Exercise-induced chest discomfort usually occurs AFTER the onset of ischemic ST abnormalities and may be associated with diastolic hypertension
- May be the only sign that obstructive CAD is present
- In chronic stable angina, chest discomfort/pain occurs LESS frequently than ischemic ST depression

---

## Topic 18: Blood Pressure

- **Normal** — systolic BP increases progressively with increasing workloads to a peak of **160 – 200 mmHg**
- **Abnormal** — failure to increase SBP > 120 mmHg; sustained drop > 10 mmHg repeatable within 15 seconds; SBP fall below standing resting values during progressive exercise

---

## Topic 19: Heart Rate

- **Normal** — sinus rate increases progressively with exercise; anxiousness can also cause an increase
- Other inappropriate increases — A-fib, deconditioning, hypovolemia, anemia, LV failure (marginal LV function)
- **Abnormal** — HR fails to increase appropriately with exercise

---

## Topic 20: Heart Rate Recovery (HRR)

> **HRR = HR peak − HR one minute later**

| Recovery condition | Threshold for abnormal |
|--------------------|------------------------|
| Upright cool-down | ≤ 12 bpm |
| Any other position | ≤ 18 bpm |
| 2 minutes into recovery | ≤ 22 bpm |

---

## Topic 21: Reasons to Terminate the GXT

- Drop in systolic BP > 10 mmHg from baseline
- Moderate to severe angina
- Ataxia, dizziness, near-syncope
- Cyanosis
- Technical difficulties monitoring ECG or BP
- Subject's desire to stop
- Sustained ventricular tachycardia
- ST elevation ≥ 1.0 mm in leads without diagnostic Q waves (other than V1 or aVR)

---

## Topic 22: Emergency Equipment

- Crash cart and defibrillator always readily available
- Medication for cardiac arrhythmias, AV block, hypotension, persistent chest pain
- IV line in high-risk patients
- Cart and supplies checked daily

---

## Topic 23: Holter Monitor — Definition & Indications

Also known as **Ambulatory Electrocardiographic Recording**. A small, wearable, **continuous** ECG recorder for **24 – 72 hours** (specified in orders).

Indications:

- Syncope
- Dizziness
- Palpitations
- Physical examination
- Arrhythmias
- Preoperative arrhythmia evaluation
- Valvular heart disease
- Post-MI patients

---

## Topic 24: Holter — Patient Prep

1. Properly prep patient for best results
2. Clean the electrode site
3. Shave any necessary chest hair
4. Use gauze or an abrasive pad with cleanser to remove oils, lotions, and excess skin cells
5. Hook up electrodes per diagram
6. Connect the cable into the monitor
7. Insert batteries to power on and obtain a baseline reading
8. Confirm the monitor has an ECG baseline, is on, and displays today's date before the patient leaves

---

## Topic 25: Event Monitor — Overview

When 24–72 hours is incapable of documenting symptoms, **longer-term monitoring** is necessary.

- Device size — about a pager
- Wear period — up to **30 days**
- Digital recordings made during symptomatic episodes; transmitted to a receiving station over standard telephone lines
- Highly effective for **infrequent events**; recording quality more variable than Holter

---

## Topic 26: Loop Event Recorder Mechanics

- Records continuously, with only a small window in memory at any moment
- When the event button is pressed, the current window is **frozen** while the device continues recording for another **30 to 60 seconds** (configurable)
- Stores **> 30 seconds of ECG before** the patient activates the recording
- **Manual systems** — patient must press the button; if syncope occurs without warning and the patient cannot actuate the device, no diagnostic information is captured
- **Auto-trigger systems** — record automatically when heart rate falls outside predetermined parameters

---

## Topic 27: Cell-Phone-Enabled Event Monitors

Some systems incorporate cell phone technology that automatically notifies a central monitoring facility when conditions are met (extreme bradycardia or tachycardia). Information is transmitted via internet/report to the physician for prompt diagnosis and treatment.

Patient prep — same steps as Holter.

---

## Topic 28: Patient Education — Do's and Don'ts

- ✅ Patient should go about their normal daily activities
- ❌ Patient should **not shower or bathe** — do not get monitor wet (sweating is the exception)
- ❌ Do not tamper with the recorder
- ❌ Do not pull on the electrode leads or disturb the electrodes (causes artifact)

---

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **GXT** = Graded Exercise Test; non-invasive; CAD diagnosis + prognosis + functional capacity + therapy response.
- **Optimal duration** — 8–12 minutes of progressive exercise.
- **Bruce** — 3-min stages, healthy patients.
- **Modified Bruce** — adds two 3-min warm-ups (1.7 mph @ 0%, 1.7 mph @ 5%); older / cardiac-limited.
- **Naughton / Weber** — 1–2 min stages, 1 MET increments; limited tolerance / compensated CHF.
- **Handrail rule** — overestimates functional capacity by up to 20%.
- **Acute MI within 2 days** = absolute contraindication.
- **Diagnostic threshold** — ≥ 85–90% of age-predicted maximum HR.
- **Normal peak SBP** — 160–200 mmHg.
- **ST depression** — ≥ 1 mm = ischemia (or ≥ 0.5 mm in V2/V3); does NOT localize.
- **ST elevation** — ≥ 1 mm J-point elevation = abnormal; classic in anterior MI.
- **Slow upsloping ST** — ≥ 1.5 mm at 80 ms past J point = abnormal.
- **HRR (1 min)** — upright ≤ 12 bpm, any other position ≤ 18 bpm, abnormal.
- **HRR (2 min)** — ≤ 22 bpm, abnormal.
- **Termination criteria** — SBP drop > 10 mmHg · moderate-severe angina · ataxia/dizziness/near-syncope · cyanosis · technical issues · patient request · sustained VT · ST elevation ≥ 1 mm in non-diagnostic-Q leads (not V1/aVR).
- **Emergency setup** — crash cart + defibrillator always ready; check daily; IV line in high-risk.
- **Holter** — continuous ECG, 24–72 hours; for frequent / daily events.
- **Event monitor** — up to 30 days; for infrequent events; loop recorder with ≥ 30 s pre-event memory.
- **Trigger types** — manual button-press · automatic (HR-based) · cellular-notify systems.
- **Patient instructions** — normal daily activities · no water (sweat = exception) · no tampering · no pulling on leads (artifact).
