> 1. **The 5-step method (recap)** — STEP 1 ASSESS THE RATE; STEP 2 ASSESS THE RHYTHM/REGULARITY; STEP 3 IDENTIFY AND EXAMINE P WAVES; STEP 4 ASSESS PR INTERVAL; STEP 5 ASSESS QRS COMPLEX. Rate methods covered in Lesson 2 (six-second × 10, 300 ÷ large boxes, 1500 ÷ small boxes, triplicate 300/150/100/75/60/50). Irregular = R-R or P-P variation > 4 SMALL BOXES (0.16 SECONDS). Intrinsic pacemaker rates: SA NODE 60–100, AV JUNCTION 40–60, VENTRICULAR/PURKINJE 20–40.
> 2. **Sinus rhythms — general characteristics** — normal heartbeat starts in the SA NODE, travels the normal pathway. ECG hallmarks: POSITIVE P WAVE BEFORE EACH QRS; P WAVES THAT LOOK ALIKE; CONSISTENT PR INTERVALS; REGULAR ATRIAL AND VENTRICULAR RHYTHM (usually).
> 3. **Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR)** — RATE 60–100 BPM. P-P REGULAR, R-R REGULAR. P WAVES POSITIVE/UPRIGHT IN LEAD II, ONE BEFORE EACH QRS, ALL ALIKE. PR INTERVAL 0.12–0.20 S, CONSTANT. QRS DURATION 0.11 S OR LESS.
> 4. **Sinus bradycardia** — RATE LESS THAN 60 BPM. All other criteria identical to NSR. Common settings: SLEEP, WELL-CONDITIONED ATHLETES, UP TO 35% OF PEOPLE UNDER 25 AT REST.
> 5. **Sinus tachycardia** — SA node fires faster than normal for the patient's age. Begins and ends GRADUALLY. At very fast rates it may be hard to tell P from T. QT interval normally SHORTENS as rate increases. RATE 101–180 BPM. P-P REGULAR, R-R REGULAR. P waves positive/upright in II, one before each QRS, all alike. PR 0.12–0.20 S (may shorten with faster rates), constant. QRS ≤ 0.11 S. CAUSES: EXERCISE, FEVER, PAIN, FEAR OR ANXIETY, HYPOXIA.
> 6. **Sinus arrhythmia** — SA node fires irregularly. R-R interval with 0.16-second difference (more than 4 small boxes). RATE 60–100 BPM. RHYTHM IRREGULAR. Other criteria identical to NSR. Slow variant = SINUS BRADY-ARRHYTHMIA; fast variant = SINUS TACHY-ARRHYTHMIA. Two flavors: RESPIRATORY SINUS ARRHYTHMIA — tied to phases of respiration and intrathoracic pressure; rate INCREASES GRADUALLY DURING INSPIRATION and DECREASES WITH EXPIRATION; most observed in CHILDREN AND ADULTS UNDER 30. NON-RESPIRATORY SINUS ARRHYTHMIA — seen in OLDER INDIVIDUALS and those with HEART DISEASE; may be due to MEDICATION EFFECTS.
> 7. **Sinus arrest / pause** — sinus impulses are NOT GENERATED. When the SA node fails, an escape pacemaker (AV junction or ventricles) SHOULD assume pacing responsibility BUT DOESN'T. Result: ABSENT PQRST COMPLEXES. Rate usually normal, varies due to pause. Rhythm IRREGULAR; pause is OF UNDETERMINED LENGTH and NOT THE SAME DISTANCE as other P-P intervals. Other criteria normal sinus when present. CAUSES: HYPOXIA, MYOCARDIAL ISCHEMIA, MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION.
> 8. **Atrial rhythms — general characteristics** — an IRRITABLE SITE WITHIN THE ATRIA discharges before the next SA node impulse is due. P-wave forms: BIPHASIC (partly positive, partly negative), FLATTENED, NOTCHED, POINTED, or LOST IN THE PRECEDING T WAVE.
> 9. **Premature-beat patterns** (apply to atrial, junctional, and ventricular ectopy): COUPLET = 2 premature beats in a row. RUN OR BURST = 3 or more in a row. BIGEMINY = every OTHER beat is premature. TRIGEMINY = every THIRD beat is premature. QUADRIGEMINY = every FOURTH beat is premature.
> 10. **Premature Atrial Complex (PAC)** — irritable site in the atria fires before the next SA node impulse, INTERRUPTING THE UNDERLYING SINUS RHYTHM. Rate usually within normal range (depends on underlying rhythm). Rhythm REGULAR WITH PREMATURE BEATS. P waves PREMATURE, POSITIVE in lead II, one before each QRS, DIFFER IN SHAPE FROM SINUS P WAVES. PR INTERVAL NORMAL OR PROLONGED depending on prematurity. QRS usually ≤ 0.11 S, may be wide or absent depending on prematurity. CAUSES: ATRIAL ENLARGEMENT, EMOTIONAL STRESS, ELECTROLYTE IMBALANCE, STIMULANTS (caffeine, tobacco, cocaine).
> 11. **Multiformed Atrial Rhythm (MAR)** — formerly WANDERING ATRIAL PACEMAKER. SIZE, SHAPE, AND DIRECTION OF P WAVES VARY. Pacemaker site shifts from SA node to ectopic atrial locations and AV junction. Rate usually 60–100 bpm but may be slow. Rhythm may be IRREGULAR. AT LEAST 3 DIFFERENT P-WAVE CONFIGURATIONS REQUIRED. PR INTERVAL VARIABLE. QRS usually ≤ 0.11 S. CAUSES: DIGITALIS TOXICITY, observed in HEALTHY PATIENTS (athletes in particular), may be observed during SLEEP.
> 12. **Multiformed Atrial Tachycardia (MAT)** — when MAR is associated with VENTRICULAR RATE GREATER THAN 100 BPM. Also known as CHAOTIC ATRIAL TACHYCARDIA. May be a PRECURSOR OF ATRIAL FIBRILLATION. Same morphology rules: pacemaker site shifts; ≥ 3 different P-wave configurations; variable PR; QRS ≤ 0.11 s. CAUSES: SEVERE COPD (classic association), ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES, HYPOKALEMIA, HYPOMAGNESEMIA.
> 13. **Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT) — overview** — arrhythmia that BEGINS ABOVE THE BIFURCATION OF THE BUNDLE OF HIS. Can include rhythms beginning at the SA NODE, ATRIAL TISSUE, or AV JUNCTION. SVT typically occurs AT REST. Often classified as PAROXYSMAL (e.g., PSVT, PAT). PAROXYSMAL = a rhythm that STARTS OR ENDS SUDDENLY.
> 14. **SVT mechanisms — three flavors** — ATRIAL TACHYCARDIA (AT): an irritable site within the atria fires automatically at a very rapid rate. AVNRT (ATRIAL VENTRICULAR NODAL RECIPROCATING TACHYCARDIA): the impulse moves in a RECIPROCATING LOOP around the AV node (junctional) area resulting in a very rapid rate. AVRT (ATRIAL VENTRICULAR RE-ENTRANT TACHYCARDIA): impulse begins above the ventricles but travels through an ECTOPIC PATHWAY. The reciprocating pattern between atria and ventricles results in a very rapid rate; causes PRE-EXCITATION OF THE VENTRICLES.
> 15. **SVT — criteria** — RATE 150–250 BPM. Rhythm typically REGULAR. P waves may or may not be present depending on whether they are BURIED WITHIN PRECEDING T WAVES; if present, differ in shape and size compared with SA-node P waves. PR interval, if present, < 0.12 SECONDS — may be difficult to measure if P waves are hidden in the preceding T wave. QRS usually ≤ 0.11 S. CAUSES: EXCESSIVE CATECHOLAMINE RELEASE, DIGITALIS TOXICITY, ELECTROLYTE IMBALANCE, HEART DISEASE, STIMULANTS (caffeine, albuterol, cocaine).
> 16. **Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome** — most common type of PRE-EXCITATION SYNDROME. Usually seen WHEN PATIENT IS NOT HAVING TACHYCARDIA. Noted to occur in conjunction with ATRIAL FIBRILLATION. Usually goes undetected until it manifests as AVRT. Most common cause of TACHYDYSRHYTHMIAS IN INFANTS AND CHILDREN, with cases leading to SUDDEN CARDIAC DEATH. ECG TRIAD: DELTA WAVE; SHORT PR INTERVAL; WIDENING OF QRS COMPLEX. Rate usually 60–100 bpm if underlying rhythm is sinus. Rhythm regular unless associated with atrial fibrillation. P waves normal and positive in lead II unless WPW is associated with A-fib. PR < 0.12 S if present. QRS usually GREATER THAN 0.12 S; SLURRED UPSTROKE OF THE QRS COMPLEX (DELTA WAVE) may be seen in one or more leads. CAUSES: believed to be CONGENITAL; MORE COMMON IN MEN than women.
> 17. **Atrial Flutter (A-Flutter) — overview** — ectopic atrial rhythm in which an irritable site fires REGULARLY at an EXTREMELY RAPID RATE. Usually paroxysmal but occasionally lasts hours. ECG hallmark: SAW-TOOTH or FLUTTER WAVES.
> 18. **Atrial Flutter — two types** — TYPE I caused by RE-ENTRY; impulse circles around large area of tissue such as the right atrium; heart rate 250–350 BPM. TYPE II referred to as ATYPICAL or VERY RAPID atrial flutter; mechanism not yet defined; heart rate 340–450 BPM. PATIENTS WITH TYPE II OFTEN DEVELOP ATRIAL FIBRILLATION.
> 19. **Atrial Flutter — criteria** — ATRIAL RATE 250–450 BPM, TYPICALLY 300 BPM. VENTRICULAR RATE VARIABLE — determined by AV blockage; usually does NOT EXCEED 180 BPM due to intrinsic conduction rate of AV junction. Atrial rhythm regular; ventricular regular OR irregular depending on AV conduction/blockade. NO IDENTIFIABLE P WAVES — saw-toothed FLUTTER WAVES are present. PR INTERVAL NOT MEASURABLE. QRS ≤ 0.11 S, may be widened if flutter waves are buried in the QRS. CAUSES: CARDIAC SURGERY, CARDIOMYOPATHY, PERICARDITIS, MYOCARDITIS.
> 20. **Atrial Fibrillation (A-Fib)** — caused by ALTERED AUTOMATICITY in one or several rapidly firing sites within the atria, OR RE-ENTRY involving one or more circuits within the atria. Sites in the atria fire at 400–600 BPM, causing the atrial muscles to QUIVER. RISK OF BLOOD CLOTS from POOLING OF BLOOD WITHIN THE ATRIA can lead to STROKE. ECG hallmark: FIBRILLATORY WAVES — a SQUIGGLY LINE — with QRS complexes. ATRIAL RATE 400–600 BPM. VENTRICULAR RATE VARIABLE — CONTROLLED < 100 BPM, UNCONTROLLED > 100 BPM. Ventricular rhythm IRREGULARLY IRREGULAR. NO IDENTIFIABLE P WAVES; FIBRILLATORY WAVES; ERRATIC, WAVY BASELINE. PR NOT MEASURABLE. QRS ≤ 0.11 S unless an intraventricular conduction delay exists. CAUSES: usually with KNOWN CARDIOVASCULAR DISEASE — CAD, HYPERTENSION, CARDIOMYOPATHY, ALCOHOL ABUSE, OBESITY, SIDE EFFECTS TO MEDICATIONS (antihistamines, beta-agonists, local anesthetics).
> 21. **Treatments — atrial rhythms** — CORRECT THE UNDERLYING CAUSE. CONSULT CARDIOLOGIST. Provide SUPPLEMENTAL OXYGEN for symptoms. SYNCHRONIZED CARDIOVERSION for persistent SVT/SVT-type dysrhythmias. ADENOSINE for NARROW-COMPLEX SVT. ANTICOAGULATION for A-FIB if PRESENT FOR 48 HOURS OR MORE.
> 22. **Junctional rhythms — general characteristics** — when the AV JUNCTION (AV node and non-branching portion of bundle of His) paces the heart, the electrical impulse must travel BACKWARD (RETROGRADE) to activate the atria. If a P wave is seen, it will be INVERTED IN LEADS II, III, AND aVF because the impulse is traveling AWAY FROM the positive electrode. Slower rate compared to sinus and atrial rhythms — normally 40–60 BPM. ECG hallmark: P WAVES ARE INVERTED, RETROGRADE, OR MISSING.
> 23. **Premature Junctional Complex (PJC)** — not an entire rhythm; PJCs happen PREMATURELY (early) in a specific underlying rhythm (typically sinus). Occurs when an irritable site within the AV junction fires before the next SA node impulse. INTERRUPTS the sinus rhythm. Because the impulse is conducted through the ventricles in the usual manner, the QRS complex is usually ≤ 0.11 SECONDS. MOST INDIVIDUALS ARE ASYMPTOMATIC. Rate usually within normal range, depends on underlying rhythm. Rhythm regular WITH PREMATURE BEATS. P waves may occur BEFORE, DURING, OR AFTER the QRS; if visible, INVERTED in II, III, aVF. If a P wave occurs before the QRS, the PR interval will usually be ≤ 0.12 S; if no P wave occurs before the QRS, there will be NO PR INTERVAL. QRS ≤ 0.11 S unless aberrantly conducted or intraventricular conduction delay exists. CAUSES: DIGITALIS TOXICITY, ELECTROLYTE IMBALANCE, STIMULANTS, ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES.
> 24. **Junctional Escape Beat (JEB) and Escape Rhythm** — unlike PJCs, junctional escape beats occur LATE — after the next expected sinus beat. Several sequential junctional escape beats = JUNCTIONAL ESCAPE RHYTHM. May be ASYMPTOMATIC; may experience signs/symptoms associated with DECREASED HEART RATE (DIZZINESS, SYNCOPE).
> 25. **Junctional Escape Rhythm — criteria** — RATE 40–60 BPM. RHYTHM VERY REGULAR. P waves may occur before, during, or after the QRS; if visible, INVERTED in II, III, aVF. PR ≤ 0.12 S if P precedes QRS; otherwise no PR interval. QRS ≤ 0.11 S unless aberrantly conducted or IVCD. CAUSES: ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES, EFFECTS OF MEDICATION (BETA-BLOCKERS, DIGITALIS, DILTIAZEM), HYPOXIA, SA NODE DISEASE.
> 26. **Accelerated Junctional Rhythm** — ectopic rhythm caused by ENHANCED AUTOMATICITY of the bundle of His resulting in a regular ventricular response at 61–100 BPM. ECG criteria same as junctional escape rhythm EXCEPT for INCREASED VENTRICULAR RATE. Rate 61–100 bpm. Rhythm very regular. P waves may occur before, during, or after the QRS; if visible, INVERTED in II, III, aVF. PR ≤ 0.12 S if P precedes QRS; otherwise none. QRS ≤ 0.11 S unless aberrantly conducted or IVCD. CAUSES: ACUTE MI, DIGITALIS TOXICITY, HYPOKALEMIA.
> 27. **Junctional Tachycardia** — ectopic rhythm that begins in the pacemaker cell found in the BUNDLE OF HIS. THREE OR MORE SEQUENTIAL PJCs at a rate > 100 BPM. RATE 101–180 BPM. RHYTHM VERY REGULAR. P waves may occur before, during, or after the QRS; if visible, INVERTED in II, III, aVF. PR ≤ 0.12 S if P precedes QRS; otherwise none. QRS ≤ 0.11 S unless aberrantly conducted or IVCD. CAUSES: ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES, DIGITALIS TOXICITY, SIDE EFFECT TO CERTAIN MEDICATIONS.
> 28. **Treatments — junctional rhythms** — CORRECT THE UNDERLYING CAUSE. CONSULT CARDIOLOGIST. Provide SUPPLEMENTAL OXYGEN for symptoms associated with slow/increased heart rate. TEMPORARY PACEMAKER (TRANSCUTANEOUS PACING) for SLOW JUNCTIONAL RHYTHMS. ADENOSINE administration for PERSISTENT JT.
>
> **High-yield distinctions to reinforce:**
> - Sinus = SA node origin → upright P in II, P before every QRS, alike, PR 0.12–0.20 s, narrow QRS.
> - Sinus rate distinctions — NSR 60–100 · brady < 60 · tachy 101–180.
> - Sinus arrhythmia — irregular R-R; respiratory variant common in <30s, increases with inspiration.
> - Sinus arrest — MISSING PQRST; pause length is NOT a multiple of P-P; caused by hypoxia / ischemia / MI.
> - Atrial origin → P-wave morphology is altered (biphasic, flattened, notched, pointed, or buried in T).
> - PAC = early P, different shape from sinus.
> - Patterns — couplet 2 · run/burst ≥ 3 · bigeminy every other · trigeminy every 3rd · quadrigeminy every 4th.
> - MAR — ≥ 3 different P-wave morphologies, rate 60–100. MAT = MAR with rate > 100, classic with severe COPD.
> - SVT 150–250 bpm, regular, P often buried in T; PR < 0.12 s if visible. Three mechanisms: AT, AVNRT, AVRT.
> - WPW triad — DELTA WAVE + SHORT PR + WIDE QRS. Congenital, more common in men, can cause SCD in kids.
> - A-flutter — saw-tooth waves; atrial 250–450 bpm (typically 300); ventricular limited by AV node; PR not measurable.
> - A-fib — IRREGULARLY IRREGULAR + fibrillatory baseline + no P waves; controlled < 100 vs uncontrolled > 100; STROKE risk from atrial clot pooling. Anticoagulate if ≥ 48 h.
> - Atrial treatments — adenosine for narrow SVT · synchronized cardioversion for persistent SVT · anticoagulation for ≥ 48-h A-fib.
> - Junctional origin → INVERTED P in II/III/aVF, or absent, or after QRS; PR ≤ 0.12 s when present; QRS narrow.
> - Junctional rates — escape 40–60 · accelerated 61–100 · tachycardia 101–180.
> - PJC vs escape — PJC EARLY · escape LATE.
> - WPW PR < 0.12 s + WIDE QRS · SVT PR < 0.12 s + NARROW QRS · junctional PR ≤ 0.12 s + NARROW QRS + inverted P.
> - Junctional treatments — transcutaneous pacing for slow · adenosine for persistent JT.

## Topic 1: The 5-Step Method (Recap)

1. **Step 1** — Assess the rate.
2. **Step 2** — Assess the rhythm/regularity.
3. **Step 3** — Identify and examine P waves.
4. **Step 4** — Assess PR interval.
5. **Step 5** — Assess QRS complex.

**Intrinsic pacemaker rates:** SA node 60–100; AV node 40–60; ventricular/Purkinje 20–40.

**Regularity threshold** — R-R or P-P variation > 4 small boxes (0.16 s) = irregular.

---

## Topic 2: Sinus Rhythms — General Characteristics

The normal heartbeat is a result of an electrical impulse that starts in the SA node and travels the normal pathway of conduction, resulting in depolarization.

ECG characteristics include:

- Positive P wave before each QRS complex
- P waves that look alike
- Consistent PR intervals
- Regular atrial and ventricular rhythm (usually)

---

## Topic 3: Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR)

- **Rate** 60–100 bpm
- **Rhythm** P-P regular, R-R regular
- **P waves** Positive/upright in lead II; precede each QRS; all alike
- **PR interval** 0.12–0.20 s, constant beat to beat
- **QRS duration** 0.11 s or less

---

## Topic 4: Sinus Bradycardia

- **Rate** Less than 60 bpm
- **Rhythm** P-P regular, R-R regular
- **P waves** Positive/upright in lead II; precede each QRS; all alike
- **PR interval** 0.12–0.20 s, constant
- **QRS duration** 0.11 s or less

Common settings:

- Adults and children during sleep
- Well-conditioned athletes
- Present in up to 35% of people under 25 years of age while at rest

---

## Topic 5: Sinus Tachycardia

If the SA node fires at a rate faster than normal for the patient's age. Begins and ends gradually. At very fast rates it may be hard to tell the difference between a P wave and a T wave. The QT interval normally shortens as the heart rate increases.

- **Rate** 101–180 bpm
- **Rhythm** P-P regular, R-R regular
- **P waves** Positive/upright in lead II; precede each QRS; all alike
- **PR interval** 0.12–0.20 s (may shorten with faster rates), constant
- **QRS duration** 0.11 s or less

Causes:

- Exercise
- Fever
- Pain
- Fear or anxiety
- Hypoxia

---

## Topic 6: Sinus Arrhythmia

When the SA node fires irregularly. R-R interval with 0.16-second difference.

- **Rate** 60–100 bpm
- **Rhythm** Irregular
- **P waves** Positive/upright in lead II; precede each QRS; all alike
- **PR interval** 0.12–0.20 s, constant
- **QRS duration** 0.11 s or less

If the rate is slower than normal, it's called **sinus brady-arrhythmia**; if faster, **sinus tachy-arrhythmia**.

**Respiratory sinus arrhythmia** — associated with the phases of respiration and changes in intrathoracic pressure. Heart rate increases gradually during inspiration and decreases with expiration. Most often observed in children and adults under 30 years of age.

**Non-respiratory sinus arrhythmia** — seen in older individuals and in those with heart disease. May be due to the effects of medications.

---

## Topic 7: Sinus Arrest / Pause

Sinus impulses are not generated. When the SA node fails to generate an impulse, an escape pacemaker (AV junction or ventricles) should assume pacing responsibility, but it doesn't. Results in absent PQRST complexes.

- **Rate** Usually normal; varies due to the pause
- **Rhythm** Irregular; the pause is of undetermined length and not the same distance as other P-P intervals (due to missing PQRST complexes)
- **P waves** Positive/upright in lead II; precede each QRS; all alike (when present)
- **PR interval** 0.12–0.20 s, constant
- **QRS duration** 0.11 s or less

Causes:

- Hypoxia
- Myocardial ischemia
- Myocardial infarction

---

## Topic 8: Atrial Rhythms — General Characteristics

An irritable site within the atria discharges before the next SA node impulse is due to discharge.

P-wave forms may be:

- Biphasic (partly positive, partly negative)
- Flattened
- Notched
- Pointed
- Lost in the preceding T wave

---

## Topic 9: Premature-Beat Patterns

- **Couplet** — 2 premature beats in a row
- **Run / burst** — 3 or more premature beats in a row
- **Bigeminy** — every other beat is a premature beat
- **Trigeminy** — every third beat is a premature beat
- **Quadrigeminy** — every fourth beat is a premature beat

---

## Topic 10: Premature Atrial Complex (PAC)

Occurs when an irritable site within the atria discharges before the next SA node impulse is due to discharge. Interrupts the underlying sinus rhythm.

- **Rate** Usually within normal range; depends on underlying rhythm
- **Rhythm** Regular with premature beats
- **P waves** Premature, positive in lead II, one before each QRS, **differ in shape** from sinus P waves
- **PR interval** Normal or prolonged depending on prematurity of the beat
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less; may be wide or absent depending on prematurity; QRS shape similar to underlying rhythm unless aberrantly conducted

Causes:

- Atrial enlargement
- Emotional stress
- Electrolyte imbalance
- Stimulants (caffeine, tobacco, cocaine)

---

## Topic 11: Multiformed Atrial Rhythm (MAR)

Formerly known as **Wandering Atrial Pacemaker**. Size, shape, and direction of P waves vary as the pacemaker site shifts from SA node to ectopic atrial sites and the AV junction.

- **Rate** Usually 60–100 bpm; may be slow
- **Rhythm** May be irregular as the pacemaker site shifts
- **P waves** Size, shape, and direction may change from beat to beat. **At least 3 different P-wave configurations required.**
- **PR interval** Variable
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less

Causes:

- Digitalis toxicity
- Observed in healthy patients (athletes in particular)
- May be observed during sleep

---

## Topic 12: Multiformed Atrial Tachycardia (MAT)

When MAR is associated with a ventricular rate greater than 100 bpm. Also known as **chaotic atrial tachycardia**. May be a precursor of atrial fibrillation.

- **Rate** Greater than 100 bpm
- **Rhythm** May be irregular as the pacemaker site shifts
- **P waves** ≥ 3 different P-wave configurations
- **PR interval** Variable
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less

Common causes:

- Severe COPD
- Acute coronary syndromes
- Hypokalemia
- Hypomagnesemia

---

## Topic 13: Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT) — Overview

Arrhythmia that begins above the bifurcation of the bundle of His. SVT can include rhythms that begin at the SA node, atrial tissue, or AV junction. SVT typically occurs at rest. Often classified as **paroxysmal** (e.g., PSVT, PAT). "Paroxysmal" describes a rhythm that either starts or ends suddenly.

---

## Topic 14: SVT Mechanisms

- **AT (Atrial Tachycardia)** — an irritable site within the atria fires automatically at a very rapid rate.
- **AVNRT (Atrial Ventricular Nodal Reciprocating Tachycardia)** — the impulse moves in a reciprocating loop around the AV node (junctional) area, resulting in a very rapid rate.
- **AVRT (Atrial Ventricular Re-entrant Tachycardia)** — impulse begins above the ventricles but travels through an ectopic pathway. The reciprocating pattern between atria and ventricles results in a very rapid rate. Causes pre-excitation of the ventricles.

---

## Topic 15: SVT — Criteria

- **Rate** 150–250 bpm
- **Rhythm** Typically regular
- **P waves** May or may not be present depending on whether they are buried within preceding T waves. If P waves are present, they will differ in shape and size compared to P waves generated by the SA node.
- **PR interval** If present, < 0.12 s. May be difficult to measure if P waves are hidden in the preceding T wave.
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less

Causes:

- Excessive catecholamine release
- Digitalis toxicity
- Electrolyte imbalance
- Heart disease
- Stimulants (caffeine, albuterol, cocaine)

---

## Topic 16: Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome

Most common type of pre-excitation syndrome. Usually seen when the patient is not having tachycardia. Noted to occur in conjunction with atrial fibrillation. Usually goes undetected until it manifests as AVRT. Most common cause of tachydysrhythmias in infants and children, with cases leading to sudden cardiac death.

ECG characteristics include:

- Delta wave
- Short PR interval
- Widening of QRS complex

- **Rate** Usually 60–100 bpm if the underlying rhythm is sinus in origin
- **Rhythm** Regular, unless associated with atrial fibrillation
- **P waves** Normal and positive in lead II unless WPW is associated with atrial fibrillation
- **PR interval** If present, < 0.12 s
- **QRS duration** Usually greater than 0.12 s; slurred upstroke of the QRS complex (delta wave) may be seen in one or more leads

Causes:

- Believed to be congenital
- More common in men than in women

---

## Topic 17: Atrial Flutter — Overview

Ectopic atrial rhythm in which an irritable site fires regularly at an extremely rapid rate. Usually paroxysmal but occasionally can last for hours.

ECG hallmark: **"saw-tooth" or "flutter waves."**

---

## Topic 18: Atrial Flutter — Two Types

- **Type I** — caused by re-entry. Impulse circles around a large area of tissue such as the right atrium. Heart rate ranges from **250–350 bpm**.
- **Type II** — referred to as "atypical" or "very rapid atrial flutter." Mechanism not yet defined. Heart rate usually **340–450 bpm**. Patients with Type II atrial flutter often develop atrial fibrillation.

---

## Topic 19: Atrial Flutter — Criteria

- **Atrial rate** 250–450 bpm, typically 300 bpm
- **Ventricular rate** Variable — determined by AV blockage; usually does not exceed 180 bpm due to the intrinsic conduction rate of the AV junction
- **Rhythm** Atrial regular; ventricular regular or irregular depending on AV conduction/blockade
- **P waves** No identifiable P waves; saw-toothed flutter waves are present
- **PR interval** Not measurable
- **QRS duration** 0.11 s or less; may be widened if flutter waves are buried in the QRS complex

Causes:

- Cardiac surgery
- Cardiomyopathy
- Pericarditis
- Myocarditis

---

## Topic 20: Atrial Fibrillation (A-Fib)

Occurs because of altered automaticity in one or several rapidly firing sites within the atria, or re-entry involving one or more circuits within the atria. Sites in the atria fire at a rate of 400–600 bpm, causing the muscles of the atria to "quiver." **Risk of blood clots from pooling of blood within the atria can lead to possible stroke.**

ECG hallmark: fibrillatory waves or "squiggly line" with QRS complexes.

- **Atrial rate** 400–600 bpm
- **Ventricular rate** Variable: controlled < 100 bpm; uncontrolled > 100 bpm
- **Rhythm** Ventricular rhythm usually irregularly irregular
- **P waves** No identifiable P waves; fibrillatory waves present; erratic, wavy baseline
- **PR interval** Not measurable
- **QRS duration** 0.11 s or less if an intraventricular conduction delay exists

Causes — usually with known cardiovascular disease:

- CAD
- Hypertension
- Cardiomyopathy
- Alcohol abuse
- Obesity
- Side effects to medications (antihistamines, beta-agonists, local anesthetics)

---

## Topic 21: Treatments — Atrial Rhythms

- Correct the underlying cause
- Consult cardiologist
- Provide supplemental oxygen for symptoms
- **Synchronized cardioversion** for persistent SVT/SVT-type dysrhythmias
- **Adenosine** for narrow-complex SVT
- **Anticoagulation** for A-fib if present for **48 hours or more**

---

## Topic 22: Junctional Rhythms — General Characteristics

If the AV junction (AV node and non-branching portion of bundle of His) paces the heart, the electrical impulse must travel in a backward (retrograde) direction to activate the atria. If a P wave is seen, it will be inverted in leads II, III, and aVF because the impulse is traveling away from the positive electrode. This results in a slower rate compared to sinus and atrial rhythms (normally 40–60 bpm).

ECG hallmark: P waves are either inverted, retrograde, or missing.

---

## Topic 23: Premature Junctional Complex (PJC)

Not an entire rhythm; PJCs happen prematurely (early) in a specific underlying rhythm (typically sinus). Occurs when an irritable site within the AV junction fires before the next SA node impulse is due to fire. This interrupts the sinus rhythm. Because the impulse is conducted through the ventricles in the usual manner, the QRS complex will usually measure 0.11 s or less.

Most individuals are asymptomatic.

- **Rate** Usually within normal range; depends on underlying rhythm
- **Rhythm** Regular with premature beats
- **P waves** May occur before, during, or after the QRS; if visible, the P wave is inverted in leads II, III, and aVF
- **PR interval** If a P wave occurs before the QRS, the PR interval will usually be 0.12 s or less; if no P wave occurs before the QRS, there will be no PR interval
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less unless aberrantly conducted or an intraventricular conduction delay exists

Causes:

- Digitalis toxicity
- Electrolyte imbalance
- Stimulants
- Acute coronary syndromes

---

## Topic 24: Junctional Escape Beat (JEB) and Escape Rhythm

Unlike PJCs, junctional escape beats occur **late** — after the next expected sinus beat. Several sequential junctional escape beats form a **junctional escape rhythm**.

May be asymptomatic; may experience signs and symptoms associated with decreased heart rate (dizziness, syncope).

---

## Topic 25: Junctional Escape Rhythm — Criteria

- **Rate** 40–60 bpm
- **Rhythm** Very regular
- **P waves** May occur before, during, or after the QRS; if visible, inverted in leads II, III, and aVF
- **PR interval** If a P wave occurs before the QRS, the PR interval will usually be 0.12 s or less; if no P wave occurs before the QRS, there will be no PR interval
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less unless aberrantly conducted or an intraventricular conduction delay exists

Causes:

- Acute coronary syndromes
- Effects of medication (beta-blockers, digitalis, diltiazem)
- Hypoxia
- SA node disease

---

## Topic 26: Accelerated Junctional Rhythm

An ectopic rhythm caused by enhanced automaticity of the bundle of His resulting in a regular ventricular response at a rate of 61–100 bpm. The ECG criteria are the same as junctional escape rhythm with the exception of an increased ventricular rate.

- **Rate** 61–100 bpm
- **Rhythm** Very regular
- **P waves** May occur before, during, or after the QRS; if visible, inverted in II, III, aVF
- **PR interval** If P precedes QRS, PR ≤ 0.12 s; if not, no PR interval
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less unless aberrantly conducted or IVCD

Causes:

- Acute MI
- Digitalis toxicity
- Hypokalemia

---

## Topic 27: Junctional Tachycardia

An ectopic rhythm that begins in the pacemaker cell found in the bundle of His. Three or more sequential PJCs at a rate > 100 bpm.

- **Rate** 101–180 bpm
- **Rhythm** Very regular
- **P waves** May occur before, during, or after the QRS; if visible, inverted in II, III, aVF
- **PR interval** If P precedes QRS, PR ≤ 0.12 s; if not, no PR interval
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less unless aberrantly conducted or IVCD

Causes:

- Acute coronary syndromes
- Digitalis toxicity
- Side effect to certain medications

---

## Topic 28: Treatments — Junctional Rhythms

- Correct the underlying cause
- Consult cardiologist
- Provide supplemental oxygen for symptoms associated with slow/increased heart rate
- **Temporary pacemaker (transcutaneous pacing)** for slow junctional rhythms
- **Adenosine** administration for persistent JT

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Sinus rhythms** — SA-node origin: upright P in II · P before every QRS · P waves alike · PR 0.12–0.20 s · narrow QRS.
- **Sinus rate distinctions** — NSR 60–100 · brady < 60 · tachy 101–180.
- **Sinus arrhythmia** — irregular R-R; respiratory variant in <30s, increases with inspiration.
- **Sinus arrest** — missing PQRST; pause is undetermined and not a multiple of P-P; hypoxia / ischemia / MI.
- **Atrial origin** — abnormal P morphology (biphasic, flattened, notched, pointed, or buried in T).
- **PAC** — early P with different shape than sinus P.
- **Premature-beat patterns** — couplet 2 · run/burst ≥ 3 · bigeminy every other · trigeminy every 3rd · quadrigeminy every 4th.
- **MAR** — ≥ 3 different P morphologies, rate 60–100.
- **MAT** — MAR + rate > 100; classic with **severe COPD**.
- **SVT** — 150–250 bpm, regular, P often buried in T; PR < 0.12 s if visible. Three mechanisms — AT, AVNRT, AVRT.
- **WPW triad** — DELTA WAVE + SHORT PR + WIDE QRS. Congenital, more common in men, causes SCD in kids.
- **A-flutter** — saw-tooth waves; atrial 250–450 bpm (typically 300); ventricular limited by AV node; PR not measurable.
- **A-fib** — IRREGULARLY IRREGULAR + fibrillatory baseline + no P waves; controlled < 100 vs uncontrolled > 100; STROKE risk; anticoagulate if ≥ 48 h.
- **Atrial treatments** — adenosine for narrow SVT · synchronized cardioversion for persistent SVT · anticoagulation for ≥ 48-h A-fib · O₂ for symptoms.
- **Junctional origin** — INVERTED P in II/III/aVF, or absent, or after QRS · PR ≤ 0.12 s when present · narrow QRS.
- **Junctional rates** — escape 40–60 · accelerated 61–100 · tachycardia 101–180.
- **PJC vs escape** — PJC EARLY · escape LATE.
- **WPW** PR < 0.12 s + WIDE QRS · **SVT** PR < 0.12 s + NARROW QRS · **junctional** PR ≤ 0.12 s + NARROW QRS + inverted P.
- **Junctional treatments** — transcutaneous pacing for slow junctional · adenosine for persistent JT.
