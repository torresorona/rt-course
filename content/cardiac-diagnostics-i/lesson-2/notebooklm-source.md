> 1. **Mean QRS axis — definition** — the average direction of depolarization that spreads through the ventricles to stimulate the muscle to contract. Many waves of depolarization happen at the same time spreading out from the SA node; the SUMMATION of all these waves gives the mean QRS axis.
> 2. **Hexaxial reference system** — formulated using the SIX LIMB LEADS of Einthoven's triangle. Leads are superimposed crossing in the CENTER OF A CIRCLE, with 30 DEGREES BETWEEN EACH LEAD. Each lead is assigned a number in degrees around the circle with positive AND negative numbers. The hexaxial reference system is used PRIMARILY TO DETERMINE MEAN QRS AXIS. Lead angles to memorize: I = 0°; II = +60°; aVF = +90°; III = +120°; aVR = −150°; aVL = −30°.
> 3. **Axis deviation — quadrant ranges** — memorize cold: NORMAL AXIS = 0° to +90°. LEFT AXIS DEVIATION = −1° to −90°. RIGHT AXIS DEVIATION = +90° to ±180°. EXTREME RIGHT AXIS DEVIATION = −91° to −179°.
> 4. **Quick look method** — use net deflection of LEAD I and LEAD aVF: BOTH POSITIVE → NORMAL. LEAD I POSITIVE, aVF NEGATIVE → LEFT AXIS DEVIATION. LEAD I NEGATIVE, aVF POSITIVE → RIGHT AXIS DEVIATION. BOTH NEGATIVE → EXTREME RIGHT AXIS DEVIATION.
> 5. **Right axis deviation — causes** — EMPHYSEMA, RIGHT VENTRICULAR HYPERTROPHY, COPD, PULMONARY EMBOLISM, LEFT VENTRICULAR MI, DEXTROCARDIA.
> 6. **Left axis deviation — causes** — HIGH DIAPHRAGM, PREGNANCY, ABDOMINAL TUMORS, HYPERKALEMIA, LEFT VENTRICULAR HYPERTROPHY, RIGHT VENTRICULAR MI.
> 7. **Coronary artery distribution & lead mapping** — leads II, III, and aVF examine tissue supplied by the RIGHT CORONARY ARTERY (RCA). Leads I, aVL, and V1–V6 evaluate tissue supplied by the LEFT CORONARY ARTERY (LCA).
> 8. **Myocardial injury — ST elevation** — ST elevation indicates ACUTE MYOCARDIAL INJURY. Criteria: ELEVATION EQUAL TO OR GREATER THAN 1 mm ABOVE THE ISOELECTRIC LINE at a point 0.04 SECONDS TO THE RIGHT OF THE J POINT, seen in 3 OR MORE CONSECUTIVE LEADS.
> 9. **STEMI evolution — four phases** — HYPERACUTE PHASE: T wave measuring MORE THAN 50% of the preceding R wave. EARLY ACUTE PHASE: ST ELEVATION in conjunction with a TALL T WAVE (UPSLOPING). LATER ACUTE PHASE: ST ELEVATION in conjunction with T WAVE INVERSION. FULLY EVOLVED PHASE: DOWN-SLOPING ST elevation with T WAVE INVERSION and DEEP ABNORMAL Q WAVES — the FIRST SIGN that TISSUE DEATH HAS OCCURRED.
> 10. **NSTEMI** — Non-ST Elevation Myocardial Infarction. NO SIGNS OF ST ELEVATION on ECG. Diagnosis is made based on the patient's SIGNS AND SYMPTOMS, HISTORY, and CARDIAC BIOMARKER TEST RESULTS (SERUM ENZYMES).
> 11. **Myocardial ischemia** — ST SEGMENT DEPRESSION: more than 0.5 mm in LEADS V2 AND V3, and greater than 1 mm IN ALL OTHER LEADS. Also: PRESENCE OF INVERTED T WAVES.
> 12. **Septal wall infarction/ischemia** — leads V1 AND V2 face the SEPTAL AREA of the left ventricle. The SEPTUM, which contains the BUNDLE OF HIS AND BUNDLE BRANCHES, is normally supplied by the LAD ARTERY.
> 13. **Anterior wall infarction/ischemia** — leads V3 AND V4 face the ANTERIOR WALL of the left ventricle. The LAD artery supplies APPROXIMATELY 40% OF THE HEART'S BLOOD and a critical section of the left ventricle. A blockage here can lead to complications: HEART FAILURE, VENTRICULAR DYSFUNCTION, and CARDIOGENIC SHOCK. If the ENTIRE ANTERIOR WALL is involved, ECG changes will be visible in V1, V2, V3, AND V4.
> 14. **Lateral wall infarction/ischemia** — leads I, aVL, V5, AND V6 view the LATERAL WALL of the left ventricle. The lateral wall may be supplied by the LEFT CIRCUMFLEX ARTERY, the LEFT ANTERIOR DESCENDING ARTERY, or a BRANCH OF THE RIGHT CORONARY ARTERY.
> 15. **Inferior wall infarction/ischemia** — leads II, III, AND aVF view the INFERIOR SURFACE of the left ventricle. The inferior wall of the LV is supplied by the POSTERIOR DESCENDING BRANCH of the RCA. INCREASED PARASYMPATHETIC NERVOUS SYSTEM ACTIVITY is common with inferior wall MI.
> 16. **Posterior wall infarction/ischemia** — on a STANDARD 12-LEAD ECG, posterior surfaces of the heart are NOT DIRECTLY VIEWED. ADDITIONAL CHEST LEADS may be used: V7, V8, AND V9, placed further LEFT and TOWARDS THE BACK. The posterior wall is supplied by the CIRCUMFLEX CORONARY ARTERY in most patients; in some patients, by the RIGHT CORONARY ARTERY.
> 17. **Right ventricular infarction (RVI)** — the right ventricle is supplied by the RIGHT VENTRICULAR MARGINAL BRANCH of the right coronary artery. OCCLUSION of the RV MARGINAL BRANCH alone results in an ISOLATED RIGHT VENTRICULAR INFARCTION. OCCLUSION of the RCA PROXIMAL TO the RV marginal branch results in an INFERIOR AND RIGHT VENTRICULAR INFARCTION. Clinical evidence of RVI involves THREE COMPONENTS: HYPOTENSION, JUGULAR VENOUS DISTENTION, and CLEAR BREATH SOUNDS.
> 18. **Right atrial enlargement** — produces an ABNORMALLY TALL INITIAL PART of the P wave. P wave may be BIPHASIC IN LEAD V1. Usually caused by conditions that INCREASE THE WORKLOAD of the right atrium.
> 19. **Left atrial enlargement** — the LATTER PART of the P wave is WIDENED. Takes longer to depolarize the enlarged muscle. P WAVE GREATER THAN 0.11 SECONDS in duration. Often NOTCHED in leads I, II, aVL, V4, V5, AND V6. Occurs because of conditions that INCREASE LEFT ATRIAL PRESSURE, VOLUME OVERLOAD, or both.
> 20. **Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH)** — abnormalities include INCREASED VOLTAGES, ST-T CHANGES with marked T WAVE INVERSION in the LATERAL PRECORDIAL LEADS. LVH may be accompanied by LEFT AXIS DEVIATION. Recognized by: DEEPER-THAN-NORMAL S WAVES, SMALL R WAVES in V1–V2; TALLER R WAVES, SMALL S WAVES in V5 AND V6. The voltage criterion: if the S WAVE AMPLITUDE IN LEAD V1 ADDED TO the R WAVE AMPLITUDE IN V5 is 35 mV OR MORE, suspect LVH.
> 21. **Right ventricular hypertrophy (RVH)** — RIGHT AXIS DEVIATION is one of the EARLIEST AND MOST RELIABLE FINDINGS. Recognized by: NORMAL R WAVE PROGRESSION IS REVERSED in the chest leads — INCREASED IN V1, PROGRESSIVELY SMALLER going toward V6.
> 22. **Sodium (135–145 mEq/L)** — HYPERNATREMIA from deficient water intake, excessive salt ingestion, severe watery diarrhea, water loss. HYPONATREMIA from prolonged diuretic therapy, excessive sodium loss from trauma, prolonged vomiting or diarrhea/laxative use. CRITICAL POINT: SODIUM DISTURBANCES DO NOT CAUSE ANY SIGNIFICANT CHANGES ON THE ECG.
> 23. **Calcium (4.5–5.5 mEq/L)** — HYPERCALCEMIA from excessive vitamin D or calcium intake — causes a PROLONGED PR INTERVAL on ECG. HYPOCALCEMIA from acute or chronic renal failure, or vitamin D deficiency — causes LONG, FLAT ST SEGMENTS and a PROLONGED QT INTERVAL on ECG.
> 24. **Potassium (3.5–5.0 mEq/L)** — HYPERKALEMIA from excessive potassium supplements or salt substitutes — causes WIDE QRS COMPLEXES and PROLONGED PR INTERVALS on ECG. HYPOKALEMIA from inadequate dietary potassium, prolonged vomiting, diarrhea/laxative use — causes a DEPRESSED ST SEGMENT on ECG.
> 25. **Five steps to interpreting an ECG** — STEP 1 ASSESS THE RATE; STEP 2 ASSESS THE RHYTHM/REGULARITY; STEP 3 IDENTIFY AND EXAMINE P WAVES; STEP 4 ASSESS PR INTERVAL; STEP 5 ASSESS QRS COMPLEX.
> 26. **Intrinsic pacemaker rates** — SA NODE = 60 to 100 beats/min. AV NODE = 40 to 60 beats/min. VENTRICULAR / PURKINJE FIBERS = 20 to 40 beats/min.
> 27. **Six-second method** — SIMPLEST, QUICKEST, and MOST COMMONLY USED, but ALSO THE MOST INACCURATE. Works for REGULAR AND IRREGULAR rhythms. VENTRICULAR RATE: count QRS complexes in a 6-second window and MULTIPLY BY 10. ATRIAL RATE: count P waves in a 6-second window and MULTIPLY BY 10.
> 28. **Large box method** — best for REGULAR rhythms. 300 LARGE BOXES = 1 MINUTE. Count the number of large boxes between R-R and DIVIDE INTO 300.
> 29. **Small box method** — best for REGULAR rhythms. 1500 SMALL BOXES = 1 MINUTE. VENTRICULAR RATE: count small boxes between R-R, DIVIDE INTO 1500. ATRIAL RATE: count small boxes between P-P, DIVIDE INTO 1500.
> 30. **Triplicate method** — best for REGULAR rhythms. Select an R wave that falls on a DARK VERTICAL LINE. Number the next six consecutive dark vertical lines: 300, 150, 100, 75, 60, 50. Where the NEXT R wave falls in relation to those lines is the rate.
> 31. **Step 2 — rhythm/regularity** — measure the distance between the P-P INTERVAL for atrial rhythm. Measure the R-R INTERVAL for ventricular rhythm. If MORE THAN 4 SMALL BOXES (0.16 SECONDS) of variation, the rhythm is IRREGULAR.
> 32. **Step 3 — P waves** — normally P waves are LEFT OF EACH QRS, PRECEDE EACH QRS, and LOOK SIMILAR in size, shape, and position. Three questions: ARE P WAVES PRESENT? IS THERE A P WAVE BEFORE EVERY QRS? DO THEY ALL LOOK THE SAME?
> 33. **Step 4 — PR interval** — ONSET of P wave to ONSET of QRS complex. NORMAL = 0.12 to 0.20 SECONDS.
> 34. **Step 5 — QRS duration** — first wave begins to deviate from baseline until the last wave levels out. NARROW QRS = 0.11 SECONDS OR LESS. WIDE QRS = MORE THAN 0.11 SECONDS.
>

> - Hexaxial system uses the 6 LIMB LEADS, 30° between each lead. Used PRIMARILY for mean QRS axis.
> - Axis quadrants — NORMAL 0°→+90° · LAD −1°→−90° · RAD +90°→±180° · EXTREME RAD −91°→−179°.
> - Quick look — I↑ aVF↑ NORMAL · I↑ aVF↓ LAD · I↓ aVF↑ RAD · I↓ aVF↓ EXTREME RAD.
> - RAD causes — emphysema, RVH, COPD, PE, LV MI, dextrocardia.
> - LAD causes — high diaphragm, pregnancy, abdominal tumors, hyperkalemia, LVH, RV MI.
> - Coronary mapping — II/III/aVF = RCA · I/aVL/V1–V6 = LCA.
> - ST elevation criteria — ≥ 1 mm at 0.04 s past J point, in 3+ consecutive leads.
> - STEMI evolution — Hyperacute T (>50% of R) → ST↑ + tall upsloping T → ST↑ + T inversion → down-sloping ST↑ + T inversion + deep abnormal Q (= tissue death, fully evolved).
> - NSTEMI — no ST elevation; diagnose by S/Sx + history + cardiac biomarkers (serum enzymes).
> - ST depression — > 0.5 mm in V2/V3, > 1 mm elsewhere; T wave inversion may be present.

> - Anterior wall complications — heart failure, ventricular dysfunction, cardiogenic shock; entire anterior wall = changes in V1–V4.
> - RVI clinical triad — HYPOTENSION + JVD + CLEAR BREATH SOUNDS. Proximal RCA occlusion → inferior + RVI; isolated RV marginal occlusion → isolated RVI.
> - RAE — abnormally tall initial P wave; biphasic in V1; RA workload (pulmonary HTN, COPD, tricuspid disease).
> - LAE — widened latter P wave > 0.11 s; notched in I, II, aVL, V4–V6; LA pressure / volume overload.
> - LVH — deep S in V1–V2, tall R in V5–V6; voltage rule S(V1) + R(V5) ≥ 35 mV; often paired with LAD; T inversion in lateral precordial leads.
> - RVH — right axis deviation = EARLIEST + most reliable finding; reversed R-wave progression — large in V1, smaller toward V6.

> - Calcium — ↑Ca → prolonged PR · ↓Ca → long flat ST + prolonged QT.
> - Potassium — ↑K → wide QRS + prolonged PR · ↓K → ST depression.
> - 5-step method — RATE → RHYTHM → P WAVES → PR INTERVAL → QRS DURATION (in that order, every time).
> - Intrinsic rates — SA 60–100 · AV 40–60 · ventricular/Purkinje 20–40.
> - Rate methods — 6-SECOND × 10 (any rhythm, least accurate) · 300 ÷ large boxes (regular) · 1500 ÷ small boxes (regular) · TRIPLICATE 300/150/100/75/60/50 (regular).
> - Regularity — > 4 small boxes (0.16 s) variation = IRREGULAR.
> - PR normal 0.12–0.20 s. QRS narrow ≤ 0.11 s, wide > 0.11 s.

## Topic 1: Mean QRS Axis — Definition

The **mean QRS axis** is the **average direction** of depolarization that spreads through the ventricles to stimulate the muscle to contract.

Many waves of depolarization occur at the same time spreading out from the SA node. The **summation** of all these waves gives the mean QRS axis.

---

## Topic 2: Hexaxial Reference System

- Formulated using the **6 limb leads** of Einthoven's triangle.
- Leads are superimposed crossing in the **center of a circle**.
- **30 degrees between each lead.**
- Each lead is assigned a number in degrees around the circle with **positive and negative numbers**.
- **Primarily used to determine mean QRS axis.**

Lead angles: I = 0°, II = +60°, aVF = +90°, III = +120°, aVR = −150°, aVL = −30°.

---

## Topic 3: Axis Deviation — Quadrant Ranges

- **Normal axis:** 0° to +90°
- **Left axis deviation:** −1° to −90°
- **Right axis deviation:** +90° to ±180°
- **Extreme right axis deviation:** −91° to −179°

---

## Topic 4: Quick Look Method

Using the net deflection of **lead I** and **lead aVF**:

- **I positive, aVF positive** → normal axis
- **I positive, aVF negative** → left axis deviation
- **I negative, aVF positive** → right axis deviation
- **I negative, aVF negative** → extreme right axis deviation

---

## Topic 5: Right Axis Deviation — Causes

- Emphysema
- Right ventricular hypertrophy
- COPD
- Pulmonary embolism
- Left ventricular MI
- Dextrocardia

---

## Topic 6: Left Axis Deviation — Causes

- High diaphragm
- Pregnancy
- Abdominal tumors
- Hyperkalemia
- Left ventricular hypertrophy
- Right ventricular MI

---

## Topic 7: Coronary Artery Distribution & Lead Mapping

- **II, III, aVF** examine tissue supplied by the **right coronary artery (RCA)**.
- **I, aVL, V1–V6** evaluate tissue supplied by the **left coronary artery (LCA)**.

---

## Topic 8: Myocardial Injury — ST Elevation

ST elevation indicates **acute myocardial injury**.

> Equal to or greater than **1 mm above the isoelectric line** at a point **0.04 seconds to the right of the J point**, seen in **3 or more consecutive leads**.

---

## Topic 9: STEMI Evolution — Four Phases

- **Hyperacute** — T wave measuring more than 50% of the preceding R wave.
- **Early acute** — ST elevation in conjunction with a tall T wave (upsloping).
- **Later acute** — ST elevation in conjunction with T wave inversion.
- **Fully evolved** — down-sloping ST elevation with T wave inversion and **deep abnormal Q waves**. **First sign that tissue death has occurred.**

---

## Topic 10: NSTEMI

- **No signs of ST elevation** on ECG.
- Diagnosis is made based on the patient's:
  - Signs and symptoms
  - History
  - Cardiac biomarker test results (serum enzymes)

---

## Topic 11: Myocardial Ischemia

- **ST segment depression** — more than **0.5 mm in V2 & V3**, and **> 1 mm in all other leads**.
- **Inverted T waves** may be present.

---

## Topic 12: Septal Wall Infarction / Ischemia

- **Leads V1 and V2** face the **septal area** of the left ventricle.
- The septum, which contains the **bundle of His and bundle branches**, is normally supplied by the **LAD artery**.

---

## Topic 13: Anterior Wall Infarction / Ischemia

- **Leads V3 and V4** face the **anterior wall** of the left ventricle.
- The **LAD artery** supplies approximately **40% of the heart's blood** and a critical section of the left ventricle.
- A blockage in this area can lead to complications: **heart failure, ventricular dysfunction, and cardiogenic shock**.
- If the **entire anterior wall** is involved, ECG changes are visible in **V1, V2, V3, and V4**.

---

## Topic 14: Lateral Wall Infarction / Ischemia

- **Leads I, aVL, V5, and V6** view the **lateral wall** of the left ventricle.
- May be supplied by the **left circumflex artery, the LAD, or a branch of the right coronary artery**.

---

## Topic 15: Inferior Wall Infarction / Ischemia

- **Leads II, III, and aVF** view the **inferior surface** of the left ventricle.
- The inferior wall of the LV is supplied by the **posterior descending branch of the RCA**.
- **Increased parasympathetic nervous system activity** is common with inferior wall MI.

---

## Topic 16: Posterior Wall Infarction / Ischemia

- On a standard 12-lead ECG, the posterior surfaces of the heart are **not directly viewed**.
- Additional chest leads may be used: **V7, V8, V9** placed further left and toward the back.
- The posterior wall is supplied by the **circumflex coronary artery** in most patients; in some patients by the **right coronary artery**.

---

## Topic 17: Right Ventricular Infarction (RVI)

- The right ventricle is supplied by the **right ventricular marginal branch** of the right coronary artery.
- **Occlusion of the RV marginal branch** alone → **isolated right ventricular infarction**.
- **Occlusion of the RCA proximal to the RV marginal branch** → **inferior and right ventricular infarction**.

**Clinical evidence of RVI involves three components:**

- Hypotension
- Jugular venous distention
- Clear breath sounds

---

## Topic 18: Right Atrial Enlargement (RAE)

- Produces an **abnormally tall initial part** of the P wave.
- P wave may be **biphasic in lead V1**.
- Usually caused by conditions that **increase the workload of the right atrium**.

---

## Topic 19: Left Atrial Enlargement (LAE)

- The **latter part of the P wave is widened** (takes longer to depolarize the enlarged muscle).
- **P wave > 0.11 seconds** in duration.
- Often **notched** in leads **I, II, aVL, V4, V5, and V6**.
- Occurs because of conditions that **increase left atrial pressure, volume overload, or both**.

---

## Topic 20: Left Ventricular Hypertrophy (LVH)

- Abnormalities include **increased voltages**, ST-T changes with marked **T wave inversion in the lateral precordial leads**.
- LVH may be accompanied by **left axis deviation**.
- Recognized by:
  - **Deeper-than-normal S waves**, **small R waves** in V1–V2.
  - **Taller R waves**, **small S waves** in V5 and V6.
- **Voltage criterion:** if the **S wave amplitude in V1** added to the **R wave amplitude in V5** is **≥ 35 mV**, suspect LVH.

---

## Topic 21: Right Ventricular Hypertrophy (RVH)

- **Right axis deviation** is one of the **earliest and most reliable findings**.
- Recognized by:
  - **Normal R wave progression is reversed** in the chest leads.
  - **R waves increased in V1**, becoming **progressively smaller toward V6**.

---

## Topic 22: Sodium (135–145 mEq/L)

- **Hypernatremia** — deficient water intake, excessive salt ingestion, severe watery diarrhea, water loss.
- **Hyponatremia** — prolonged diuretic therapy, excessive sodium loss from trauma, prolonged vomiting or diarrhea/laxative use.
- **Sodium disturbances do NOT cause significant changes on the ECG.**

---

## Topic 23: Calcium (4.5–5.5 mEq/L)

- **Hypercalcemia** — excessive vitamin D or calcium intake. **Causes prolonged PR interval.**
- **Hypocalcemia** — acute/chronic renal failure or vitamin D deficiency. **Causes long, flat ST segments and prolonged QT interval.**

---

## Topic 24: Potassium (3.5–5.0 mEq/L)

- **Hyperkalemia** — excessive potassium supplements or salt substitutes. **Causes wide QRS complexes and prolonged PR intervals.**
- **Hypokalemia** — inadequate dietary potassium, prolonged vomiting, diarrhea/laxative use. **Causes depressed ST segment.**

---

## Topic 25: Five Steps to Interpreting an ECG

1. **Step 1** — Assess the rate
2. **Step 2** — Assess the rhythm/regularity
3. **Step 3** — Identify and examine P waves
4. **Step 4** — Assess PR interval
5. **Step 5** — Assess QRS complex

---

## Topic 26: Intrinsic Pacemaker Rates

- **SA node** = 60 to 100 beats/min
- **AV node** = 40 to 60 beats/min
- **Ventricular / Purkinje fibers** = 20 to 40 beats/min

---

## Topic 27: Six-Second Method

- Simplest, quickest, **most commonly used**, but also the **most inaccurate**.
- Works for **regular AND irregular** rhythms.
- **Ventricular rate** — count QRS complexes within a 6-second window and **multiply by 10**.
- **Atrial rate** — count P waves within a 6-second window and **multiply by 10**.

---

## Topic 28: Large Box Method

- Best for **regular** rhythms.
- **300 large boxes = 1 minute.**
- **Rate = 300 ÷ (number of large boxes between R-R).**

---

## Topic 29: Small Box Method

- Best for **regular** rhythms.
- **1500 small boxes = 1 minute.**
- **Ventricular rate** — count small boxes between R-R, divide into 1500.
- **Atrial rate** — count small boxes between P-P, divide into 1500.

---

## Topic 30: Triplicate Method

- Best for **regular** rhythms.
- Select an **R wave that falls on a dark vertical line**.
- Number the next six consecutive dark vertical lines: **300, 150, 100, 75, 60, 50**.
- Where the **next R wave** falls in relation to those lines is the rate.

---

## Topic 31: Step 2 — Rhythm / Regularity

- Measure the distance between the **P-P interval** for **atrial rhythm**.
- Measure the distance between the **R-R interval** for **ventricular rhythm**.
- If variation is **more than 4 small boxes (0.16 seconds)**, the rhythm is **irregular**.

---

## Topic 32: Step 3 — P Waves

Normally P waves are:

- **Left of each QRS complex.**
- **Precede each QRS complex.**
- **Look similar** in size, shape, and position.

Three questions to answer:

1. **Are P waves present?**
2. **Is there a P wave before every QRS?**
3. **Do they all look the same?**

---

## Topic 33: Step 4 — PR Interval

- From the **onset of the P wave** to the **onset of the QRS complex**.
- **Normal: 0.12 – 0.20 seconds.**

---

## Topic 34: Step 5 — QRS Duration

- First wave of the complex begins to deviate from baseline until the last wave levels out at, above, or below the baseline.
- **Narrow QRS = 0.11 seconds or less.**
- **Wide QRS = more than 0.11 seconds.**

---

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Mean QRS axis** = average direction of ventricular depolarization (summation of all wavefronts).
- **Hexaxial system** = 6 limb leads, 30° apart, used primarily to determine mean QRS axis.
- **Axis quadrants** — Normal 0° → +90° · LAD −1° → −90° · RAD +90° → ±180° · Extreme RAD −91° → −179°.
- **Quick look** — I↑ aVF↑ NORMAL · I↑ aVF↓ LAD · I↓ aVF↑ RAD · I↓ aVF↓ extreme RAD.
- **RAD causes** — emphysema · RVH · COPD · PE · LV MI · dextrocardia.
- **LAD causes** — high diaphragm · pregnancy · abdominal tumors · hyperkalemia · LVH · RV MI.
- **Coronary mapping** — II/III/aVF = RCA · I/aVL/V1–V6 = LCA.
- **ST elevation criteria** — ≥ 1 mm above isoelectric line at 0.04 s past J point, in 3+ consecutive leads.
- **STEMI evolution** — Hyperacute T (> 50 % of R) → ST↑ + tall upsloping T → ST↑ + T inversion → down-sloping ST↑ + T inversion + deep abnormal Q (tissue death).
- **NSTEMI** — no ST elevation; diagnose by S/Sx + history + cardiac biomarkers.
- **ST depression** — > 0.5 mm V2/V3, > 1 mm elsewhere; ± T wave inversion.
- **Walls** — Septal V1/V2 (LAD) · Anterior V3/V4 (LAD, ~40 % of heart) · Lateral I, aVL, V5/V6 (circumflex/LAD/RCA branch) · Inferior II/III/aVF (RCA — posterior descending; ↑ parasympathetic) · Posterior V7/V8/V9 (circumflex or RCA) · RV (RV marginal branch).
- **Anterior MI complications** — heart failure · ventricular dysfunction · cardiogenic shock.
- **RVI triad** — HYPOTENSION + JVD + CLEAR BREATH SOUNDS. Proximal RCA = inferior + RVI; isolated RV marginal = isolated RVI.
- **RAE** — tall initial P · biphasic in V1 · ↑ RA workload.
- **LAE** — widened latter P > 0.11 s · notched in I, II, aVL, V4–V6 · ↑ LA pressure / volume.
- **LVH** — deep S V1/V2 + tall R V5/V6 · S(V1) + R(V5) ≥ 35 mV · often paired with LAD · T inversion in lateral precordial leads.
- **RVH** — right axis deviation (earliest + most reliable) · reversed R progression — large in V1, smaller toward V6.
- **Sodium** — no significant ECG change.
- **Calcium** — ↑Ca → prolonged PR · ↓Ca → long flat ST + prolonged QT.
- **Potassium** — ↑K → wide QRS + prolonged PR · ↓K → ST depression.
- **5 steps** — RATE → RHYTHM → P WAVES → PR INTERVAL → QRS.
- **Intrinsic rates** — SA 60–100 · AV 40–60 · ventricular/Purkinje 20–40.
- **Rate methods** — 6-second × 10 (any rhythm) · 300 ÷ large boxes (regular) · 1500 ÷ small boxes (regular) · triplicate 300/150/100/75/60/50 (regular).
- **Irregular** — R-R variation > 4 small boxes (0.16 s).
- **PR normal** 0.12–0.20 s · **QRS narrow** ≤ 0.11 s · **QRS wide** > 0.11 s.
