> 1. **Ventricular rhythms — general characteristics** — the ventricles are the heart's LEAST EFFICIENT PACEMAKER. When an ectopic ventricular site assumes pacing, the impulse BYPASSES the normal intraventricular conduction pathway and stimulation of the ventricles occurs ASYNCHRONOUSLY. Ventricular beats and rhythms may originate from any part of the ventricles. Ventricles assume pacing IF: the SA NODE FAILS TO DISCHARGE; an impulse from the SA node IS BLOCKED; the SA node RATE OF DISCHARGE IS SLOWER THAN THE VENTRICLES; an IRRITABLE SITE IN THE VENTRICLES produces an early beat or rapid rhythm. ECG hallmarks: P WAVES ARE EITHER RETROGRADE OR MISSING; WIDE AND BIZARRE ventricular complexes measuring GREATER THAN 0.12 SECONDS.
> 2. **Premature Ventricular Complex (PVC)** — arises from an irritable site within either ventricle. A PREMATURE BEAT, occurring earlier than the next expected sinus beat. Occurs in an underlying rhythm; typically sinus. Can occur in HEALTHY PEOPLE WITH APPARENTLY NORMAL HEARTS and with NO APPARENT CAUSE. FREQUENCY OF PVCs INCREASES WITH AGE. Rate usually within normal range, depends on underlying rhythm. Rhythm essentially regular WITH PREMATURE BEATS; if the PVC is INTERPOLATED, the rhythm will be REGULAR. P waves usually absent OR with retrograde conduction may appear AFTER the QRS (usually upright in the ST segment or T wave). PR INTERVAL NONE with the PVC because the ectopic originates in the ventricles. QRS DURATION USUALLY 0.12 SECONDS OR GREATER, WIDE AND BIZARRE; T WAVE USUALLY IN OPPOSITE DIRECTION OF QRS.
> 3. **Types of PVCs** — UNIFORM (UNIFOCAL) PVCs: premature ventricular beats that LOOK THE SAME in the same lead and originate from the SAME ANATOMICAL SITE. MULTIFORM (MULTIFOCAL) PVCs: appear DIFFERENT FROM ONE ANOTHER in the same lead; sometimes arise from DIFFERENT ANATOMICAL SITES. INTERPOLATED PVCs: occur BETWEEN TWO NORMALLY CONDUCTED QRS COMPLEXES but DO NOT INTERFERE with the normal cardiac cycle.
> 4. **Patterns associated with PVCs** — PAIRS/COUPLET = 2 premature beats in a row. RUNS/BURSTS = 3 OR MORE premature beats in a row. BIGEMINY = every OTHER beat is a premature beat. TRIGEMINY = every THIRD beat is a premature beat. QUADRIGEMINY = every FOURTH beat is a premature beat. PVC causes: AGE, HYPOXIA, STRESS/ANXIETY, STIMULANTS, MYOCARDIAL ISCHEMIA.
> 5. **Ventricular Escape Beat** — UNLIKE PVCs, ventricular escape beats occur LATE — after the next expected sinus beat.
> 6. **Idioventricular Rhythm (IVR)** — exists when 3 or more ventricular escape beats occur in a row at a rate of 20–40 BPM. RATE 20–40 BPM. Rhythm essentially regular. P waves usually absent or, with retrograde conduction, may appear after the QRS (upright in ST or T). PR none. QRS usually ≥ 0.12 s, wide and bizarre; T wave usually opposite direction of QRS. CAUSES: supraventricular pacemaker either FAILS TO GENERATE, IS SLOWER than ventricular rate, or generates impulses that ARE BLOCKED — MI, DIGITALIS TOXICITY, ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES.
> 7. **Accelerated IVR (AIVR)** — exists when three or more ventricular beats occur in a row at a rate of 41–100 BPM. Considered a BENIGN ESCAPE RHYTHM that APPEARS WHEN THE SINUS RATE SLOWS and DISAPPEARS WHEN THE SINUS RATE SPEEDS UP. RATE 41–100 BPM. Rhythm essentially regular. P, PR, QRS as for IVR. CAUSES: ACUTE MYOCARDITIS, DIGITALIS TOXICITY, COCAINE TOXICITY, DILATED CARDIOMYOPATHY.
> 8. **Ventricular Tachycardia (V-Tach) — overview** — exists when 3 OR MORE PVCs occur in a row at a rate > 100 BPM. NON-SUSTAINED VT = a short run lasting LESS THAN 30 SECONDS. SUSTAINED VT = persists for MORE THAN 30 SECONDS.
> 9. **V-Tach criteria** — RATE 101–250 BPM. Rhythm essentially regular. P waves may be present or absent; if present, they have NO SET RELATIONSHIP to the QRS complexes, appearing between the QRSs at a rate DIFFERENT from that of the VT. PR none. QRS ≥ 0.12 s; gradual alteration in amplitude and direction; a typical cycle consists of 5–20 QRS complexes.
> 10. **V-Tach morphologies** — MONOMORPHIC VT: QRS complexes are of the SAME SHAPE AND AMPLITUDE. POLYMORPHIC VT: QRS complexes VARY in shape and amplitude. TORSADES DE POINTES: polymorphic VT that occurs in the presence of a LONG QT INTERVAL (greater than 0.45–0.50 seconds), gradual amplitude changes, and TWISTING in the isoelectric line. CAUSES: ACID-BASE IMBALANCE, ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES, CARDIOMYOPATHY, ELECTROLYTE IMBALANCE (hypokalemia, hyperkalemia, hypomagnesemia).
> 11. **Ventricular Fibrillation (V-Fib)** — CHAOTIC RHYTHM that originates in the ventricles with NO ORGANIZED DEPOLARIZATION. The ventricular myocardium QUIVERS, so there is NO EFFECTIVE MYOCARDIAL CONTRACTION and NO PULSE. The rhythm is IRREGULARLY IRREGULAR with chaotic deflections that VARY in shape and amplitude. NO NORMAL-LOOKING WAVEFORMS are visible. COARSE V-FIB = waves easily visible (3 OR MORE mm). FINE V-FIB = LOW AMPLITUDE waves (LESS THAN 3 mm). Rate cannot be determined. Rhythm rapid and chaotic with no pattern. P waves, PR interval, QRS NOT DISCERNIBLE. CAUSES: dysrhythmias (e.g., VT), ACUTE CORONARY SYNDROMES, VENTRICULAR HYPERTROPHY, ELECTROLYTE IMBALANCE, SEVERE HEART FAILURE.
> 12. **Treatments — ventricular rhythms** — CORRECT THE UNDERLYING CAUSE. CONSULT CARDIOLOGIST. SUPPLEMENTAL OXYGEN for symptoms. STABLE BUT SYMPTOMATIC V-TACH: administer ANTIARRHYTHMICS (e.g., AMIODARONE). PULSELESS V-TACH and V-FIB: CPR + DEFIBRILLATE.
> 13. **Asystole (cardiac standstill)** — TOTAL ABSENCE OF VENTRICULAR ELECTRICAL ACTIVITY. NO ventricular rate or rhythm, NO PULSE, NO CARDIAC OUTPUT. May occur TEMPORARILY following termination of a tachycardia with MEDICATIONS, DEFIBRILLATION, OR SYNCHRONIZED CARDIOVERSION. If atrial activity is present, the rhythm is called "P WAVE" ASYSTOLE. Ventricular rate not usually discernible but atrial activity may be seen. Rhythm: ventricular not discernible, atrial may be discernible. P waves not discernible. PR not discernible. QRS ABSENT. CAUSES: MI, PULMONARY EMBOLISM, TENSION PNEUMOTHORAX, CARDIAC TAMPONADE, SEVERE HYPOXIA.
> 14. **Treatment for asystole** — CPR. SUPPLEMENTAL OXYGEN. Administration of VASOPRESSOR (e.g., EPINEPHRINE, VASOPRESSIN). DO NOT DEFIBRILLATE.
> 15. **Heart blocks — Atrioventricular (AV) blocks** — dysrhythmia in which a DELAY OR INTERRUPTION in impulse conduction from the ATRIA to the VENTRICLES occurs because of a TRANSIENT or PERMANENT anatomic or functional impairment.
> 16. **Types of AV blocks** — FIRST DEGREE AV BLOCK. SECOND DEGREE AV BLOCK (incomplete blocks): TYPE I (Mobitz 1 or Wenckebach phenomenon); TYPE II (Mobitz II); 2:1. THIRD DEGREE AV BLOCK (complete block).
> 17. **Block Jingle** — IF THE R IS FAR FROM P, THEN IT MUST BE 1ST DEGREE. IF P-R GETS LONGER, THEN A QRS DROPS, THEN IT MUST BE WENCKEBACH (TYPE I). IF P-R STAYS NORMAL AND QRS QUITS, THEN IT MUST BE A MOBITZ II. IF P AND QRS BEAT INDEPENDENTLY, THEN IT MUST BE A COMPLETE THIRD DEGREE.
> 18. **First-degree AV block** — all components of the cardiac cycle, EXCEPT THE PR INTERVAL, are usually within normal limits. Despite its name, the sinus impulse is NOT BLOCKED but instead DELAYED for the same period before they are conducted to the ventricles, resulting in a PR INTERVAL > 0.20 SECONDS. ECG hallmark: CONSISTENT PR INTERVALS > 0.20 SECONDS. Rate usually within normal range, depends on underlying rhythm. Rhythm regular. P waves normal in size and shape, one positive (upright) P wave before each QRS in II, III, aVF. PR PROLONGED (> 0.20 s) BUT CONSTANT. QRS usually ≤ 0.11 s. CAUSES: can be BENIGN especially in athletes; ACUTE MI; ACUTE ENDOCARDITIS OR MYOCARDITIS.
> 19. **Second-degree AV block, Type I (Mobitz I / Wenckebach)** — impulse blockage/delay that occurs ABOVE THE BUNDLE OF HIS. Impulses generated by the SA node take LONGER AND LONGER to conduct through the AV node until finally a sinus impulse is BLOCKED — DROPPED BEAT. ECG hallmark: PROGRESSING PR INTERVALS, FOLLOWED BY DROPPED BEAT. Pattern repeats — WENCKEBACH PHENOMENON. Rate: ATRIAL FASTER than ventricular; ventricular often slow. Rhythm: ATRIAL REGULAR (P's plot through on time); VENTRICULAR IRREGULAR. P waves normal in size and shape; SOME P WAVES ARE NOT FOLLOWED BY A QRS (more P's than QRSs). PR LENGTHENS WITH EACH CYCLE until a P wave appears WITHOUT A QRS; PR after the non-conducted beat is SHORTER than the interval preceding it. QRS usually > 0.11 s, periodically absent after P waves. (Note: source says > 0.11 s; many sources show narrow QRS. Use the source value.) CAUSES: can be BENIGN especially in athletes; ACUTE MI; ACUTE ENDOCARDITIS OR MYOCARDITIS.
> 20. **Second-degree AV block, Type II (Mobitz II)** — conduction delay/blockage occurs BELOW THE AV NODE within the HIS-PURKINJE SYSTEM. SA node generates impulses normally, so each P wave occurs at a regular interval across the rhythm strip. HOWEVER, NOT EVERY P WAVE IS FOLLOWED BY A QRS — DROPPED BEAT. Impulses generated by the SA node are conducted to the ventricles at the same rate with CONSISTENT and usually NORMAL PR intervals UNTIL AN IMPULSE IS SUDDENLY BLOCKED. ECG hallmarks: CONSISTENT PR INTERVALS WITH DROPPED BEATS; QRS DURATION may be NARROW OR WIDE (> 0.11 seconds). Atrial rate faster than ventricular. Atrial regular; ventricular irregular. P waves normal; some not followed by QRS. PR within normal range or slightly prolonged but CONSTANT for conducted beats; SOME SHORTENING of the PR may follow a non-conducted P wave. QRS usually 0.10 s or less but PERIODICALLY DROPPED. CAUSES: LCA disease (LCA supplies blood to bundle branches), ACUTE MYOCARDITIS, CARDIOMYOPATHY, FIBROSIS OF CONDUCTION SYSTEM.
> 21. **2:1 AV block** — second-degree AV block. TWO CONDUCTED P WAVES MUST OCCUR CONSECUTIVELY (and they don't in 2:1). When TWO P WAVES OCCUR FOR EVERY ONE QRS, classification depends on the WIDTH OF THE QRS: TYPE I = NORMAL QRS within 0.11 SECONDS. TYPE II = WIDE QRS GREATER THAN 0.11 SECONDS. ECG hallmark: CONSISTENT PATTERN OF 2 P WAVES BEFORE A QRS. Atrial rate TWICE the ventricular rate. Atrial regular; ventricular regular. P waves normal in size and shape; every other P wave NOT followed by QRS. PR CONSTANT. QRS may be narrow or wide; complexes absent after every other P wave.
> 22. **Third-degree AV block (complete block)** — impulses generated by the SA node are BLOCKED BEFORE REACHING THE VENTRICLES, so NO P WAVES ARE CONDUCTED. Block may occur at the AV NODE, BUNDLE OF HIS, or BUNDLE BRANCHES. ATRIA AND VENTRICLES BEAT INDEPENDENTLY of each other. A SECONDARY PACEMAKER (either junctional or ventricular) stimulates the ventricles; therefore, the QRS may be NARROW OR WIDE depending on the location of the escape pacemaker and the condition of the intraventricular conduction system. ECG hallmarks: DROPPED BEATS WITH NO PATTERN IN PR INTERVAL; NO COMMUNICATION BETWEEN ATRIA AND VENTRICLES. Atrial rate FASTER than (and INDEPENDENT of) ventricular rate. Atrial regular; ventricular regular; NO RELATIONSHIP between atrial and ventricular rhythms. P waves normal in size and shape. PR NONE — atria and ventricles beat independently. QRS narrow or wide depending on escape origin. CAUSES: ACUTE MI, ACUTE MYOCARDITIS, side effects to drugs (AMIODARONE, BETA-BLOCKERS, DILTIAZEM), FIBROSIS OF THE CONDUCTION SYSTEM.
> 23. **Intraventricular conduction delay (BBB)** — disruption of impulse conduction from the bundle of His through either the RIGHT or LEFT BUNDLE BRANCH. QRS 0.10–0.12 SECONDS = INCOMPLETE branch block. QRS GREATER THAN 0.12 SECONDS = COMPLETE bundle branch block. ECG hallmark: rSR or "M" complexes — RABBIT EARS.
> 24. **Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)** — impulse travels through AV node and DOWN THE LEFT BUNDLE BRANCH into the interventricular septum. SEPTAL DEPOLARIZATION moves LEFT TO RIGHT, producing a SMALL INITIAL R WAVE in V1. As the left bundle continues to conduct, the entire LEFT VENTRICLE is depolarized RIGHT TO LEFT — signal goes AWAY FROM V1 — results in NEGATIVE S WAVE. Typically results in RIGHT AXIS DEVIATION. Produces an rSR PATTERN — RABBIT EARS. NORMAL R-WAVE PROGRESSION IS REVERSED in the chest leads.
> 25. **Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)** — septum is depolarized by the RIGHT BUNDLE BRANCH (as is the right ventricle). Causes NEGATIVE QRS COMPLEXES with SMALL R WAVES and DEEP S WAVES in leads V1 AND V2, and TALL R WAVES in leads V5 AND V6.
> 26. **R/LBBB shortcut — WiLLiaM MaRRoW** — a memorization trick using V1 and V6 morphology. WiLLiaM = LBBB: V1 looks like W, V6 looks like M. MaRRoW = RBBB: V1 looks like M, V6 looks like W. Read the FIRST AND LAST LETTERS of WiLLiaM and MaRRoW to recall V1 → V6.
> 27. **Paced rhythms** — in temporary or paced rhythms, PACING SPIKES will appear BEFORE the QRS complex. This is due to ELECTRICAL CAPTURE — the alteration of ECG rhythms and proper temporary pacing impulse delivery to successfully depolarize the atria and ventricles. ECG hallmark: PACING SPIKES.
>
> **High-yield distinctions to reinforce:**
> - Wide QRS (> 0.12 s) ± retrograde or absent P = ventricular origin.
> - Wide-complex rate ladder — IVR 20–40 · AIVR 41–100 · VT 101–250.
> - PVC types — uniform (same site, same shape) · multiform (different shapes) · interpolated (between normal beats, no disruption).
> - Premature-beat patterns — couplet 2 · run/burst ≥ 3 · bigeminy every other · trigeminy every 3rd · quadrigeminy every 4th.
> - VT flavors — monomorphic (same QRS) · polymorphic (varying QRS) · torsades (polymorphic + long QT > 0.45–0.50 s + twisting baseline).
> - V-fib coarse ≥ 3 mm vs fine < 3 mm. Always pulseless.
> - Asystole — total absence of ventricular electrical activity; CPR + epinephrine; NEVER DEFIBRILLATE.
> - Symptomatic stable V-tach → AMIODARONE. Pulseless V-tach / V-fib → CPR + DEFIBRILLATION.
> - 1st-degree AV block — PR > 0.20 s, constant; every P conducts. Often benign; can be MI / endocarditis / myocarditis.
> - Mobitz I (Wenckebach) — PR longer-longer-drop; block is ABOVE the bundle of His; pattern repeats; often benign.
> - Mobitz II — PR constant, sudden dropped beats; block is BELOW the AV node (His–Purkinje); LCA disease, myocarditis, fibrosis.
> - 2:1 AV block — every other P drops; classify by QRS width (narrow → Type I, wide → Type II).
> - Third-degree (complete) — atria and ventricles independent; PR meaningless; escape from junction (narrow QRS) or ventricle (wide QRS).
> - Block Jingle — far PR → 1° · longer-longer-drop → Wenckebach · stays normal then drops → Mobitz II · P and QRS independent → 3°.
> - BBB — incomplete 0.10–0.12 s · complete > 0.12 s · rSR rabbit ears in V1 (RBBB) or V6 (LBBB).
> - WiLLiaM (LBBB: W in V1, M in V6) · MaRRoW (RBBB: M in V1, W in V6).
> - Paced rhythm = pacing spikes immediately before captured complexes.

## Topic 1: Ventricular Rhythms — General Characteristics

The ventricles are the heart's **least efficient pacemaker**. When an ectopic site within a ventricle assumes responsibility for pacing, the impulse **bypasses the normal intraventricular conduction pathway** and stimulation of the ventricles occurs **asynchronously**. Ventricular beats and rhythms may originate from any part of the ventricles.

The ventricles assume responsibility for pacing the heart if:

- The SA node fails to discharge
- An impulse from the SA node is blocked
- The SA node rate of discharge is slower than the ventricles
- An irritable site in the ventricles produces an early beat or rapid rhythm

ECG hallmarks:

- P waves are either retrograde or missing
- Wide and bizarre ventricular complexes measuring **> 0.12 seconds**

---

## Topic 2: Premature Ventricular Complex (PVC)

A PVC arises from an irritable site within either ventricle. A "premature" beat, occurring earlier than the next expected sinus beat. Occurs in an underlying rhythm; typically sinus.

Can occur in healthy people with apparently normal hearts and with no apparent cause. Frequency of PVCs increases with age.

- **Rate** Usually within normal range; depends on underlying rhythm
- **Rhythm** Essentially regular with premature beats; if interpolated, the rhythm stays regular
- **P waves** Usually absent; with retrograde conduction, may appear after the QRS (upright in ST or T)
- **PR interval** None with the PVC (ectopic origin in the ventricles)
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.12 s or greater, wide and bizarre; T wave usually opposite direction of QRS

---

## Topic 3: Types of PVCs

- **Uniform / unifocal PVCs** — beats look the same in the same lead and originate from the same anatomical site.
- **Multiform / multifocal PVCs** — beats appear different from one another in the same lead. May arise from different anatomical sites.
- **Interpolated PVCs** — occur between two normally conducted QRS complexes but do not interfere with the normal cardiac cycle.

---

## Topic 4: Patterns Associated with PVCs

- **Pairs / couplet** — 2 premature beats in a row
- **Runs / bursts** — 3 or more premature beats in a row
- **Bigeminy** — every other beat is a premature beat
- **Trigeminy** — every third beat is a premature beat
- **Quadrigeminy** — every fourth beat is a premature beat

PVC causes:

- Age
- Hypoxia
- Stress / anxiety
- Stimulants
- Myocardial ischemia

---

## Topic 5: Ventricular Escape Beat

Unlike PVCs, ventricular escape beats occur **late** — after the next expected sinus beat.

---

## Topic 6: Idioventricular Rhythm (IVR)

Exists when 3 or more ventricular escape beats occur in a row at a rate of **20–40 bpm**.

- **Rate** 20–40 bpm
- **Rhythm** Essentially regular
- **P waves** Usually absent; with retrograde conduction, may appear after the QRS
- **PR interval** None
- **QRS duration** Usually ≥ 0.12 s, wide and bizarre; T wave opposite direction of QRS

Causes — supraventricular pacemaker fails, runs slower, or its impulses are blocked:

- MI
- Digitalis toxicity
- Acute coronary syndromes

---

## Topic 7: Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR)

Three or more ventricular beats in a row at a rate of **41–100 bpm**. Considered a benign escape rhythm that **appears when the sinus rate slows** and **disappears when the sinus rate speeds up**.

- **Rate** 41–100 bpm
- Other criteria identical to IVR

Causes:

- Acute myocarditis
- Digitalis toxicity
- Cocaine toxicity
- Dilated cardiomyopathy

---

## Topic 8: Ventricular Tachycardia (V-Tach) — Overview

Three or more PVCs in a row at a rate **> 100 bpm**.

- **Non-sustained VT** — short run lasting less than 30 seconds
- **Sustained VT** — persists for more than 30 seconds

---

## Topic 9: V-Tach — Criteria

- **Rate** 101–250 bpm
- **Rhythm** Essentially regular
- **P waves** May be present or absent; if present, no set relationship to the QRSs and appear between the QRSs at a different rate
- **PR interval** None
- **QRS duration** ≥ 0.12 s; gradual alteration in amplitude and direction; a typical cycle = 5–20 QRS complexes

---

## Topic 10: V-Tach Morphologies

- **Monomorphic VT** — QRS complexes are the same shape and amplitude.
- **Polymorphic VT** — QRS complexes vary in shape and amplitude.
- **Torsades de Pointes** — polymorphic VT in the presence of a long QT interval (> 0.45–0.50 s), gradual amplitude changes, and "twisting" around the isoelectric line.

Causes:

- Acid–base imbalance
- Acute coronary syndromes
- Cardiomyopathy
- Electrolyte imbalance — hypokalemia, hyperkalemia, hypomagnesemia

---

## Topic 11: Ventricular Fibrillation (V-Fib)

Chaotic rhythm originating in the ventricles with no organized depolarization. The ventricular myocardium quivers and there is no effective myocardial contraction and no pulse. The rhythm is irregularly irregular, with chaotic deflections that vary in shape and amplitude. No normal-looking waveforms are visible.

- **Coarse V-Fib** — waves easily visible (≥ 3 mm)
- **Fine V-Fib** — low amplitude (< 3 mm)

Criteria:

- **Rate** Cannot be determined
- **Rhythm** Rapid and chaotic
- **P waves**, **PR interval**, **QRS** — all not discernible

Causes:

- Other dysrhythmias (e.g., VT)
- Acute coronary syndromes
- Ventricular hypertrophy
- Electrolyte imbalance
- Severe heart failure

---

## Topic 12: Treatment — Ventricular Rhythms

- Correct the underlying cause
- Consult cardiologist
- Provide supplemental oxygen for symptoms
- **Stable but symptomatic V-Tach** → **antiarrhythmics (e.g., amiodarone)**
- **Pulseless V-Tach and V-Fib** → **CPR + defibrillation**

---

## Topic 13: Asystole (Cardiac Standstill)

Total absence of ventricular electrical activity. No ventricular rate or rhythm, pulse, or cardiac output. May occur temporarily following termination of a tachycardia with medications, defibrillation, or synchronized cardioversion. If atrial activity is present, the rhythm is called **"P wave" asystole**.

- **Rate** Ventricular rate not usually discernible; atrial activity may be seen
- **Rhythm** Ventricular not discernible; atrial may be discernible
- **P waves**, **PR interval** not discernible
- **QRS duration** Absent

Causes:

- MI
- Pulmonary embolism
- Tension pneumothorax
- Cardiac tamponade
- Severe hypoxia

---

## Topic 14: Treatment for Asystole

- CPR
- Supplemental oxygen
- Vasopressor (e.g., epinephrine, vasopressin)
- **Do NOT defibrillate**

---

## Topic 15: Heart Blocks — AV Blocks

A dysrhythmia in which a delay or interruption in impulse conduction from the atria to the ventricles occurs because of a transient or permanent anatomic or functional impairment.

---

## Topic 16: Types of AV Blocks

- First-degree AV block
- Second-degree AV block (incomplete)
  - Type I (Mobitz I / Wenckebach phenomenon)
  - Type II (Mobitz II)
  - 2:1
- Third-degree AV block (complete block)

---

## Topic 17: Block Jingle

> If the R is far from P, then it must be **1st degree**.
> If P-R gets longer then a QRS drops, then it must be a **Wenckebach (Type I)**.
> If P-R stays normal and QRS quits, then it must be a **Mobitz II**.
> If P and QRS beat independently, then it must be a complete **third degree**.

---

## Topic 18: First-Degree AV Block

All components of the cardiac cycle, except the PR interval, are usually within normal limits. Despite its name, the sinus impulse is not blocked but instead delayed for the same period before being conducted to the ventricles, resulting in a PR interval > 0.20 seconds.

ECG hallmark: consistent PR intervals **> 0.20 seconds**.

- **Rate** Usually within normal range
- **Rhythm** Regular
- **P waves** Normal in size and shape; one positive (upright) P wave before each QRS in II, III, aVF
- **PR interval** Prolonged (> 0.20 s) but constant
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.11 s or less

Causes:

- Can be benign (especially in athletes)
- Acute MI
- Acute endocarditis or myocarditis

---

## Topic 19: Second-Degree AV Block, Type I (Mobitz I / Wenckebach)

Impulse blockage/delay occurs above the bundle of His. Impulses generated by the SA node take longer and longer to conduct through the AV node until finally a sinus impulse is blocked ("dropped beat"). Pattern repeats — Wenckebach phenomenon.

ECG hallmark: progressively lengthening PR intervals followed by a dropped beat.

- **Rate** Atrial rate faster than ventricular; ventricular often slow
- **Rhythm** Atrial regular (P's plot through on time); ventricular irregular
- **P waves** Normal; some P waves are not followed by a QRS
- **PR interval** Lengthens with each cycle until a P wave appears without a QRS; PR after the non-conducted beat is shorter than the interval preceding it
- **QRS duration** Usually > 0.11 s, periodically absent after P waves

Causes:

- Can be benign (especially in athletes)
- Acute MI
- Acute endocarditis or myocarditis

---

## Topic 20: Second-Degree AV Block, Type II (Mobitz II)

Conduction delay/blockage occurs below the AV node (within the His–Purkinje system). The SA node generates impulses normally. However, not every P wave is followed by a QRS ("dropped" beat). Conducted impulses travel at the same rate with consistent, usually normal PR intervals until an impulse is suddenly blocked.

ECG hallmarks: consistent PR intervals with dropped beats. QRS duration may be narrow or wide (> 0.11 s).

- **Rate** Atrial rate faster than ventricular
- **Rhythm** Atrial regular; ventricular irregular
- **P waves** Normal; some not followed by a QRS
- **PR interval** Within normal range or slightly prolonged but constant for conducted beats; some shortening of the PR may follow a non-conducted P wave
- **QRS duration** Usually 0.10 s or less but periodically dropped

Causes:

- LCA disease (LCA supplies blood to bundle branches)
- Acute myocarditis
- Cardiomyopathy
- Fibrosis of the conduction system

---

## Topic 21: 2:1 AV Block

A second-degree AV block. Two consecutive conducted P waves never occur. When two P waves occur for every one QRS, classification depends on the width of the QRS:

- **Type I** — normal QRS within 0.11 seconds
- **Type II** — wide QRS > 0.11 seconds

ECG hallmark: consistent pattern of 2 P waves before each QRS.

- **Rate** Atrial rate twice the ventricular rate
- **Rhythm** Atrial regular; ventricular regular
- **P waves** Normal; every other P wave not followed by a QRS
- **PR interval** Constant
- **QRS duration** May be narrow or wide

---

## Topic 22: Third-Degree AV Block (Complete Block)

Impulses generated by the SA node are blocked before reaching the ventricles, so no P waves are conducted. Block may occur at the AV node, bundle of His, or bundle branches.

Atria and ventricles beat independently of each other. A secondary pacemaker (junctional or ventricular) stimulates the ventricles; therefore, the QRS may be narrow or wide depending on the location of the escape pacemaker and the condition of the intraventricular conduction system.

ECG hallmarks:

- "Dropped" beats with no pattern in PR interval
- No communication between atria and ventricles

- **Rate** Atrial rate faster than (and independent of) the ventricular rate; ventricular rate determined by the origin of the escape rhythm
- **Rhythm** Atrial regular; ventricular regular; no relationship between the atrial and ventricular rhythms
- **P waves** Normal in size and shape
- **PR interval** None — atria and ventricles beat independently
- **QRS duration** Narrow or wide depending on the escape pacemaker

Causes:

- Acute MI
- Acute myocarditis
- Side effects of drugs (amiodarone, beta-blockers, diltiazem)
- Fibrosis of the conduction system

---

## Topic 23: Intraventricular Conduction Delay (Bundle Branch Block)

A disruption of the impulse conduction from the bundle of His through either the right or left bundle branch.

- **Incomplete BBB** — QRS measures **0.10 – 0.12 s**
- **Complete BBB** — QRS measures **> 0.12 s**

ECG hallmark: rSR or "M" complexes (rabbit ears).

---

## Topic 24: Right Bundle Branch Block (RBBB)

- Impulse travels through the AV node and down the **left bundle branch** into the interventricular septum.
- Septal depolarization moves left to right → produces a small initial R wave in V1.
- As the left bundle continues to conduct, the entire left ventricle is depolarized right to left → signal goes away from V1 → negative S wave.
- Typically results in **right axis deviation**.
- Produces an rSR pattern — "rabbit ears."
- Normal R-wave progression is reversed in the chest leads.

---

## Topic 25: Left Bundle Branch Block (LBBB)

- The septum is depolarized by the **right bundle branch** (as is the right ventricle).
- Causes negative QRS complexes with **small R waves and deep S waves in V1 and V2** and **tall R waves in V5 and V6**.

---

## Topic 26: R/LBBB Shortcut — WiLLiaM MaRRoW

A memorization trick using V1 and V6 morphology:

- **WiLLiaM** = LBBB → V1 looks like **W**, V6 looks like **M**.
- **MaRRoW** = RBBB → V1 looks like **M**, V6 looks like **W**.

Read the first and last letters of each word to recall V1 → V6.

---

## Topic 27: Paced Rhythms

In temporary or paced rhythms, **pacing spikes** appear before the QRS complex. This is due to **electrical capture** — the alteration of ECG rhythms and proper temporary pacing impulse delivery to successfully depolarize the atria and ventricles.

ECG hallmark: pacing spikes.

---

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Ventricular origin** = wide QRS > 0.12 s + retrograde or absent P waves.
- **Wide-complex rate ladder** — IVR 20–40 · AIVR 41–100 · VT 101–250.
- **PVC types** — uniform / multiform / interpolated.
- **Premature patterns** — couplet 2 · run/burst ≥ 3 · bigeminy every other · trigeminy every 3rd · quadrigeminy every 4th.
- **VT flavors** — monomorphic · polymorphic · torsades de pointes (long QT > 0.45–0.50 s, twisting baseline).
- **V-fib** — coarse ≥ 3 mm vs fine < 3 mm; pulseless; CPR + defib.
- **Asystole** — total absence of ventricular activity; CPR + epinephrine; NEVER DEFIBRILLATE.
- **Treatment shortcuts** — symptomatic stable V-tach → amiodarone · pulseless V-tach / V-fib → CPR + defib · asystole → CPR + vasopressors.
- **1st-degree AV block** — PR > 0.20 s, constant; every P conducts.
- **Mobitz I (Wenckebach)** — PR longer-longer-drop; block above bundle of His; pattern repeats.
- **Mobitz II** — PR constant, sudden dropped beats; block below AV node (His–Purkinje).
- **2:1 AV block** — every other P drops; classify by QRS width (narrow → Type I, wide → Type II).
- **3rd-degree (complete)** — atria and ventricles independent; PR meaningless; escape rhythm sets the ventricular rate.
- **Block Jingle** — far PR → 1° · longer-longer-drop → Wenckebach · stays normal then drops → Mobitz II · P and QRS independent → 3°.
- **BBB** — incomplete 0.10–0.12 s · complete > 0.12 s · rSR / "rabbit ears."
- **RBBB** — rSR pattern in V1, normal R progression reversed, right axis deviation.
- **LBBB** — small R + deep S in V1/V2, tall R in V5/V6.
- **WiLLiaM MaRRoW** — W in V1 + M in V6 = LBBB · M in V1 + W in V6 = RBBB.
- **Paced rhythm** — pacing spikes before each captured complex confirm electrical capture.
