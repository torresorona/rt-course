# Abnormal Chest X-ray & Advanced Imaging Interpretation — NotebookLM Source

## Unit 1: COPD on Chest X-ray

### Patient Assessment
- Barrel chest (increased A-P diameter), clubbing, cyanosis
- Dyspnea, accessory muscle use, pursed-lip breathing
- Diminished aeration with bilateral expiratory wheeze
- Tympanic or hyperresonant percussion note
- Congested cough productive of thick sputum

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Hyperlucent** — lungs appear abnormally dark due to air trapping
- **Hyperinflated** — lungs overexpanded beyond normal volume
- **Diaphragms lowered and flattened** — chronic hyperinflation pushes diaphragms downward
- **Increased space between ribs** — intercostal spaces widened
- **Increased retrosternal airspace** — on lateral view, space behind sternum enlarged

## Unit 2: Pneumonia

### Patient Assessment
- Diaphoretic, cyanotic, tachypnea
- Crackles, bronchial breath sounds, whispered pectoriloquy
- Flat or dull percussion over affected area
- Productive cough (yellow/green or rust-colored sputum)
- Febrile, tachycardia, hypertension

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Consolidation** — radiopaque area where air is replaced by fluid/pus
- **Air bronchogram** — air-filled bronchi visible as dark lines against opaque lung

## Unit 3: Atelectasis

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Increased radiopacity** — collapsed segment appears whiter
- **Increased air bronchograms** — crowded bronchial markings
- **Displacement of fissures toward collapsed lung** — key sign differentiating from consolidation

## Unit 4: Pleural Effusion

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Blunting of costophrenic angles** — fluid in most dependent pleural region
- Fluid layers on lateral decubitus view
- Can be left or right lateral depending on affected side

## Unit 5: Pneumothorax & Tension Pneumothorax

### Patient Assessment
- Diaphoresis, cyanosis, tracheal/mediastinal shift away from affected side
- Tachypnea, reduced movement, diminished/absent breath sounds
- Hyperresonant/tympanic percussion, tachycardia, pulsus paradoxus
- Tension pneumothorax: tracheal deviation away, mediastinal shift, flattened diaphragm, cardiovascular compromise

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Visceral pleural line** — thin white line of pleura separated from chest wall
- **Vascular markings absent** — no lung markings peripheral to pleural line
- **Increased lucency** — affected side appears abnormally dark

## Unit 6: Hemothorax

### Patient Assessment
- Associated with trauma
- Similar to pleural effusion but with blood in pleural space
- May cause hypovolemic shock

### Key Radiographic Terms
- Same as pleural effusion (opacification, blunted costophrenic angle)
- Homogeneous opacity that may completely opacify hemithorax

## Unit 7: Left Heart Failure / Pulmonary Edema

### Patient Assessment
- Peripheral/pedal edema, diaphoresis, cyanosis
- Tachypnea, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
- Crackles, wheezing, pink frothy secretions
- Flat/dull percussion, increased tactile and vocal fremitus

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Mild:** Increased vascular markings, cephalization (upper lobe vessels more prominent)
- **Moderate:** Batwing/butterfly perihilar infiltrates sparing periphery
- **Severe:** Kerley B lines (thin horizontal lines at bases), diffuse alveolar opacification

## Unit 8: Pulmonary Embolism

### Patient Assessment
- Anxious, diaphoretic, cyanotic, cool/clammy skin
- Sudden-onset dyspnea, tachypnea
- Wheezing, crackles, pleural friction rub, hemoptysis
- CXR often normal — does NOT rule out PE

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Wedge-shaped infiltrate** (Hampton hump) — triangular opacity with base against pleura
- **Plate-like or discoid atelectasis** — linear atelectasis due to surfactant loss

## Unit 9: ARDS

### Patient Assessment
- Cyanotic, tachypnea, substernal/intercostal retractions
- Bronchial breath sounds, crackles
- Flat/dull percussion, tachycardia, hypertension

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Bilateral diffuse infiltrates** — all lung zones on both sides
- **Poorly defined opacities** — no clear borders
- **Fluffy/patchy appearance** — irregular cloud-like densities
- **Ground glass appearance** — hazy opacity not obscuring bronchovascular markings

## Unit 10: Epiglottitis

### Patient Assessment
- Sudden onset within 6-8 hours
- Pale/cyanotic, drooling, hoarseness, inspiratory stridor
- Dysphagia, tongue thrusts forward, muffled voice/cry, jaw jutted forward
- Tachypnea, diminished breath sounds

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Thumb sign** — swollen rounded epiglottis on lateral neck

## Unit 11: Croup (Laryngotracheobronchitis)

### Patient Assessment
- Gradual onset over 2-3 days following a cold
- More common in fall and winter
- Barking cough, hoarse voice, accessory muscle use, cyanosis
- Tachypnea, diminished breath sounds, inspiratory stridor

### Key Radiographic Terms
- **Steeple sign** — subglottic narrowing producing tapered appearance on lateral neck

## Unit 12: Foreign Body Aspiration

### Patient Assessment
- Cough, wheeze, stridor, dyspnea, cyanosis
- Children 1-3 years at highest risk
- Twice as common in males
- Right and left main bronchi similar angle until age 15
- Most common: food (especially nuts), teeth, dental devices

### Key Points
- Normal CXR does NOT exclude foreign body
- Treatment is bronchoscopic removal

## Unit 13: Flail Chest

### Patient Assessment
- Anxious, cyanosis, bruising over involved area
- Shallow rapid respirations, paradoxical chest movement
- Diminished breath sounds, possible pneumothorax signs

### Key Radiographic Terms
- Multiple rib fractures (2+ fractures in 2+ contiguous ribs)
- Often associated with pulmonary contusion

## Unit 14: Computed Tomography (CT)

### Principles
- Cross-sectional X-ray images
- Far greater detail than plain radiography

### Key Diagnoses
- **Bronchiectasis** — tram track / signet ring sign
- **Pneumonia** — better characterization of extent and cavitation
- **Pulmonary nodules** — high-resolution detection
- **Pulmonary embolism** — CTPA gold standard
- **Interstitial pulmonary fibrosis** — honeycombing, traction bronchiectasis

## Unit 15: Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)

### Principles
- Strong magnetic fields + radio waves (no ionizing radiation)
- Two- and three-dimensional views

### Key Diagnoses
- Thoracic aneurysms, congenital vascular anomalies, tumor positioning

### RT Safety Considerations
- Claustrophobic patients may not tolerate
- Obese patients may not fit
- Fluidic (gas-powered) ventilators required
- Non-ferrous resuscitation equipment (detachable non-rebreathing valves)
- Aluminum gas cylinders instead of steel

## Unit 16: Ultrasound

### Principles
- High-frequency sound waves detect reflected echoes

### Limitations
- Poor for lung parenchyma (air blocks sound waves)

### RT Applications
- Rapid heart function and volume assessment
- Guidance for central line and arterial line placement
- Pleural effusion detection and thoracentesis guidance

## Unit 17: V/Q Scan

### Indications
- Pulmonary embolism (especially when CTPA contraindicated)
- Pre-op COPD evaluation

### Ventilation Scan
- Inhaled radioisotope (xenon gas) maps airflow distribution
- Obstruction prevents gas from filling area

### Perfusion Scan
- IV radioactive albumin lodges in pulmonary capillaries
- Maps distribution of blood flow

### Interpretation
- Normal ventilation + abnormal perfusion = pulmonary embolism

## Unit 18: PET Scan

### Principles
- Nuclear medicine imaging using radioactive glucose tracer
- Tracer distributes throughout body, metabolically active areas "light up"

### Indications
- Cancer detection and treatment monitoring
- Brain disorders, heart disease

### Note
- Often combined with CT (PET/CT) for precise anatomical localization
