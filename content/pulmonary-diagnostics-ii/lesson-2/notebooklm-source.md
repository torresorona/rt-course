>
> 1. **The hydrolysis equation and why it matters** — the master reaction is CO2 + H2O reversibly equals H2CO3 reversibly equals H+ plus HCO3-. Push the reaction RIGHT and the body generates more hydrogen ion (more acid). Push it LEFT (blow off CO2) and the body becomes less acidic. This single equation links the LUNGS to the BLOOD chemistry.
>
> 2. **Hydrogen ion and pH definition** — free [H+] in blood must be maintained within VERY NARROW LIMITS to maintain life. pH is the customary way to express [H+] — defined as the NEGATIVE LOG of the free [H+]. Normal arterial pH = 7.35 to 7.45. The relationship between pH and [H+] is INVERSE: increase pH = decrease [H+]; decrease pH = increase [H+]. Other equivalent descriptions: "inversely proportional to [H+]" and "log of the reciprocal of [H+]."
>
> 3. **Acid vs. base behavior** — an ACID DONATES or releases H+ and corresponds to pH less than 7.35. A BASE ACCEPTS H+ and corresponds to pH greater than 7.45.
>
> 4. **pH homeostasis — the three regulators** — homeostasis is maintenance of a constant internal environment. Blood pH is maintained through THREE systems: the LUNGS, the KIDNEYS, and the BLOOD BUFFERS. The LUNGS and KIDNEYS precisely maintain levels of acids and bases. The BLOOD BUFFERS serve a primarily PROTECTIVE role — they prevent LARGE CHANGES in pH when abnormal conditions expose blood to acid–base abnormalities.
>
> 5. **The lungs and CO2 — production vs. elimination** — arterial PCO2 at any moment depends on the QUANTITY OF CO2 ENTERING blood from tissues (production) and the QUANTITY OF CO2 LEAVING via the lungs (elimination). The METABOLIC RATE determines the QUANTITY OF CO2 PRODUCED. LUNG FUNCTION (alveolar ventilation, gas exchange — actually breathing in and out) determines the QUANTITY OF CO2 ELIMINATED.
>
> 6. **CO2 production and the type of foodstuff** — production depends on BOTH THE QUANTITY AND THE NATURE of metabolism. Nature of metabolism depends on the type of foodstuff being burned (carbohydrate, lipid/fat, protein). CARBOHYDRATE METABOLISM PRODUCES MORE CO2 THAN FAT METABOLISM. This is why nutrition matters for ventilator weaning.
>
> 7. **Review of general chemistry — metabolism** — METABOLISM is the SUM OF ALL CHEMICAL PROCESSES that occur in the body. It supports GROWTH, ENERGY PRODUCTION, ELIMINATION OF WASTE, and impacts the DISTRIBUTION OF NUTRIENTS in blood after digestion.
>
> 8. **The mitochondria and ATP** — inside each cell is the MITOCHONDRIA — the cell's "POWERHOUSE" — where the KREBS CYCLE generates energy from nutrients. CELLULAR RESPIRATION converts the energy of carbohydrates, proteins, and lipids into ATP. The relationship between NUTRITION and RESPIRATORY STATUS is RECIPROCAL throughout the human life cycle. A fully functioning pulmonary system supplies the OXYGEN needed for cellular metabolism of the three macronutrients: CARBOHYDRATES (CHO), PROTEINS, and LIPIDS.
>
> 9. **Three sources of energy** — ENERGY METABOLISM is the process of generating ATP from nutrients. CARBOHYDRATE = SHORT-TERM energy, the body's PREFERRED energy source, stored in LIVER AND MUSCLE as GLYCOGEN or as FAT. LIPID/FAT = LONG-TERM energy (e.g., brisk walking), MORE ENERGY PER GRAM = more efficient storage; when carbohydrate metabolism is deficient more fat is used and weight loss may result. PROTEIN = GROWTH AND REPAIR.
>
> 10. **Macronutrient O2 to CO2 ratios** — all three macronutrients produce CO2 when broken down, just at different rates. CARBOHYDRATE: 1 mole O2 consumed yields 1 mole CO2 produced — RATIO 1 to 1.0. LIPID/FAT: 1 mole O2 consumed yields 0.7 mole CO2 produced — RATIO 1 to 0.7. PROTEIN: 1 mole O2 consumed yields 0.8 mole CO2 produced — RATIO 1 to 0.8.
>
> 11. **Respiratory quotient (RQ)** — RQ is the ratio of CO2 production (V-dot CO2) to O2 consumption (V-dot O2). Formula: RQ = V-dot CO2 / V-dot O2. RQ greater than 1.0 suggests EXCESSIVE CARBOHYDRATE OR CALORIE PROVISION which can result in INCREASED CO2 PRODUCTION and cause DIFFICULTY WEANING FROM MECHANICAL VENTILATION. RQ less than 0.7 suggests UNDERFEEDING and use of KETONES as a fuel source.
>
> 12. **Pulmonary disorders affected by nutrition** — ASTHMA, CYSTIC FIBROSIS, COPD, EMPHYSEMA, ARDS. Nutritional requirements and status of patients with pulmonary disease are MAJOR FACTORS that influence acute and long-term outcomes.
>
> 13. **CO2 elimination — when ventilation can't keep up** — sometimes increases in CO2 production CANNOT BE OFFSET by increased ventilation. This may occur when CO2 production is HIGH (BURNS, TOTAL PARENTERAL NUTRITION, SEPSIS) or when the VENTILATORY SYSTEM IS COMPROMISED.
>
> 14. **CO2 excretion — normal response** — a normal respiratory system responds to increased CO2 with a PARALLEL RISE in alveolar ventilation and CO2 excretion. MOST CLINICAL CHANGES IN PaCO2 ARE A RESULT OF CHANGES IN ALVEOLAR VENTILATION. INCREASE in alveolar ventilation = DECREASED PaCO2. DECREASE in alveolar ventilation = INCREASED PaCO2.
>
> 15. **Four basic forms of CO2 transport in blood** — DISSOLVED CO2; CARBONIC ACID (H2CO3); BICARBONATE (HCO3-); CARBAMINO COMPOUNDS. Normal arterial PCO2 = 40 mmHg.
>
> 16. **Carbonic acid and bicarbonate** — concentration of carbonic acid [H2CO3] varies DIRECTLY with the quantity of dissolved CO2 because they are linked through the hydrolysis reaction. Carbonic acid is in equilibrium with both dissolved CO2 and bicarbonate. Some CO2 entering the blood ultimately forms HCO3- (small amount in this direct conversion). HCO3- IS THE MAJOR MECHANISM OF CO2 TRANSPORT and accounts for APPROXIMATELY 80% of CO2 transport from tissues to lungs.
>
> 17. **Blood buffers — what they do** — buffers serve a PROTECTIVE role, preventing large changes in pH when abnormal conditions expose blood to acid–base abnormalities. They allow LARGE H+ CHANGES with RELATIVELY LITTLE CHANGE in free [H+]; LESSEN the change in [H+]; PREVENT EXTREME CHANGES; ACCEPT or DONATE H+ depending on whether there is acid or base excess; CONVERT STRONG acids and bases into WEAK acids and bases; MAINTAIN A NARROW RANGE of free [H+] required to sustain life.
>
> 18. **The two major buffer systems** — HEMOGLOBIN INSIDE THE RBC and the KIDNEYS. Red blood cells (erythrocytes) house the MAJOR BUFFER (Hb in RBCs). Reversible binding of [H+] occurs with EVERY RBC. Other buffers mentioned: BICARBONATE (HCO3-), PHOSPHATE (HPO4), AMMONIA (NH3-).
>
> 19. **Gas exchange and transport — oxygen** — 98.5% of O2 is BOUND TO HEMOGLOBIN (the HALDANE EFFECT governs this carrying capacity). 1.5% is dissolved in plasma.
>
> 20. **Gas exchange and transport — carbon dioxide** — 23% of CO2 is BOUND TO HEMOGLOBIN (carbamino) — the BOHR EFFECT governs the related O2 affinity changes. 7% is DISSOLVED IN PLASMA. 70% binds with WATER to form HCO3-.
>
> 21. **Factors affecting O2 loading and unloading** — three big factors beyond curve shape: BLOOD pH, BODY TEMPERATURE, and ERYTHROCYTE 2,3-DPG concentration.
>
> 22. **pH and the HbO2 curve** — LOW pH (acidic) shifts the curve to the RIGHT — Hb saturation for a given PO2 DECREASES, meaning Hb AFFINITY FOR O2 DECREASES. HIGH pH (alkalotic) shifts the curve to the LEFT — Hb saturation for a given PO2 INCREASES, meaning Hb AFFINITY FOR O2 INCREASES.
>
> 23. **Body temperature and the HbO2 curve** — DECREASE in body temperature shifts the curve to the LEFT, INCREASING Hb's affinity for O2. INCREASE in body temperature shifts the curve to the RIGHT, DECREASING Hb's affinity for O2.
>
> 24. **2,3-DPG and the HbO2 curve** — 2,3-DPG is the organic phosphate 2,3-diphosphoglycerate, abundant in RBCs, where it forms a LOOSE CHEMICAL BOND with the GLOBIN CHAINS of DEOXYGENATED Hb. HIGH 2,3-DPG concentrations shift the HbO2 curve to the RIGHT (promoting O2 UNLOADING) — DECREASING Hb affinity for O2. LOW 2,3-DPG concentrations shift the HbO2 curve to the LEFT — INCREASING Hb affinity for O2.
>
> 25. **Bohr vs. Haldane effects** — BOHR EFFECT describes how CO2/H+/temperature change Hb's AFFINITY FOR O2. HALDANE EFFECT describes how O2 BINDING affects Hb's ability to CARRY CO2.
>
> 26. **Acid–base definitions — patient vs. blood vocabulary** — ACIDOSIS = abnormal PHYSIOLOGIC condition (the patient) where strong acid is gained or base is lost — "acidotic" — DECREASED pH. ALKALOSIS = abnormal physiologic condition where strong base is gained or acid is lost — "alkalotic" — INCREASED pH. ACIDEMIA = abnormal state of the BLOOD, pH below normal. ALKALEMIA = abnormal state of the BLOOD, pH above normal.
>
> 27. **ROME mnemonic** — RESPIRATORY = OPPOSITE: PaCO2 has an INVERSE effect on pH. Increase CO2 = decrease pH = ACIDOSIS. Decrease CO2 = increase pH = ALKALOSIS. METABOLIC = EQUAL: HCO3- has a DIRECT effect on pH. Increase HCO3- = increase pH = ALKALOSIS. Decrease HCO3- = decrease pH = ACIDOSIS.
>
> 28. **Blood gas components** — pH is BOTH metabolic AND respiratory. PaCO2 = RESPIRATORY component, INVERSELY related to pH (increase PaCO2 = decrease pH; decrease PaCO2 = increase pH). HCO3- = METABOLIC component, LINEARLY related to pH (increase HCO3- = increase pH; decrease HCO3- = decrease pH). BE/BD (Base Excess / Base Deficit) = METABOLIC, also linearly related to pH.
>
> 29. **Normal ABG values** — pH 7.35 to 7.45. PaCO2 35 to 45 mmHg. HCO3- 22 to 26 mEq/L.
>
> 30. **Seven-step ABG interpretation** — STEP 1 categorize pH: acidotic if less than 7.4, alkalotic if greater than 7.4. STEP 2 evaluate CO2 (35–45 normal). STEP 3 evaluate HCO3- (22–26 normal). STEP 4 what is causing the abnormal pH — apply ROME. STEP 5 is there compensation? (none, partial, or fully compensated — meaning is the OTHER ORGAN helping out?). STEP 6 assess for HYPOXEMIA — abnormally low O2 in the BLOOD. STEP 7 assess for HYPOXIA — deficient O2 reaching the TISSUES (global or local).
>
> 31. **Acute / uncompensated respiratory acidosis** — caused by HYPOVENTILATION (decreased alveolar ventilation relative to CO2 production). Etiologies: COPD, NEUROMUSCULAR DISORDER, DRUG/SEDATION OVERDOSE, ROSC.
>
> 32. **Acute / uncompensated respiratory alkalosis** — caused by HYPERVENTILATION (increased alveolar ventilation relative to CO2 production). Etiologies: ACUTE ASTHMA, PULMONARY EMBOLISM, FEVER, PREGNANCY, HYPOXIA.
>
> 33. **Acute / uncompensated metabolic acidosis** — etiologies: KETOACIDOSIS, LACTIC ACIDOSIS, RENAL FAILURE, DIARRHEA, SALICYLATE (ASPIRIN) POISONING.
>
> 34. **Acute / uncompensated metabolic alkalosis** — etiologies: FLUID LOSS FROM THE GI TRACT, SEVERE VOMITING, N-G TUBE COMPLICATIONS, DIURETICS, SEVERE POTASSIUM DEPLETION, EXCESS ADMINISTRATION OF HCO3-.
>

## Topic 1: The Hydrolysis Equation

**CO₂ + H₂O ⇌ H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻**

This single reversible reaction connects CO₂ (lung-controlled) to free hydrogen ion (acid). Push it right → more H⁺. Push it left (blow off CO₂) → less H⁺.

---

## Topic 2: Hydrogen Ion and pH

- Free [H⁺] in the blood must be maintained within **very narrow limits** — alterations have **profound, life-threatening effects**.
- **pH = the negative log of free [H⁺].**
- **Normal arterial pH = 7.35 – 7.45.**
- **Inverse relationship:**
  - ↑ pH → ↓ [H⁺]
  - ↓ pH → ↑ [H⁺]
- pH descriptions: "inversely proportional to [H⁺]" · "negative log of [H⁺]" · "log of the reciprocal of [H⁺]."

---

## Topic 3: Acid vs. Base

- **Acid** — donates / releases H⁺ — **pH < 7.35**.
- **Base** — accepts H⁺ — **pH > 7.45**.

---

## Topic 4: pH Homeostasis — Three Regulators

Maintenance of a constant internal environment is **homeostasis**. Blood pH is regulated by:

- **Lungs** — precise control of acids/bases (rapid).
- **Kidneys** — precise control of acids/bases (slow).
- **Blood buffers** — primarily **protective**; prevent **large changes in pH**.

---

## Topic 5: The Lungs and CO₂

Arterial PCO₂ depends on:

- **CO₂ entering** the blood (driven by **metabolic rate**).
- **CO₂ leaving** the blood (driven by **alveolar ventilation / lung function**).

**Carbohydrate metabolism produces MORE CO₂ than fat metabolism.**

---

## Topic 6: Review of General Chemistry — Metabolism

- **Metabolism** = sum of all chemical processes; supports growth, energy production, waste elimination, and nutrient distribution.
- **Mitochondria** = cell's powerhouse; **Krebs cycle** generates energy.
- **Cellular respiration** = converts carbs/proteins/lipids → **ATP**.
- Nutrition and respiratory status are **reciprocal**.
- Pulmonary system supplies O₂ for metabolism of the three macronutrients: **carbs (CHO), proteins, lipids**.

---

## Topic 7: Three Sources of Energy

| # | Source | Role | Storage |
|---|--------|------|---------|
| 1 | **Carbohydrate** | Short-term, **preferred** | Liver and muscle as **glycogen**, or as fat |
| 2 | **Lipid / Fat** | Long-term (brisk walking); more energy/g — efficient storage. Used more when carb metabolism is deficient → may cause weight loss | Adipose |
| 3 | **Protein** | Growth and repair | — |

### O₂ : CO₂ Ratios

| Macronutrient | O₂ : CO₂ |
|---------------|----------|
| Carbohydrate | **1 : 1.0** |
| Lipid / Fat | **1 : 0.7** |
| Protein | **1 : 0.8** |

---

## Topic 8: Respiratory Quotient (RQ)

- **RQ = V̇CO₂ / V̇O₂** (CO₂ produced ÷ O₂ consumed).
- **RQ > 1.0** → excessive carbohydrate or calorie provision → ↑ CO₂ → **difficulty weaning from mechanical ventilation**.
- **RQ < 0.7** → underfeeding / use of **ketones** as fuel.

### Pulmonary Disorders Affected by Nutrition

- Asthma
- Cystic fibrosis
- COPD
- Emphysema
- ARDS

Nutritional status is a **major factor** in acute and long-term outcomes for these patients.

---

## Topic 9: CO₂ Elimination

- A normal respiratory system responds to ↑CO₂ with a **parallel rise in alveolar ventilation**.
- **Most clinical PaCO₂ changes = alveolar ventilation changes.**
  - ↑ alveolar ventilation → ↓ PaCO₂
  - ↓ alveolar ventilation → ↑ PaCO₂
- Increased CO₂ production sometimes can NOT be offset (high CO₂ states: **burns, TPN, sepsis**, or compromised ventilatory system).

---

## Topic 10: CO₂ Transport — Four Forms

1. **Dissolved CO₂**
2. **Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃)**
3. **Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻)** — **major form (~80%)** of CO₂ transport from tissues to lungs
4. **Carbamino compounds**

**Normal arterial PCO₂ = 40 mmHg.**

[H₂CO₃] varies **directly** with dissolved CO₂ (linked via hydrolysis).

---

## Topic 11: Blood Buffers

**Role:** protective — prevent large changes in pH.

Buffers:
- Allow large H⁺ changes with little change in free [H⁺]
- Lessen and prevent extreme changes in [H⁺]
- Accept or donate H⁺ depending on excess
- Convert **strong** acids/bases into **weak** acids/bases
- Maintain narrow [H⁺] range required for life

### Two Major Buffer Systems

- **Hemoglobin inside RBCs** (the **major** buffer; reversible H⁺ binding occurs in every RBC)
- **Kidneys**

Other buffers: **bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻), phosphate (HPO₄), ammonia (NH₃⁻)**.

---

## Topic 12: Gas Exchange & Transport — O₂

| Form | % |
|------|----|
| Bound to hemoglobin | **98.5%** |
| Dissolved in plasma | **1.5%** |

**Haldane Effect** governs O₂ carrying capacity of Hb.

---

## Topic 13: Gas Exchange & Transport — CO₂

| Form | % |
|------|----|
| Bound to Hb (carbamino) | **23%** |
| Dissolved in plasma | **7%** |
| Bound with water as HCO₃⁻ | **70%** |

**Bohr Effect** governs Hb's affinity for O₂ when CO₂/H⁺ change.

---

## Topic 14: Factors Affecting O₂ Loading/Unloading on Hb

Three key factors:

1. Blood pH
2. Body temperature
3. RBC concentration of **2,3-DPG**

### pH

| pH | Curve | Affinity |
|----|-------|----------|
| **Low (acidic)** | RIGHT | ↓ (releases O₂) |
| **High (alkalotic)** | LEFT | ↑ (holds O₂) |

### Body Temperature

| Temp | Curve | Affinity |
|------|-------|----------|
| **↓ Temp** | LEFT | ↑ |
| **↑ Temp** | RIGHT | ↓ |

### 2,3-DPG

- Found abundantly in RBCs; binds the **globin chains of deoxygenated Hb**.
- **High 2,3-DPG** → curve RIGHT → ↓ Hb affinity (promotes **O₂ unloading**).
- **Low 2,3-DPG** → curve LEFT → ↑ Hb affinity.

### Bohr vs. Haldane

- **Bohr Effect** = how CO₂/H⁺/temp affect Hb's **affinity for O₂**.
- **Haldane Effect** = how O₂ binding affects Hb's ability to **carry CO₂**.

---

## Topic 15: Acid–Base Definitions — Patient vs. Blood

- **Acidosis** = patient condition; strong acid gained or base lost → ↓ pH ("acidotic").
- **Alkalosis** = patient condition; strong base gained or acid lost → ↑ pH ("alkalotic").
- **Acidemia** = blood pH below normal.
- **Alkalemia** = blood pH above normal.

---

## Topic 16: ROME Mnemonic

> **R**espiratory = **O**pposite · **M**etabolic = **E**qual

### Respiratory (CO₂) — INVERSE

| PaCO₂ | pH | Disorder |
|-------|-----|----------|
| ↑ CO₂ | ↓ pH | **Respiratory Acidosis** |
| ↓ CO₂ | ↑ pH | **Respiratory Alkalosis** |

### Metabolic (HCO₃⁻) — DIRECT

| HCO₃⁻ | pH | Disorder |
|-------|-----|----------|
| ↑ HCO₃⁻ | ↑ pH | **Metabolic Alkalosis** |
| ↓ HCO₃⁻ | ↓ pH | **Metabolic Acidosis** |

### Blood Gas Components

- **pH** — both metabolic and respiratory.
- **PaCO₂** — respiratory; **inverse** to pH.
- **HCO₃⁻** — metabolic; **linear/direct** with pH.
- **BE / BD** — metabolic; linear/direct with pH.

---

## Topic 17: Normal ABG Values

| Value | Range |
|-------|-------|
| **pH** | 7.35 – 7.45 |
| **PaCO₂** | 35 – 45 mmHg |
| **HCO₃⁻** | 22 – 26 mEq/L |

---

## Topic 18: Seven Steps of ABG Interpretation

1. **Categorize the pH** — acidotic <7.4 or alkalotic >7.4.
2. **Evaluate CO₂** — normal 35–45.
3. **Evaluate HCO₃⁻** — normal 22–26.
4. **What is causing the abnormal pH?** Apply **ROME**.
5. **Compensation?** None / partial / fully compensated. *Is the other organ helping?*
6. **Hypoxemia?** — abnormally low O₂ in the **blood**.
7. **Hypoxia?** — deficient O₂ reaching **tissues** (global or local).

---

## Topic 19: The Four Primary Acid–Base Disorders (Acute / Uncompensated)

### 1. Respiratory Acidosis — Hypoventilation

Causes: **COPD · Neuromuscular disorder · Drug / sedation overdose · ROSC**

### 2. Respiratory Alkalosis — Hyperventilation

Causes: **Acute asthma · Pulmonary embolism · Fever · Pregnancy · Hypoxia**

### 3. Metabolic Acidosis

Causes: **Ketoacidosis · Lactic acidosis · Renal failure · Diarrhea · Salicylate (ASA) poisoning**

### 4. Metabolic Alkalosis

Causes: **Fluid loss from GI tract · Severe vomiting · N-G tube complications · Diuretics · Severe potassium depletion · Excess HCO₃⁻ administration**
