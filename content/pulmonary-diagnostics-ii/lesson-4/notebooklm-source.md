> 2. **PA vs. AP Projections** — PA (Posterior-Anterior) projection is the standard: X-rays travel from posterior to anterior, image receptor touches the anterior chest, minimizing heart magnification. AP (Anterior-Posterior) projection: X-rays travel from anterior to posterior, image receptor is behind the back, commonly used for bedridden ICU patients using a portable machine. AP films artificially magnify the heart shadow.
>
> 3. **Lateral and Lateral Decubitus** — Lateral projection views structures behind the heart or lung bases. Lateral Decubitus position: patient lying on the affected side, highly valuable for detecting small pleural effusions.
>
> 4. **Lateral Neck Radiographs in Children** — Valuable for identifying upper airway obstruction. CROUP reveals subglottic narrowing known as the STEEPLE SIGN. EPIGLOTTITIS reveals a swollen, enlarged epiglottis known as the THUMB SIGN. Both require prompt therapy to prevent complete obstruction.
>
> 5. **Technical Film Quality — Three Factors** — PENETRATION, ROTATION, and INSPIRATION.
>
> 6. **Penetration / Exposure Criteria** — Degree to which X-rays pass through the body. Proper penetration: vertebrae are just visible behind the heart shadow with distinct intercostal spaces. Underexposed (under-penetrated): too white, cannot see intervertebral discs through the heart. Overexposed (over-penetrated): too black, shows black lung parenchyma without visible blood vessels.
>
> 7. **Rotation Criteria** — Patient should not be rotated. Verification: heads of the clavicles should be level and equidistant from the vertebral column.
>
> 8. **Inspiration Criteria** — Taken at full inspiration. Verification: 5 to 6 anterior ribs should be visible above the diaphragm, or 8 to 10 posterior ribs should be visible.
>
> 9. **Normal Chest X-ray General Landmarks** — Both hemidiaphragms are rounded and dome-shaped. Right hemidiaphragm is slightly higher than the left due to the liver, resting at the sixth anterior rib. Trachea is midline. Bilateral radiolucency with sharp costophrenic angles.
>
> 10. **Systematic Landmarks — Trachea and Mediastinum** — Trachea is a dark midline area; shift indicates a unilateral problem. Mediastinum contains the heart, lymphatics, vessels, and main bronchi; shifts with pleural effusion or pneumothorax.
>
> 11. **Hila and A-P Diameter** — Hila consist of major bronchi and pulmonary vessels; left hilum is normally higher than the right. A-P diameter is increased with COPD, barrel chest, and hyperinflation.
>
> 12. **Costophrenic Angles and Diaphragm** — Costophrenic angles are obliterated (blunted) by pleural effusions. Diaphragm is flattened in COPD.
>
> 13. **Heart Size and Silhouette** — Takes up less than half the thoracic cavity (C/T ratio less than 50 percent). 1/3 on the right, 2/3 on the left. Right border has two bulges (SVC, right atrium); left border has three bulges (aorta, pulmonary artery, left ventricle). Cardiomegaly indicates congestive heart failure or pericardial effusion.
>
> 14. **Soft Tissue, Ribs, and Pleura** — Subcutaneous emphysema occurs when free air (hyperlucency) tracks into surrounding soft tissue. Pleura is only visible when an abnormality is present (thickening, effusion, pneumothorax).
>
> 15. **Tube Verification — ETT and Trach** — ETT tip must be below vocal cords, below superior clavicular margin, and 2 to 5 cm above the carina. Trach tubes must be confirmed inside the trachea.
>
> 16. **Catheter Verification — Pacemaker, Swan-Ganz, CVC** — Pacemaker electrodes terminate in the right ventricle apex. Swan-Ganz catheters appear in the right lower lung field to measure pulmonary artery pressures. Central Venous Catheters (CVC) terminate in the lower superior vena cava just above the right atrium.
>
> 17. **Chest Tubes and Nasogastric Tubes** — Chest tubes must have the break in the radiopaque line fully inside the pleural space to drain air (pneumothorax) or fluid (hemothorax). NG tubes must reside in the stomach 2 to 6 cm below the diaphragm.

## Unit 1: Overview & Primary Indications

Chest radiography provides dynamic structural and baseline evaluation. Primary clinical indications include:
- Detecting alterations of the lung (infiltrates, atelectasis, masses)
- Determining appropriate therapy (bronchodilators, diuresis)
- Evaluating treatment effectiveness
- Verifying tube and monitoring catheter positions
- Observing disease progression
- Assessing the patient post-invasive procedure

---

## Unit 2: Positions & Projections

- **PA Projection (Posterior-Anterior):** Beam travels back-to-front. Standard upright position. Minimizes artificial magnification of the cardiac shadow.
- **AP Projection (Anterior-Posterior):** Beam travels front-to-back. Used for portable ICU films in bedridden patients. **Artificially magnifies the heart silhouette** because the heart is further from the image receptor.
- **Lateral Position:** Side projection against the image receptor.
- **Lateral Decubitus:** Patient lies on the affected side. Most sensitive projection for detecting **small pleural effusions**.
- **Lateral Neck Projections (Pediatric Airway Obstruction):**
  - **Croup:** Subglottic narrowing known as the **steeple sign**.
  - **Epiglottitis:** Swollen epiglottis known as the **thumb sign**.

## Unit 3: Technical Film Quality Validation

Evaluate every chest X-ray for **Penetration**, **Rotation**, and **Inspiration**.

### 1. Penetration / Exposure
- **Proper:** Thoracic vertebrae are just visible behind the heart shadow.
- **Underexposed:** Film is too white; cannot visualize intervertebral discs through the heart.
- **Overexposed:** Film is too black; lung parenchyma appears black without visible blood vessels.

### 2. Rotation
- Clavicle heads must be level and equidistant from the vertebral column spinous processes.

### 3. Inspiration
- Full inspiration yields **5 to 6 anterior ribs** or **8 to 10 posterior ribs** visible above the diaphragm.

## Unit 4: The Normal Chest Radiograph & Systematic Landmarks

### Normal General Features
- Domes of both hemidiaphragms are smooth and rounded.
- **Right hemidiaphragm is slightly higher** at the level of the sixth anterior rib due to the liver.
- Trachea is a midline vertical radiolucent column.
- Sharp acute costophrenic angles bilaterally.

### Specific Landmarks & Associated Pathology
- **Trachea:** Shifts away from tension pneumothorax/effusion; shifts toward atelectasis.
- **Mediastinum:** Central compartment; shifts with unilateral volume or pressure changes.
- **Hila:** Major bronchi and vessels; **left hilum sits higher** than the right.
- **A-P Diameter:** Increased in **COPD / Barrel chest** due to chronic hyperinflation.
- **Costophrenic Angles:** Obliterated or **blunted by pleural effusions**.
- **Diaphragm:** **Flattened** in severe COPD.
- **Heart Silhouette:** Takes up less than half the chest width (**C/T ratio < 50%**). **Cardiomegaly** indicates CHF or pericardial effusion.
- **Soft Tissue:** Air tracking into soft tissue represents **subcutaneous emphysema**.
- **Pleural Space:** Only visible when abnormal (thickening, effusion, pneumothorax).

## Unit 5: Verification of Device & Hardware Placement

| Hardware / Tube | Target Landmark Location | Clinical Context |
|-----------------|--------------------------|------------------|
| **Endotracheal Tube (ETT)** | **2 – 5 cm above the carina** (below vocal cords and clavicular margin, above T5) | Advanced airway patency. Too deep enters right mainstem bronchus. |
| **Tracheostomy Tube** | Mid-trachea inside the airway lumen | Long-term ventilation access. |
| **Pacemaker** | Wires terminate securely in the **right ventricle apex** | Cardiac conduction regulation. |
| **Swan-Ganz Catheter** | Tip placed in the **right lower lung field** | Measures pulmonary artery pressures. |
| **Central Venous Catheter (CVC)** | Lower **Superior Vena Cava (SVC)** directly above the right atrium | Long-term venous access and central pressure monitoring. |
| **Chest Tube** | Break in the radiopaque stripe must be **fully inside the pleural space** | Drains air (pneumothorax) or fluid/blood (hemothorax). |
| **Nasogastric (NG) Tube** | Positioned inside the **stomach 2 – 6 cm below the diaphragm** | Enteral feeding or gastric decompression. |
