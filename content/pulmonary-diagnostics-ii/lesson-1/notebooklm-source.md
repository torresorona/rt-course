> Walk through each unit in the order below, with airtime roughly proportional to the amount of content in each unit:
>
> 1. **What an ABG is and why we draw it** — an ARTERIAL PUNCTURE is the puncture of a peripheral artery to obtain a sample for the DIRECT measurement of THREE values: pH, PaCO2, and PaO2. The three big indications are: ONE — evaluating the adequacy of VENTILATION via PaCO2, OXYGENATION via PaO2, and ACID-BASE STATUS via pH. TWO — assessing the need for therapeutic intervention like OXYGEN THERAPY, MECHANICAL VENTILATION, or DIAGNOSTIC EVALUATION such as EXERCISE DESATURATION. THREE — monitoring the SEVERITY AND PROGRESSION of a documented disease process.
>
> 2. **ABG contraindications** — NEGATIVE MODIFIED ALLEN TEST (no collateral circulation); ARTERIOVENOUS SHUNT in dialysis patients; LESION at the puncture site; LIMB with PERIPHERAL VASCULAR DISEASE; an IMPROPERLY FUNCTIONING blood gas analyzer.
>
> 3. **ABG complications and hazards** — THROMBOSIS, HEMATOMA, ARTERIOSPASM (transient constriction of the artery), PAIN, AIR EMBOLI, INFECTION, PERIPHERAL NERVE DAMAGE, and VASOVAGAL REACTION which can result in LOSS OF CONSCIOUSNESS.
>
> 4. **Sample sites by age group** — ADULT sites: RADIAL, BRACHIAL, FEMORAL, DORSALIS PEDIS. NEWBORN: UMBILICAL ARTERY — patent during the first 24 TO 48 HOURS after birth via catheter insertion. INFANT: RADIAL and SCALP (the SUPERFICIAL TEMPORAL ARTERY). PEDIATRICS: RADIAL, BRACHIAL, and CAPILLARY.
>
> 5. **Equipment and supplies** — the syringe is a 1-, 3-, or 5-mL self-filling, disposable, plastic syringe pre-filled with DRY LITHIUM HEPARIN anticoagulant. Needle is 20- TO 25-GAUGE, SHORT-BEVELED for adults. Needle length is 5/8 to 1 1/2 INCHES, with the LONGEST needles used for BRACHIAL or FEMORAL sites. Children and neonates get a 25-GAUGE syringe.
>
> 6. **Glass vs. plastic syringes** — GLASS SYRINGES ARE NO LONGER THE STANDARD. PLASTIC SYRINGES are MORE PERMEABLE and susceptible to SUBSTANTIAL DIFFUSION OF ROOM AIR. PaO2 can be SKEWED if BUBBLES are present in the sample. Use quality plastic syringes with or without pre-filled heparin.
>
> 7. **Anticoagulant and accessories** — the anticoagulant of CHOICE for ABG sampling is LITHIUM HEPARIN. DRY anticoagulant is preferred; LIQUID may be added if dry is unavailable. ICE helps preserve the blood sample by SLOWING METABOLIC ACTIVITY. LOCAL ANESTHETIC may relieve discomfort and minimize the risk of VASOCONSTRICTION.
>
> 8. **Why the radial artery is the vessel of choice** — RADIAL is the vessel of choice. Use the NON-DOMINANT hand. AVOID near a SURGICAL SCAR. Pulsations are readily palpable APPROXIMATELY 1 INCH FROM THE WRIST where the artery passes above the RADIUS BONE. ALWAYS verify there is COLLATERAL CIRCULATION before puncturing — if absent, radial puncture is NOT recommended.
>
> 9. **Modified Allen test** — used to determine the adequacy of ULNAR circulation. Steps: patient CLENCHES THE FIST forcing blood from the hand; clinician applies EXTERNAL PRESSURE TO BOTH the radial AND ulnar arteries; patient RELAXES (not full extension) the hand which BLANCHES the palm and fingers; RELEASE the ULNAR artery — the hand should FLUSH within 5 TO 10 SECONDS if ulnar flow is adequate. Alternatively, ulnar circulation can be checked with a DOPPLER ULTRASONIC FLOW INDICATOR.
>
> 10. **Radial ABG procedure** — patient seated or lying down; wrist EXTENDED approximately 30 DEGREES with towels. Wash hands, don gloves. PALPATE A DEFINITE PULSE — DO NOT puncture if a palpable pulse cannot be distinguished. Site prepped with 70% ISOPROPYL ALCOHOL. Hold the syringe LIKE A PENCIL OR DART. Insert at a 30 TO 45-DEGREE ANGLE OR LESS with the BEVEL TURNED UPWARD. Advance the needle SLOWLY. For redirection, WITHDRAW ALMOST TO THE SKIN SURFACE before redirecting. Be very cautious to avoid VENOUS BLOOD CONTAMINATION. ARTERIAL blood FLASHES/PULSATES as it enters the syringe.
>
> 11. **After the radial puncture** — once 1 TO 2 cc is obtained, withdraw the needle quickly and apply STERILE GAUZE. Apply pressure for 3 TO 5 MINUTES — but 10 TO 15 MINUTES if the patient is on anticoagulant therapy or has a bleeding disorder. INSPECT for bleeding before bandaging. REMOVE AIR BUBBLES, CAP THE SYRINGE, and gently mix by INVERSION AND ROTATION to prevent clot formation.
>
> 12. **Specimen labeling** — patient NAME; DATE AND TIME; ID NUMBER; PUNCTURE SITE; ALLEN'S TEST RESULT (positive or negative); OXYGEN DEVICE OR INVASIVE/NONINVASIVE SETTINGS; PATIENT TEMPERATURE. Then analyze, assess results, enter in the chart, and REPORT CRITICAL RESULTS to the attending physician.
>
> 13. **Brachial artery** — SECOND CHOICE in adults when radial is unsuitable. The brachial is the major artery of the upper arm and BIFURCATES INTO THE RADIAL AND ULNAR just below the elbow. Located a SHORT DISTANCE ABOVE THE BEND OF THE ELBOW on the INTERNAL surface of the arm where it passes over the HUMERUS. SPECIAL HAZARD: PUNCTURE OF THE MEDIAN NERVE — possible PERMANENT NERVE DAMAGE. Use the NON-DOMINANT side. Procedure: extend the arm; rotate the wrist until the STRONGEST pulse is found just above the skin crease; trace the artery 2 TO 3 CM up the arm with another finger; insert at a 45-DEGREE angle. After puncture follow the same procedure as radial.
>
> 14. **Femoral artery** — RESERVED FOR EMERGENCIES — hypotensive patient with poor peripheral pulses. Located along the INGUINAL LIGAMENT with the patient lying flat with legs extended. LARGE DIAMETER makes it an easy target, BUT it lies DEEP below the skin adjacent to the FEMORAL NERVE AND VEIN. Insert NEARLY PERPENDICULAR to the skin surface. SPECIAL HAZARDS: LARGE QUANTITIES OF BLOOD MAY SEEP from this vessel and may go UNNOTICED because of its DEEP, INCONSPICUOUS LOCATION; INCREASED RISK OF INFECTION; COLLATERAL CIRCULATION IS ALMOST NONEXISTENT.
>
> 15. **A-line indications and contraindications** — INDICATIONS: FREQUENT ARTERIAL BLOOD GAS MEASUREMENTS and CONTINUOUS BLOOD PRESSURE MONITORING. CONTRAINDICATIONS: NEGATIVE MODIFIED ALLEN TEST; COAGULOPATHY OR BLEEDING DISORDERS; POOR PERIPHERAL/DISTAL CIRCULATION. HAZARDS: INFECTION, THROMBOSIS, ISCHEMIA, NERVE DAMAGE.
>
> 16. **A-line dampening** — a DAMPENED waveform appears flattened. Causes: CLOTS IN THE CATHETER; TIP OF THE CATHETER AGAINST THE WALL of the vessel; AIR BUBBLES IN THE LINE OR CLOT IN THE TRANSDUCER.
>
> 17. **A-line abnormally high or low readings** — caused by IMPROPER CALIBRATION or IMPROPER TRANSDUCER POSITION. Transducer position rule: TRANSDUCER BELOW THE LEVEL OF THE HEART — reading reads HIGHER than actual. TRANSDUCER ABOVE THE LEVEL OF THE HEART — reading reads LOWER than actual.
>
> 18. **Drawing blood from an A-line** — access is through a THREE-WAY STOPCOCK. (Reference: Box 19.4 in Egan's outlines the proper procedure.)
>
> 19. **Capillary blood gas — when and why** — used as an ALTERNATIVE to direct arterial access in INFANTS and SMALL CHILDREN. Provides ESTIMATES of ARTERIAL pH and PCO2. CAPILLARY PO2 is OF LITTLE VALUE in estimating arterial oxygenation. Clinicians must be VERY CAUTIOUS using capillary blood gases for clinical decisions.
>
> 20. **Capillary BG contraindications** — DO NOT puncture at: FINGERS OF NEONATES; PREVIOUS PUNCTURE SITES; SWOLLEN OR EDEMATOUS TISSUE; CYANOTIC, POORLY PERFUSED TISSUE; AREAS OF INFECTION. Also do NOT perform on patients LESS THAN 24 HOURS OLD (insufficient peripheral perfusion) or WHEN ACCURATE OXYGENATION ANALYSIS IS NEEDED.
>
> 21. **Capillary BG hazards** — INFECTION, CONTAMINATION, BURNS, HEMATOMA, NERVE DAMAGE, PAIN, BLEEDING, SCARRING.
>
> 22. **Capillary BG equipment** — LANCET, PRE-HEPARINIZED CAPILLARY TUBES, SMALL METAL STIRRER BAR (the "METAL FLEA"), MAGNET, CLAY OR WAX SEALANT/CAPS, GAUZE OR COTTON BALLS, BANDAGES, SKIN ANTISEPTIC, and WARMING PADS at 42 DEGREES CELSIUS.
>
> 23. **Capillary BG procedure** — the MOST COMMON SITE for sampling is the HEEL, specifically the LATERAL ASPECT OF THE PLANTAR SURFACE.
>
> 24. **Capillary BG troubleshooting** — INADEQUATE WARMING of the capillary bed leads to inaccurate samples. SQUEEZING THE PUNCTURE SITE causes VENOUS AND LYMPHATIC CONTAMINATION of the sample.
>
> **High-yield distinctions to reinforce:**
> - ABG measures THREE values DIRECTLY: pH, PaCO2, PaO2.
> - Vessel ORDER for adults: RADIAL (1st choice) → BRACHIAL (2nd choice) → FEMORAL (emergencies only). Dorsalis pedis is also an adult site.
> - NEWBORN site = UMBILICAL artery (24–48 hr window).
> - INFANT scalp site = SUPERFICIAL TEMPORAL artery.
> - Anticoagulant of choice = LITHIUM HEPARIN, preferably DRY.
> - PLASTIC syringes leak room air → bubbles SKEW PaO2 → REMOVE AIR BUBBLES before analysis. ICE preserves the sample by slowing metabolism.
> - MODIFIED ALLEN TEST checks ULNAR collateral flow. POSITIVE = flush in 5–10 s = OK to puncture. NEGATIVE = inadequate = DO NOT puncture.
> - Radial puncture angle: 30–45° with the BEVEL UP. Brachial: 45°. Femoral: nearly PERPENDICULAR.
> - Hold pressure 3–5 MIN normally; 10–15 MIN if anticoagulated or has a bleeding disorder.
> - Mix the sample by INVERSION and ROTATION (not shaking).
> - BRACHIAL danger = MEDIAN NERVE injury. FEMORAL dangers = HIDDEN BLEEDING, INFECTION, NO COLLATERAL CIRCULATION.
> - A-line indications: FREQUENT ABGs and CONTINUOUS BP monitoring.
> - DAMPENING causes: CLOT IN CATHETER, TIP AGAINST VESSEL WALL, AIR BUBBLES, CLOT IN TRANSDUCER.
> - TRANSDUCER POSITION RULE: BELOW heart = reads HIGH; ABOVE heart = reads LOW.
> - A-line sampling = THREE-WAY STOPCOCK.
> - CAPILLARY blood gas: estimates pH and PCO2 only — PO2 is UNRELIABLE.
> - DO NOT cap-stick a baby less than 24 HOURS OLD or use the FINGERS OF NEONATES.
> - Most common cap stick site = HEEL, LATERAL PLANTAR SURFACE.
> - WARM the heel to 42°C. NEVER squeeze the site (causes venous/lymphatic contamination).

## Topic 1: Definition & Indications for ABGs

**Arterial puncture** — puncture of a peripheral artery to obtain an arterial blood sample for the **direct measurement** of:

- **pH**
- **PaCO₂**
- **PaO₂**

**Indications:**

1. **Evaluation of the adequacy of a patient's:**
   - **Ventilation** — PaCO₂
   - **Oxygenation** — PaO₂
   - **Acid–base status** — pH
2. **Assess the need for therapeutic intervention:**
   - Oxygen therapy
   - Mechanical ventilation
   - Diagnostic evaluation (e.g., exercise desaturation)
3. **Monitor the severity and progression** of a documented disease process.

---

## Topic 2: ABG Contraindications

- **Negative Modified Allen test**
- **Arteriovenous shunt** (dialysis patient)
- **Lesion at the puncture site**
- Limb with **peripheral vascular disease**
- **Improperly functioning blood gas analyzer**

---

## Topic 3: ABG Complications & Hazards

- Thrombosis
- Hematoma
- Arteriospasm (transient constriction of the artery)
- Pain
- Air emboli
- Infection
- Peripheral nerve damage
- **Vasovagal reaction** — can result in **loss of consciousness**

---

## Topic 4: Sample Sites by Age Group

| Age Group | Sites |
|-----------|-------|
| **Adult** | Radial, Brachial, Femoral, Dorsalis pedis |
| **Newborn** | Umbilical artery (patent during the first **24–48 hours** after birth via catheter) |
| **Infant** | Radial, Scalp (**superficial temporal artery**) |
| **Pediatrics** | Radial, Brachial, Capillary |

## Topic 5: Equipment & Supplies

- **Syringe** — 1-, 3-, or 5-mL self-filling disposable plastic, pre-filled with **dry lithium heparin** anticoagulant.
- **Needle** — 20- to 25-gauge, short-beveled for adult arterial sampling.
  - Length **⅝–1½ in** (longest needles for brachial or femoral).
  - Children/neonates — **25-gauge** syringe.
- **Glass vs. plastic** — Glass syringes are **no longer the standard**.
  - Plastic syringes are **more permeable** and susceptible to **substantial diffusion of room air**.
  - PaO₂ can be **skewed** if bubbles are present in the sample.
- **Anticoagulant** — **lithium heparin** is the anticoagulant of choice; **dry** is preferred over liquid.
- **Ice** — slows metabolic activity to preserve the sample.
- **Local anesthetic** — may relieve discomfort and minimize vasoconstriction.

---

## Topic 6: Radial Artery — Why It's the Vessel of Choice

- **Vessel of choice** for arterial puncture
- Use the **non-dominant hand**
- **Avoid surgical scar sites**
- Pulsations are readily palpable approximately **1 inch from the wrist** where the artery passes above the **radius bone**
- **Verify collateral circulation** — if absent, radial puncture is **not recommended**

---

## Topic 7: Modified Allen Test

Used to determine the adequacy of **ulnar** circulation:

1. Patient **clenches the fist**, forcing blood from the hand
2. Clinician applies **external pressure** to **both** the radial **and** ulnar arteries
3. Patient **relaxes** the hand (not full extension) — palm and fingers will **blanch**
4. **Release the ulnar artery** — hand should **flush within 5–10 seconds** if ulnar flow is adequate

Alternatively, ulnar circulation can be assessed with a **Doppler ultrasonic flow indicator**.

---

## Topic 8: Radial ABG Procedure

1. Patient seated or lying down; wrist **extended ~30°** with towel support.
2. **Wash hands**, don **gloves**.
3. Palpate a **definite pulse** — **do not puncture** if no palpable pulse.
4. Prep site with **70% isopropyl alcohol**.
5. Hold the syringe **like a pencil or dart**.
6. Insert needle at **30–45° angle (or less)**, **bevel up**, advancing **slowly**.
7. **Redirection:** withdraw needle **almost to the skin surface** before redirecting.
8. Be **very cautious** to avoid **venous blood contamination**.
9. Arterial blood **flashes/pulsates** as it enters the syringe.

---

## Topic 9: After the Radial Puncture

- Sample obtained (**1–2 cc**) → withdraw needle quickly → apply sterile gauze.
- **Hold pressure** for:
  - **3–5 minutes** — normal patient
  - **10–15 minutes** — patient on anticoagulants or with bleeding disorder
- Inspect for bleeding before bandaging.
- **Remove air bubbles**, cap the syringe.
- Mix the specimen by **inversion and rotation** to prevent clot formation.

---

## Topic 10: Specimen Labeling

Label with:

- Patient name
- Date and time
- ID #
- Puncture site
- Allen's test (positive or negative)
- Oxygen device or invasive/noninvasive settings
- Patient temperature

Then analyze, assess, document, and **report critical results to the attending physician**.

---

## Topic 11: Brachial Artery

- **Second choice** in adults when radial is unsuitable.
- Major artery of the upper arm; **bifurcates into radial and ulnar** just below the elbow.
- Located **a short distance above the bend of the elbow** on the **internal surface** of the arm where it passes over the **humerus**.

**Hazards:**
- **Puncture of the median nerve**
- Trauma → **permanent median nerve damage**
- Use the **non-dominant side**

**Procedure:**
- Extend the arm.
- Rotate the wrist until the **strongest pulse** is felt just above the skin crease.
- **Trace the artery 2–3 cm** up the arm with another finger.
- Insert at a **45° angle**.
- After puncture, follow the same post-puncture steps as radial.

---

## Topic 12: Femoral Artery

- **Reserved for emergencies** — hypotensive patient with poor peripheral pulses.
- Located along the **inguinal ligament**; patient lies flat with legs extended.
- **Large diameter** = easy target, BUT lies **deep below the skin** adjacent to the **femoral nerve and vein**.
- Insert needle **nearly perpendicular** to the skin surface.

**Hazards:**
- Large quantities of blood may **seep unnoticed** because of the deep, inconspicuous location.
- **Increased risk of infection**.
- **Collateral circulation is almost nonexistent**.

---

## Topic 13: A-Line Indications, Contraindications, Hazards

**Indications:**
- Frequent arterial blood gas measurements
- Continuous blood pressure monitoring

**Contraindications:**
- Negative Modified Allen test
- Coagulopathy / bleeding disorders
- Poor peripheral / distal circulation

**Hazards:**
- Infection
- Thrombosis
- Ischemia
- Nerve damage

---

## Topic 14: A-Line Dampening

A **dampened** waveform appears flattened. Causes:

- **Clots in the catheter**
- **Tip of the catheter against the wall** of the vessel
- **Air bubbles** in the line, or a **clot in the transducer**

---

## Topic 15: A-Line Abnormally High or Low Readings

Causes:

- **Improper calibration**
- **Improper transducer position**

**Transducer position rule:**
- Transducer **below** the level of the heart → reads **HIGHER** than actual.
- Transducer **above** the level of the heart → reads **LOWER** than actual.

---

## Topic 16: Drawing Blood from an A-Line

- Access for sampling from most intravascular lines is provided by a **three-way stopcock**.
- See **Box 19.4** in Egan's for the full step-by-step procedure.

---

## Topic 17: Capillary Blood Gas — Indications & Limits

- **Alternative to direct arterial access** in infants and small children.
- Provides **estimates of arterial pH and PCO₂**.
- **Capillary PO₂ is of little value** in estimating arterial oxygenation.
- Clinicians must be **very cautious** when using capillary blood gases to guide clinical decisions.

---

## Topic 18: Capillary BG Contraindications

Do **not** perform a capillary puncture at:

- Fingers of neonates
- Previous puncture sites
- Swollen or edematous tissue
- Cyanotic, poorly perfused tissue
- Areas of infection

Do **not** perform on:

- Patients **less than 24 hours old** (insufficient peripheral perfusion)
- When **accurate oxygenation analysis is needed**

---

## Topic 19: Capillary BG Hazards

- Infection
- Contamination
- **Burns**
- Hematoma
- Nerve damage
- Pain
- Bleeding
- Scarring

---

## Topic 20: Capillary BG Equipment

- Lancet
- Pre-heparinized capillary tubes
- Small metal stirrer bar (**metal flea**)
- Magnet
- Clay or wax sealant or caps
- Gauze or cotton balls
- Bandages
- Skin antiseptic
- Warming pads (**42 °C**)

## Topic 21: Capillary BG Procedure

- Most common site = **heel**, specifically the **lateral aspect of the plantar surface**.

## Topic 22: Capillary BG Troubleshooting

- **Inadequate warming** of the capillary bed → inaccurate sample.
- **Squeezing the puncture site** → **venous and lymphatic contamination** of the sample.

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **ABG measures 3 values directly:** pH, PaCO₂, PaO₂.
- **Adult site order:** RADIAL → BRACHIAL → FEMORAL (emergencies only). Dorsalis pedis is an alternate adult site.
- **Newborn site:** UMBILICAL artery (first **24–48 hr** of life).
- **Infant sites:** RADIAL + SCALP (superficial temporal artery).
- **Pediatric sites:** RADIAL, BRACHIAL, CAPILLARY.
- **Anticoagulant of choice:** lithium heparin (DRY preferred).
- **Plastic syringe issue:** permeable to room air → bubbles skew **PaO₂** → remove air bubbles, transport on **ice**.
- **MODIFIED ALLEN TEST:** checks **ULNAR** flow. Positive = flushes 5–10 s = OK. Negative = DO NOT puncture.
- **Wrist extension:** ~30° on a towel.
- **Insertion angles:** Radial 30–45° (bevel up) · Brachial 45° · Femoral nearly perpendicular.
- **Pressure post-puncture:** 3–5 min normally · 10–15 min if anticoagulated or has a bleeding disorder.
- **Mix sample:** by INVERSION and ROTATION (not shaking).
- **Brachial unique hazard:** MEDIAN NERVE damage.
- **Femoral unique hazards:** hidden bleeding, infection, **almost no collateral circulation**.
- **A-line indications:** frequent ABGs · continuous BP monitoring.
- **A-line contraindications:** negative Allen, coagulopathy, poor distal flow.
- **DAMPENING causes:** clot in catheter · tip against vessel wall · air bubble or clot in transducer.
- **Transducer rule:** BELOW heart → HIGH reading; ABOVE heart → LOW reading.
- **A-line sampling:** through a **three-way stopcock**.
- **Capillary BG measures pH & PCO₂ only** — PO₂ is **unreliable**.
- **Capillary BG age cutoff:** not in patients **<24 hr old**; never on **fingers of neonates**.
- **Most common cap stick site:** **heel — lateral plantar surface**.
- **Warm to 42 °C; never squeeze** — squeezing causes **venous/lymphatic contamination**.
