## Topic 1: General Clinical Presentation — Color

The first thing you notice about a patient is often their color.

- **Cyanosis (blue)** — hypoxemia.
- **Erythema (red)** — infection, inflammation.
- **Ashen (grey/dusky)** — anemia, shock.
- **Jaundice (yellow)** — high bilirubin, liver problems.

---

## Topic 2: Vital Signs — Temperature

### Normal Ranges

- Oral: 97.0° – 99.5° F
- Axillary: 96.7° – 98.5° F
- Rectal / Ear: 98.7° – 100.5° F

### Fever (Febrile)

Fever is an elevation of the body's temperature. It presents in three patterns:

- **Sustained** — varies less than a degree within 24 hours.
- **Remittent** — elevated with wide variations.
- **Intermittent** — elevation with a return to normal and subnormal spikes.

Diaphoresis or night sweats can occur with fever.

**Common causes:**

- Infection (most common)
- Dehydration
- Reactions to substances, drugs, or protein breakdown

---

## Topic 3: Vital Signs — Pulse

Pulse can be seen on the monitor or evaluated for rate, rhythm, and strength through palpation.

- Normal: 60 – 100 bpm
- Tachycardia: > 100 bpm
- Bradycardia: < 60 bpm

---

## Topic 4: Vital Signs — Blood Pressure

Blood pressure is the force exerted against the wall of the arteries as blood moves through them.

- **Systolic pressure** — peak force exerted during contraction of the left ventricle. Normal: 90 – 140 mmHg.
- **Diastolic pressure** — force remaining during relaxation of the ventricles. Normal: 60 – 90 mmHg.
- **Normal BP:** 120/80 mmHg.

**Hypotension** (decreased BP) indicates poor perfusion — hypovolemia, CHF.
**Hypertension** (increased BP) indicates cardiac stress and hypoxemia.

---

## Topic 5: Breathing Rates & Patterns — Counting and Normal

Count chest rise and fall to obtain the rate:

- 30 seconds × 2
- 20 seconds × 3
- 15 seconds × 4

Normal resting adult rate: **12 – 18 breaths per minute**.

**Eupnea** is the term for normal respiratory rate, rhythm, and depth.

---

## Topic 6: Rate-Based Patterns

- **Tachypnea** — RR > 20 bpm. Causes: hypoxia, fever, pain.
- **Bradypnea** — RR < 12 bpm. Causes: sleep, drugs, alcohol.
- **Apnea** — complete absence of breathing. Causes: cardiac arrest, drug overdose, severe brain trauma.

---

## Topic 7: Hyperpnea

Increased rate and depth of breathing. Caused by metabolic disorders.

---

## Topic 8: Kussmaul's

Labored breathing with increased respiratory rate and volume.

**Cause:** late stages of severe metabolic acidosis. The patient becomes "air hungry," and the desperate gasping characteristic of Kussmaul's breathing appears involuntary.

---

## Topic 9: Cheyne-Stokes

Gradually increasing then decreasing in depth and rate with periods of apnea.

**Causes:**

- Stroke
- Traumatic brain injuries
- Brain tumors
- Carbon dioxide poisoning
- Metabolic encephalopathy
- First-time high-altitude sickness
- Normal side effect of IV morphine administration

---

## Topic 10: Biot's

Irregular breathing with long periods of apnea. Each breath has the same depth.

**Causes:**

- Increased intracranial pressure
- Damage to the medulla oblongata by stroke (CVA) or trauma
- Pressure on the medulla due to herniation
- Prolonged opioid abuse

---

## Topic 11: Ataxic

A completely irregular breathing pattern with irregular pauses and unpredictable periods of apnea. As breathing continues to deteriorate, ataxic breathing begins to merge with agonal respirations.

**Causes:**

- Damage to the medulla oblongata secondary to trauma or stroke
- Usually indicates a very poor prognosis

---

## Topic 12: Apneustic

Prolonged gasping inspiration followed by extremely short, insufficient expiration.

**Cause:** damage to the upper part of the pons.

---

## Topic 13: Position-Related Breathing Patterns

- **Trepopnea** — difficulty breathing on one side but not when the patient turns to the other side. Associated with disorders of the chest that occur on only one side.
- **Platypnea** — difficulty breathing unless lying flat; relieved by a recumbent position.
- **Orthopnea** — must sit or stand to breathe properly; associated with left heart failure.

### Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND)

Sudden onset of difficulty breathing that occurs when a sleeping patient is lying flat. Caused by the gradual transfer of fluid in the lower extremities to the lungs. Associated with coughing. Relieved when the patient assumes an upright position.

---

## Topic 14: Physical Examination — Head and Neck

### Face

Facial expressions help determine distress, pain, alertness, mood, and general character.

- Look for signs of cyanosis around the lips.
- Pursed-lip breathing is a classic COPD finding.
- Excessive sweating.

### Eyes

The eye exam is part of the neurologic assessment. Check for normal pupillary reflex — **PERRLA**. Cranial nerves II and III must be intact for normal reflexes. Head trauma, tumor, central nervous system disease, and certain medications can cause abnormal findings.

### Neck

Inspection of the neck helps identify tracheal deviation, jugular venous pressure, and accessory muscle use.

---

## Topic 15: Physical Examination — Chest

Assess the **pattern and effort** of breathing, and assess the thoracic configuration.

### Normal

Normal A-P distance is less than the transverse distance. An increased A-P diameter is a sign of COPD — aka barrel-chested.

### Abnormal Chest Configurations

- **Pectus excavatum** — depression of part or all of the sternum.
- **Pectus carinatum** — anterior protrusion of the sternum.

### Abnormal Spinal Configurations

- **Scoliosis** — lateral curvature.
- **Kyphosis** — anteroposterior curvature.
- **Kyphoscoliosis** — combination of kyphosis and scoliosis.

---

## Topic 16: Physical Examination — Hands

**Digital clubbing** is a sign of pulmonary disease caused by chronic hypoxemia. Clubbing is present when the angle of the nail bed and skin increases.

---

## Topic 17: Palpation — Definition

Palpation is the use of the hands to feel for body movement, lumps, masses, and skin characteristics.

---

## Topic 18: Chest Palpation

Chest palpation looks for:

- Asymmetrical chest movements
- Estimated thoracic expansion
- Skin and tissue quality
- Vocal fremitus

**Vocal fremitus** = vibrations created by the vocal cords during phonation, transmitted down the tracheobronchial tree and through the alveoli to the chest wall.

**Tactile fremitus** = when those vibrations are felt on the chest wall as the patient talks.

---

## Topic 19: Pulse Palpation

Assess for rate, rhythm, and force.

### Rate

- Normal: 60 – 100 bpm
- Tachycardia: > 100 bpm
- Bradycardia: < 60 bpm

### Rhythm

- **Regular**
- **Regularly irregular** — irregular pulse that occurs in a continuous pattern (beat beat pause · beat beat pause…)
- **Irregularly irregular** — no pattern

### Force

- **Normal**
- **Bounding** — full pulse that is difficult to depress with fingertips
- **Weak** — low volume, compresses easily
- **Absent** — cannot be felt

### Paradoxical Pulse ("Pulsus Paradoxus")

Pulse/blood pressure varies with respiration — systolic pressure drops more than 10 mmHg during inhalation at rest.

**Caused by:** severe air trapping, cardiac tamponade, status asthmaticus.

---

## Topic 20: Palpating a Pulse — Technique and Sites

### Technique

1. Place pads of the index and middle fingers lightly over the pulse point.
2. Compress until maximum pulsation is felt.
3. Evaluate rhythm and strength.
4. Count beats felt to obtain the rate — 30 s × 2, 20 s × 3, or 15 s × 4.

### Frequently Used Sites

- **Radial artery** — most commonly used.
- **Carotid artery** — neck below the jaw, lateral to the larynx/trachea.
- **Brachial artery** — anterior aspect of the elbow, gently pressing against the underlying bone.
- **Femoral artery** — central thigh between the pubic symphysis and anterior superior iliac spine.
- **Popliteal pulse** — posterior knee.
- **Posterior tibial pulse** — posterior and inferior to the medial malleolus.
- **Dorsalis pedis pulse** — groove between the first and second toes, slightly medial on the dorsum of the foot.
- **Temporal pulse** — temple, directly in front of the ear.

---

## Topic 21: Trachea Palpation

Palpate the trachea for deviation — a shift of the trachea to one side. Depress the index finger into the suprasternal notch and palpate.

---

## Topic 22: Blood Pressure — Technique

### Patient Positioning

- Seat the patient for at least 5 minutes prior to measurement.
- Arm rests on a table so the midpoint of the upper arm is at heart level.
- Patient does not cross their legs before or during the measurement.

### Cuff Selection and Placement

- Bladder in the cuff should encircle **80% of the arm**.
- Too large or too small results in **false high measurements**.
- Place the cuff mid-way between the shoulder and the elbow.
- Markings on the cuff should be centered over the brachial artery.

### Measurement

1. Position and secure the gauge so it can be easily seen and read.
2. Locate the brachial artery by palpation, then place the stethoscope on the artery.
3. Close the valve and inflate the cuff.
4. Inflate to 70 mmHg, then increase until the pulse can no longer be felt.
5. Release pressure slowly, listening carefully and watching the gauge.
6. Note the point at which the first sound is heard — **systolic**.
7. Note the point at which the last sound or change in sound is heard — **diastolic**.
8. Release pressure completely after the diastolic value is obtained.

### Retry Rules

- If unsure of the reading, fully deflate the cuff for at least 30 seconds before reattempting.
- Do NOT allow the cuff to remain inflated for longer than 20 seconds.
