## Topic 1: What Patient Inspection Covers

Patient assessment is the key to diagnosing the patient properly. In this lesson, inspection involves five parallel activities: communicating with the patient, keeping patient privacy and confidentiality, monitoring cardiopulmonary signs and symptoms, assessing vital signs, and performing a physical assessment.

---

## Topic 2: Communicating with the Patient — Nonverbal Cues

Nonverbal communication often tells you more than the words a patient chooses. Pay attention to:

- **Body movement** — how the patient holds themselves, guards, or moves.
- **Facial expression** — grimacing, tightness, relaxation.
- **Touch** — whether the patient reaches for you or pulls away.
- **Eye movement** — tracking, avoidance, or darting eyes.

---

## Topic 3: Patient Privacy & Confidentiality — Proxemic Zones

Respecting the patient's space is part of building rapport. As trust builds, you move closer — never the other way around.

### Social Space (4 – 12 feet)

Social space is used by the therapist to help build and establish rapport and allows you to see the "big picture."

### Personal Space (18 inches – 4 feet)

Personal space is used to garner sensitive patient information. It allows the patient to feel more comfortable sharing.

### Intimate Space (0 – 18 inches)

Intimate space is reserved primarily for physical examination and treatment. It should be done **after** establishing rapport and asking for permission.

---

## Topic 4: OLDCART — The Symptom Interview Framework

When a patient reports a symptom, walk through OLDCART to make sure you've covered every angle:

- **O** – Onset
- **L** – Location
- **D** – Duration
- **C** – Characteristics
- **A** – Associated manifestations
- **R** – Relieving factors
- **T** – Treatment

---

## Topic 5: Cough

Cough is a reflex triggered by stimulation of receptors located in the pharynx, larynx, and large bronchi. Triggers include inflammation, mucus, and foreign materials. It is the **most common symptom** in patients with pulmonary disease.

When assessing a cough, ask:

- Is it dry or loose?
- Is it productive or non-productive?
- Is it acute or chronic?
- Does it occur more frequently at certain times (such as at night, with exercise, or with cold air)?

---

## Topic 6: Sputum Production

Sputum production is most often caused by mucous gland inflammation.

- **Sputum** — substance expelled from the tracheobronchial tree, pharynx, mouth, sinuses, and nose via coughing or throat clearing.
- **Phlegm** — secretions from the lungs and tracheobronchial tree **not contaminated** by oral secretions.

### Assessment: Color

- **Normal** — clear, colorless, egg white.
- **Mucoid** (white-gray and thick) — emphysema, pulmonary tuberculosis, early chronic bronchitis, asthma.
- **Purulent** (green, rusty, or yellow — contains pus) — infection; pneumonia caused by *Haemophilus influenzae*, streptococci, or staphylococci.
- **Mucopurulent** — infection, cystic fibrosis.
- **Brown** — smoker, old blood.
- **Frothy pink** — pulmonary edema.
- **Blood-streaked / hemoptysis** — bronchogenic carcinoma, tuberculosis.

### Assessment: Consistency

Thin, thick, viscous (gelatinous), tenacious (extremely sticky), and frothy.

### Assessment: Odor

**Fetid** is a foul odor that can indicate:

- **Lung abscess** — local collection of pus and dead tissue.
- **Aspiration** — inhalation of foreign material into the lungs.
- **Anaerobic infection** — bacteria thriving without oxygen.
- **Bronchiectasis** — repeated lung infections/inflammation that dilates the bronchi and bronchioles.

### Assessment: Quantity

Scant, small, moderate, large, copious.

## Topic 7: Dyspnea (Shortness of Breath)

Dyspnea is the **subjective** experience of breathing discomfort. It consists of qualitatively distinct sensations that vary in intensity — it is difficulty breathing as perceived by the patient. Dyspnea can impair work, exercise, and activities of daily living.

### ADLs — "DEATH"

- **D**ressing
- **E**ating
- **A**mbulating
- **T**ransferring / Toileting
- **H**ygiene

### Common Causes of Dyspnea

- Asthma (acute on chronic)
- Pneumothorax
- Pneumonia
- Pulmonary emboli
- Pulmonary edema
- Hyperventilation
- Poor physical conditioning

### Assessment Scales

- **Modified Borg** — 0 to 10 scoring system with descriptive terms; used to assess dyspnea after performing certain activities.
- **American Thoracic Society (ATS) SOB Scale** — 0 to 4 range; uses specific terms to depict degree of dyspnea; used with questionnaires to quantify dyspnea during ADLs.
- **Dyspnea-12 Survey** — quantifies breathlessness using 12 physical and psychosocial descriptors; utilized for asthma patients.

---

## Topic 8: Pulmonary-Induced Chest Pain

Pulmonary-induced chest pain usually involves the chest wall or parietal pleura. It can be induced by pneumonia, lung abscess, and pulmonary infarction.

- **Pleuritic pain** is the most common symptom — sharp, stabbing pain normally felt during inspiration.
- **Chest wall pain** presents as a dull, constant ache.
- Pain can also be caused by intercostal or pectoral muscles.

---

## Topic 9: Edema

Edema is soft tissue swelling resulting from abnormal fluid accumulation. It is associated with pulmonary, cardiac, kidney, and liver disease.

### Peripheral (Dependent) Edema

The right ventricle becomes enlarged and unable to pump all the blood to the lungs. The venous system engorges, and due to high pressure, fluid is pushed out into the tissues.

- First seen in the lower areas of the body.
- As heart failure worsens, edema accumulates in the abdominal organs and other extremities.

### Distribution

- **Bilateral** — suggests pulmonary hypertension, heart failure, or venous insufficiency.
- **Unilateral** — normally caused by venous obstruction or deep vein thrombosis.
- **Pedal** — a manifestation of chronic lung disease.

---

## Topic 10: Pain Level

Everyone feels pain differently. Pain should be compared to the worst pain the patient has ever experienced in their life.

Questions to consider:

- What makes the pain better or worse?
- Is the pain gradual or sudden?

### Wong-Baker Faces Scale

A 0 to 10 scale used to grade a patient's pain — especially important for patients who can't communicate clearly.

- **0** = smiling and pain free.
- **10** = weeping in agony.

---

## Topic 11: Levels of Consciousness (LOC)

Know the language, because you will chart it:

- **Sensorium** (baseline)
- **Confused**
- **Delirious**
- **Lethargic**
- **Obtunded**
- **Stuporous**
- **Comatose**

---

## Topic 12: Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

Observation begins the moment you first see the patient and continues throughout the exam. GCS is graded in three categories.

- **Eye opening response** — 1 to 4 points
- **Verbal response** — 1 to 5 points
- **Motor response** — 1 to 6 points

### Eye Opening (E)

- **E4 — Spontaneous:** eyes are open and focused; patient can recognize you and follow eye movements.
- **E3 — To speech/voice:** patient opens eyes when spoken to or when directed to do so.
- **E2 — To pain:** patient opens eyes when given some sort of painful stimuli.
- **E1 — None.**

### Verbal (V)

- **V5 — Oriented:** patient can talk and answer questions about person, place, time, and the event that led to the current condition.
- **V4 — Confused:** patient can talk and speak coherently, but is not entirely oriented to person, place, time, and event.
- **V3 — Inappropriate words:** patient answers with an inappropriate response, or excessive use of profanity not associated with anger.
- **V2 — Incomprehensible words:** unintelligible words or sounds.
- **V1 — None.**

### Motor (M) — from the source

- **M3 — Flexion (decorticate posturing):** abnormal posturing usually caused by severe brain trauma; body curls into a protective posture by flexing the arms into the chest.
- **M2 — Extension (decerebrate posturing):** body is abnormally extended; arms and legs may be extended and very rigid or difficult to move.
- **M1 — None.**

*(Motor is scored 1 to 6 overall.)*

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Proxemic zones:** Social 4–12 ft (rapport), Personal 18 in–4 ft (sensitive info), Intimate 0–18 in (exam/treatment after rapport + permission).
- **Nonverbal cues:** body movement, facial expression, touch, eye movement.
- **OLDCART:** Onset, Location, Duration, Characteristics, Associated manifestations, Relieving factors, Treatment.
- **Cough reflex site:** pharynx, larynx, large bronchi; most common pulmonary symptom.
- **Sputum vs phlegm:** phlegm = not contaminated by oral secretions.
- **Sputum colors:** clear/egg white normal · white-gray mucoid (emphysema, TB, early chronic bronchitis, asthma) · green/rusty/yellow purulent (infection — H. influenzae, strep, staph) · mucopurulent (infection, CF) · brown (smoker, old blood) · frothy pink (pulmonary edema) · blood-streaked/hemoptysis (bronchogenic carcinoma, TB).
- **Sputum consistency:** thin, thick, viscous, tenacious, frothy.
- **Fetid sputum:** lung abscess, aspiration, anaerobic infection, bronchiectasis.
- **Sputum quantity:** scant, small, moderate, large, copious.
- **Dyspnea = subjective;** ADLs = "DEATH" (Dressing, Eating, Ambulating, Transferring/toileting, Hygiene).
- **Dyspnea scales:** Modified Borg 0–10 (post-activity), ATS SOB 0–4 (ADLs), Dyspnea-12 (asthma).
- **Pleuritic pain:** sharp, stabbing, inspiratory (most common pulmonary chest pain).
- **Chest wall pain:** dull, constant ache.
- **Edema bilateral** = pulmonary HTN, HF, venous insufficiency · **unilateral** = venous obstruction / DVT · **pedal** = chronic lung disease.
- **Wong-Baker:** 0–10, 0 = smiling/pain free, 10 = weeping in agony; especially for patients who can't communicate clearly.
- **LOC ladder (alert → out):** Sensorium → Confused → Delirious → Lethargic → Obtunded → Stuporous → Comatose.
- **GCS:** Eye 1–4, Verbal 1–5, Motor 1–6.
- **Decorticate (M3):** flexion, arms to chest (protective). **Decerebrate (M2):** extension, rigid — deeper/worse lesion.
