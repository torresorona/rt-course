> Walk through each unit in the order below, with airtime roughly proportional to the amount of content in each unit:
>
> 1. **Percussion — definition and chest technique** — percussion = tapping on a surface to evaluate the underlying structures; finger placed firmly against the body, struck with a fingertip from the other hand. Chest percussion produces both a sound AND a palpable vibration. Technique: middle finger of the non-dominant hand placed firmly against the chest wall PARALLEL to the ribs, palm and other fingers held off the chest; strike with the tip of the right middle finger or lateral aspect of the right thumb near the base of the terminal phalanx, quick sharp blow. Motion is generated at the WRIST — not the elbow or shoulder. Keep the stationary finger firm against the wall; strike and immediately withdraw — the two fingers should be in contact for ONLY an instant.
> 2. **Lung percussion sounds** — five sounds, each with its description and cause: RESONANT (normal air-filled lung); FLAT (soft, high-pitched, short duration — atelectasis); DULL (medium intensity/pitch/duration — heard over fluid-filled organs like heart or liver; pleural effusion or pneumonia); HYPERRESONANT (very loud, lower in pitch, longer in duration — emphysematous lung, pneumothorax); TYMPANIC (loud and drum-like with a high pitch — gastric bubble, air-filled stomach).
> 3. **Auscultation overview** — process of listening to sounds, covering lung, voice, and heart. Most commonly used physical assessment technique of the respiratory and cardiovascular system.
> 4. **Lung auscultation — setup** — patient sitting upright, breathing a little deeper through an open mouth if possible; diaphragm placed directly on skin (hospital gown or thin shirt is acceptable); stethoscope tubing should not touch anything (extraneous noise); auscultate each position BILATERALLY using a systematic method.
> 5. **Analyzing breath sounds** — six things to note: identify the breath sound; identify intensity/loudness (mild, moderate, severe); identify WHEN during the respiratory cycle (inspiratory, expiratory, or both); location (bilateral, anterior vs posterior, upper vs lower); do the sounds change with therapy; do the sounds change with coughing.
> 6. **Normal breath sounds** — vesicular (over most of the lung fields); bronchovesicular (over the mainstem bronchus); bronchial/tracheal (only over the trachea).
> 7. **Adventitious breath sounds — definition** — added irregular sounds produced within a normal sound.
> 8. **Crackles (rales)** — three intensities with very specific recommended interventions (memorize the pairing): COARSE rales → patient needs SUCTIONING; MEDIUM rales → recommend CHEST PHYSICAL THERAPY; FINE rales → recommend IPPB, HEART DRUGS, DIURETICS, and OXYGEN.
> 9. **Wheezes** — UNILATERAL "localized" wheezing suggests TUMORS or FOREIGN BODY ASPIRATION. CARDIAC ASTHMA = wheezing and dyspnea in patients with heart failure (CHF) in the ABSENCE of asthma or COPD; caused by VASCULAR CONGESTION and PERIVASCULAR EDEMA from FLUID OVERLOAD.
> 10. **Voice auscultation — concept** — voice sounds through normal air-filled lung are muffled and unclear because vibrations travel poorly through air; vocal resonance is INCREASED when sound travels through a solid or liquid — so voice auscultation is most helpful in consolidation, pneumonia, atelectasis, pleural effusion, tumor, or abscess.
> 11. **Egophony** — abnormally enhanced vocal resonance with a high-pitched, nasal quality; indicates consolidation, pleural effusion, or abscess. Patient repeats "E"; egophony exists when the letter is heard as a nasal "A" through the stethoscope.
> 12. **Bronchophony** — abnormally LOUD and CLEAR transmission of voice sounds through an area of INCREASED DENSITY. Patient repeats "99" while you auscultate the thorax.
> 13. **Whispered pectoriloquy** — enhanced voice heard through the chest wall; MORE SENSITIVE than bronchophony. Patient whispers "one, two, three"; whispered words normally sound faint and indistinct — if heard loudly and distinctly, suspect consolidation.
> 14. **Heart auscultation — basics** — listening to the sounds of the heart; normal heart sounds are caused by the CLOSURE OF THE VALVES; patient sits upright.
> 15. **Four auscultation regions** (memorize each): AORTIC = 2nd–3rd intercostal space, RIGHT sternal border; PULMONIC = 2nd–3rd intercostal space, LEFT sternal border; TRICUSPID = 3rd, 4th, 5th, and 6th intercostal spaces, LEFT sternal border; MITRAL = near the apex, 5th–6th intercostal space, MID-CLAVICULAR line.
> 16. **S1 and S2** — S1 ("lub") = first heart sound, occurs at the BEGINNING of ventricular contraction; closure of the atrioventricular valves — MITRAL and TRICUSPID. S2 ("dub") = second heart sound, occurs when SYSTOLE ENDS; closure of the AORTIC and PULMONIC valves.
> 17. **S3 and S4** — extra sounds from unusual blood flow mechanisms; BOTH heard at the LEFT 5TH INTERCOSTAL SPACE, MID-CLAVICULAR LINE. S3 = rapid ventricular filling; may suggest CHF. S4 = most often caused by a STIFF VENTRICLE — hypertension or AFTER a myocardial infarction.
> 18. **Murmurs** — extra sound heard in conjunction with S1 and S2; caused by blood REGURGITATING into the chamber it came from. Causes: valvular dysfunction; blood pushed through an abnormal opening such as ATRIAL SEPTAL DEFECT (ASD) or PATENT DUCTUS ARTERIOSUS (PDA).
> 19. **Pulse oximetry — indications** — monitor adequacy of ARTERIAL OXYHEMOGLOBIN SATURATION; quantify the patient's response to therapeutic intervention and diagnostic procedures; comply with mandated regulations.
> 20. **Contraindications & precautions** — no significant contraindications noted (an ABG may be more properly warranted for additional information); precautions = false-negatives or false-positives.
> 21. **SpO₂ vs SaO₂** — SpO₂ = pulse oximetry; NOT to be confused with SaO₂, which is measured with HEMOXIMETRY. Measurement of blood Hgb saturations using SPECTROPHOTOMETRY. Portable, noninvasive.
> 22. **How it works** — operates using the LAMBERT-BEER LAW (measures light absorbed and transmitted by a substance to identify its presence and determine its concentration); uses light to detect tiny volume changes in living tissue during pulsatile blood flow; uses TWO WAVELENGTHS of light — one RED and one INFRARED; measures transmission through living tissue such as a FINGER or EARLOBE.
> 23. **Sensor** — two sides: one emits the red and infrared beams; the other measures via a PHOTODETECTOR. The output signal is transferred to produce the display on the monitor.
> 24. **Signal components** — BASELINE = stable absorbance of the tissue bed; PEAK = pulse component caused by intermittent arterial flow through the tissues.
> 25. **Procedure** — varies depending on site and probe; place sensor on the selected site; observe the waveform if available.
> 26. **Documentation** — date; time; results for SpO₂; patient position; probe type and site placement; FiO₂ being provided to the patient.

## Topic 1: Percussion — Definition

Percussion is the act of **tapping on a surface to evaluate the underlying structures**. It is done by placing a finger firmly against a body part and striking that finger with a fingertip from the other hand.

---

## Topic 2: Chest Percussion

Chest percussion produces a **sound and a palpable vibration** that are useful for evaluating the underlying lung tissue.

### Technique

- Place the **middle finger of the non-dominant hand** firmly against the chest wall, **parallel to the ribs**, with the palm and other fingers held off the chest.
- The tip of the **middle finger on the right hand**, or the **lateral aspect of the right thumb**, strikes the finger against the chest near the base of the terminal phalanx with a **quick, sharp blow**.
- Movement of the striking hand should be generated at the **wrist** — not the elbow or shoulder.
- The percussion note is clearest if the examiner keeps the finger firmly against the chest wall, strikes it, and immediately withdraws.
- The two fingers should be in contact for only an instant.

---

## Topic 3: Lung Percussion Sounds

- **Resonant** — normal air-filled lung.
- **Flat** — soft, high-pitched, short duration; associated with **atelectasis**.
- **Dull** — medium intensity, pitch, and duration; heard over **fluid-filled organs such as heart or liver**; also heard with **pleural effusion or pneumonia**.
- **Hyperresonant** — very loud, lower in pitch, longer in duration; associated with **emphysematous lung** and **pneumothorax**.
- **Tympanic** — loud and drum-like with a high pitch; heard over a **gastric bubble** or **air-filled stomach**.

---

## Topic 4: Auscultation — Overview

Auscultation covers **lung**, **voice**, and **heart** auscultation. Lung auscultation is the most commonly used physical assessment technique of the respiratory and cardiovascular system.

---

## Topic 5: Lung Auscultation — Setup

- Patient should be sitting upright, breathing a little deeper through an open mouth (if possible).
- Diaphragm should be placed directly on skin. A hospital gown or thin shirt is acceptable.
- Stethoscope tubing should not touch anything — it produces extraneous noises.
- Auscultate each position bilaterally and utilize a systematic method.

---

## Topic 6: Analyzing Breath Sounds

1. Identify the breath sound.
2. Identify the intensity or loudness — mild, moderate, severe.
3. Identify when during the respiratory cycle it is heard — inspiratory, expiratory, or both.
4. Location — bilateral, anterior vs posterior, upper vs lower.
5. Do the breath sounds change with therapy?
6. Do the breath sounds change with coughing?

---

## Topic 7: Normal Breath Sounds

- **Vesicular** — heard over most of the lung fields.
- **Bronchovesicular** — heard over the mainstem bronchus.
- **Bronchial / Tracheal** — heard only over the trachea.

---

## Topic 8: Adventitious Breath Sounds — Definition

Adventitious breath sounds are **added, irregular sounds produced within a normal sound**.

---

## Topic 9: Crackles (Rales)

- **Coarse rales** — patient needs **suctioning**.
- **Medium rales** — recommend **chest physical therapy**.
- **Fine rales** — recommend **IPPB, heart drugs, diuretics, and oxygen**.

---

## Topic 10: Wheezes

- **Unilateral "localized" wheezing** — tumors; foreign body aspiration.
- **Cardiac asthma** — wheezing and dyspnea in patients with heart failure (CHF) in the absence of asthma or COPD; caused by vascular congestion and perivascular edema from fluid overload.

---

## Topic 11: Voice Auscultation — Concept

Auscultation of voice sounds through a normal air-filled lung produces a muffled, unclear sound because sound vibrations travel poorly through air. **Vocal resonance is increased when sound travels through a solid or liquid** — as with consolidation, pneumonia, atelectasis, pleural effusion, tumor, or abscess.

---

## Topic 12: Egophony

Abnormally enhanced vocal resonance with a **high-pitched, nasal quality**. Indicates an area of consolidation, pleural effusion, or abscess.

**How to assess:** auscultate the chest while the patient repeats "E." Egophony exists when this letter is heard as a nasal "A" through the stethoscope.

---

## Topic 13: Bronchophony

Abnormally **loud and clear** transmission of voice sounds through an area of increased density.

**How to assess:** the patient repeats the number "99" while auscultating the thorax.

---

## Topic 14: Whispered Pectoriloquy

Enhanced voice heard through the chest wall — **more sensitive than bronchophony**.

**How to assess:** have the patient whisper "one, two, three." Whispered words normally sound faint and indistinct. If they are heard loudly and distinctly, suspect consolidation of lung tissue.

---

## Topic 15: Heart Auscultation — Basics

The process of listening to sounds of the heart. **Normal heart sounds are caused by the closure of the valves**. Have the patient sit in an upright position when auscultating.

---

## Topic 16: Heart Regions

- **Aortic region** — between the 2nd and 3rd intercostal spaces at the right sternal border.
- **Pulmonic region** — between the 2nd and 3rd intercostal spaces at the left sternal border.
- **Tricuspid region** — between the 3rd, 4th, 5th, and 6th intercostal spaces at the left sternal border.
- **Mitral region** — near the apex of the heart between the 5th and 6th intercostal spaces in the mid-clavicular line.

---

## Topic 17: S1 and S2

- **S1 ("lub")** — first heart sound. Occurs at the **beginning of ventricular contraction**. Closure of the **atrioventricular valves** — mitral and tricuspid.
- **S2 ("dub")** — second heart sound. Occurs when **systole ends**. Closure of the **aortic and pulmonic valves**.

---

## Topic 18: S3 and S4

Both are extra sounds generated by unusual blood flow mechanisms. Both are heard at the **left 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line**.

- **S3** — results from **rapid ventricular filling**. May suggest **Congestive Heart Failure**.
- **S4** — caused most often by a **stiff ventricle** such as in **hypertension** or **after a myocardial infarction**.

---

## Topic 19: Murmurs

Extra sound heard **in conjunction with S1 and S2**. Caused by **blood regurgitating into the chamber it came from**.

**Causes:**

- Valvular dysfunction.
- Blood pushed through an abnormal opening — **atrial septal defect (ASD)** or **patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)**.

---

## Topic 20: Pulse Oximetry — Indications

- Monitor adequacy of arterial oxyhemoglobin saturation.
- Quantify the patient's response to therapeutic interventions and diagnostic procedures.
- Comply with mandated regulations.

---

## Topic 21: Contraindications & Precautions

- No significant contraindications noted. An **ABG** may be more properly warranted for additional information.
- **Precautions:** false-negatives or false-positives.

---

## Topic 22: SpO₂ vs SaO₂

- **SpO₂** refers to pulse oximetry.
- **SaO₂** is measured with **hemoximetry** — not the same thing.
- Measurement of blood Hgb saturations using **spectrophotometry**.
- Portable, **noninvasive** device.

---

## Topic 23: How Pulse Oximetry Works

- Operates using the **Lambert-Beer law** — measures light absorbed and transmitted by a substance to identify its presence and determine its concentration.
- Uses light to detect tiny volume changes that occur in living tissue during pulsatile blood flow.
- Uses **two wavelengths of light** — one **red** and one **infrared**.
- Measures transmission through **living tissue** such as a **finger** or **earlobe**.

---

## Topic 24: Sensor & Signal

- Sensor has two sides — one emits the red and infrared beams; the other measures via a **photodetector**.
- The output signal is transferred to produce the display on the monitor.
- **Baseline** — stable absorbance of the tissue bed.
- **Peak** — pulse component caused by intermittent arterial flow through the tissues.

---

## Topic 25: Procedure

- Varies depending on site and probe.
- Place the sensor on the selected site.
- Observe the waveform (if available).

---

## Topic 26: Documentation

Record:

- Date
- Time
- Results for SpO₂
- Patient position
- Probe type and site placement
- FiO₂ being provided to the patient
