# Patient History & Laboratory Values — Complete Study Guide
## Respiratory Therapy Course — Patient Assessment, Lesson 1

---

## Topic 1: Patient Assessment Overview

Patient assessment is the systematic review of the patient's history and data before and during care. In this lesson we cover three pillars of that review: the patient history, the chart review, and the laboratory results.

---

## Topic 2: Patient History — The Four Components

The patient history is made up of four components: Background Information, Past Medical History, Family Medical History, and Occupational / Environmental History.

### Background Information

Background information explains who the patient is and what type of disease they are likely to develop. It gives information about previous illnesses and care. It also includes factors that may affect care, such as cultural or religious factors. Finally, it provides screening information.

### Past Medical History

Past medical history may include illnesses since birth, surgeries and hospitalizations, allergies, current medications, and drug and smoking history. For drug and smoking history specifically, ask the patient: what age they started, what type of substance, how much they use, and if they quit, how long ago they quit.

### Family Medical History

The purpose of the family medical history is to learn the health status of the patient's blood relatives. It is a record of diseases with hereditary links — a classic example is cystic fibrosis. It includes parents, siblings, grandparents, and aunts and uncles.

We are specifically looking for possible hereditary-linked pulmonary and cardiovascular diseases such as asthma, lung cancer, cystic fibrosis, emphysema, neuromuscular disorders, and sleep disturbances.

### Occupational and Environmental History

This part of the history gathers information on possible work-related or geographically related exposures, such as asbestos, coal, dusts, fumes, or gases. Family members can also be exposed, so it is important to ask the patient if their family members worked in areas with increased risk of exposure.

---

## Topic 3: Chart Review

The chart review consists of reviewing five types of notes and orders in the patient's chart: the Admission Note, Progress Notes, Physician Orders, Clinical Notes, and any DNR, DNI, or CMO orders.

### Admission Note

The admission note is written by the admitting physician and contains important facts pertaining to the reason the patient has been admitted to the hospital. It provides the patient's baseline status.

### Progress Note

Progress notes are daily annotations by the attending physician or nurse. They detail the patient's progress toward the treatment goals.

### Physician Orders

Physician orders are the techniques and treatments a physician believes are needed to provide the best care for the patient.

### Clinical Note

The clinical note is the assessment performed by the physician's team.

### DNR, DNI, and CMO Orders

For code status orders — Do Not Resuscitate, Do Not Intubate, and Comfort Measures Only — always verify the order in the patient chart, ensure the patient wristband is in place, and discuss with the care team.

## Topic 4: Laboratory Values — The Four Categories

There are four main categories of lab values: the Complete Blood Count (CBC), the Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP), Microbiology Tests, and Coagulation Tests.

---

## Topic 5: Complete Blood Count (CBC)

The CBC gives an overall quantity assessment of White Blood Cells, Hemoglobin, Hematocrit, Red Blood Cells, and Platelets.

### White Blood Cells (WBC)

The primary role of white blood cells is fighting infections. The normal range for total WBC is 4,000 to 11,000.

There are five types of white blood cells:

- **Neutrophils** are the first line of defense against microbial infections. They come in two forms. **Bands** are immature cells, making up normally 4 percent of WBC, and they are increased with bacterial infections. **Segmented** neutrophils are the mature cells, normally 60 percent of WBC, and they are decreased with bacterial infections.
- **Eosinophils** are involved in immune system regulation, control of parasitic infections, and allergic reactions. They are normally around 2 percent. Eosinophils are elevated with allergic reactions and are associated with asthma.
- **Basophils** are involved in immune system regulation, control of parasitic infections, and allergic reactions. Normal range is 0.4 to 1 percent.
- **Lymphocytes**, which include T cells and B cells, are important in the body's defense against foreign microorganisms and cells. Normal range is 25 to 35 percent.
- **Monocytes** are responsible for phagocytosis — the ingestion of organisms invading the body. Normal range is 4 to 6 percent. Elevation is associated with tuberculosis.

Two key WBC abnormalities to know:

- **Leukocytosis** is increased WBC. It is a sign of an inflammatory response, most commonly the result of a significant infection. When WBC is significantly increased — greater than 16,000 — it is an indication for the patient to be on antibiotics.
- **Leukopenia** is decreased WBC. It means the patient is immunosuppressed and has an increased risk of infection.

### Red Blood Cells (RBC)

The normal red blood cell count is 4 to 6 million per cubic millimeter. Red blood cells supply oxygen to the tissues.

- **Anemia** is decreased RBC. It occurs with blood loss — hemorrhage — or when production is inadequate. Oxygen-carrying capability is reduced, and the patient is more likely to experience tissue hypoxia.
- **Polycythemia** is increased RBC. The body responds to less oxygen available for tissues by releasing erythropoietin, also called EPO. This stimulates bone marrow to make more RBCs to offset low oxygen levels. Polycythemia increases the viscosity of the blood. It is seen in patients with chronic tissue hypoxemia, such as COPD, and in people who live at high altitudes.

### Hemoglobin (Hgb)

Hemoglobin carries oxygen — specifically, 1.34 milliliters of oxygen per gram of hemoglobin. The normal range for males is 13.5 to 15.5 grams per deciliter. The normal range for females is 12.5 to 14.5 grams per deciliter.

### Hematocrit (Hct)

Hematocrit is the portion of whole blood that is red blood cells. It reflects the patient's hydration status. The normal value for males is 42 to 52 percent. The normal value for females is 37 to 48 percent.

### Platelets

- **Thrombocytosis** is a high platelet count — greater than 400,000 — and it means the patient has an increased tendency to form blood clots, also called thromboses.
- **Thrombocytopenia** is a low platelet count — less than 150,000 — and it means the patient is likely to have bleeding problems.

## Topic 6: Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP)

The BMP is the most-ordered lab and is the standard lab to assess overall health. It tests important electrolytes, fasting glucose level, and two renal function tests.

### Sodium (Na+)

The normal range for sodium is 135 to 145 millimoles per liter. Sodium is the primary extracellular cation. It is crucial to maintain fluid balance and nerve impulse conduction.

- **Hypernatremia** is increased sodium. It happens when the body loses a large amount of water compared to the amount of sodium lost. It is caused by profuse sweating, diarrhea, and renal disease.
- **Hyponatremia** is decreased sodium. It is caused by excess water intake or fluid retention — drinking too much water, excessive ADH secretion, severe vomiting or diarrhea, congestive heart failure (CHF), and renal failure.

### Potassium (K+)

Normal range is 3.5 to 5.0 millimoles per liter. Potassium is the primary intracellular cation. It is crucial to maintain normal heart and kidney function and acid-base balance.

- **Hyperkalemia** is high potassium. It is caused by metabolic acidosis and kidney failure.
- **Hypokalemia** is low potassium. It is caused by metabolic alkalosis and excessive excretion.

### Chloride (Cl-)

Normal range is 98 to 106 milliequivalents per liter. Chloride is the primary extracellular anion. It is crucial for maintaining acid-base balance.

- **Hyperchloremia** is increased chloride. It is caused by excessive chloride administration, metabolic acidosis, and diabetes insipidus.
- **Hypochloremia** is decreased chloride. It is caused by prolonged severe vomiting, metabolic alkalosis, and severe burns.

### Total CO2 (TCO2)

Normal range is 22 to 29 milliequivalents per liter.

- **Decreased TCO2** is caused by metabolic acidosis, hyperventilation, and severe diarrhea.
- **Increased TCO2** is caused by ventilatory failure.

### Glucose

Normal range is 70 to 105 milligrams per deciliter. Glucose is the primary energy source for body tissues and blood cells.

- **Hyperglycemia** is increased glucose. It is mostly caused by type I or type II diabetes. It can also be caused by medications, acute stress, and pancreatic dysfunction.
- **Hypoglycemia** is decreased glucose. It is a relatively uncommon problem. It is caused by excessive insulin administration or dietary intake.

### Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN)

Normal range is 7 to 20 milligrams per deciliter.

- **Increased BUN** is caused by acute kidney disease and chronic renal failure.
- **Decreased BUN** is caused by liver disease and malnutrition.

### Creatinine

Normal range is 0.7 to 1.3 milligrams per deciliter. Both BUN and creatinine are used to assess renal function: elevation indicates kidney disease, and decreased values indicate liver disease.

## Topic 7: Microbiology Tests

There are three microbiology tests to know: the Sputum Gram Stain, the Sputum Culture, and the Acid Fast test.

### Sputum Gram Stain

The sputum gram stain is indicated for patients suspected to have an infection in the lungs or airway. The purpose is to determine the specific microorganism causing the infection in order to choose the best antibiotic for the patient.

The procedure is performed in a laboratory. A staining solution is applied, and the sample is analyzed under a microscope to verify the sample's quality. After verification, the gram stain reaction is introduced.

A **Gram positive** result identifies organisms like Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Diplococcus, and Pneumococcus. Gram positive infections are treated with Penicillins.

A **Gram negative** result identifies organisms like Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Haemophilus influenzae, and Escherichia coli. Gram negative infections are treated with Streptomycin, Gentamycin, or Colimycin.

### Sputum Culture

The sputum culture is done after the sputum sample is found to be adequate. Once the organism matures, it is examined microscopically to determine the exact sensitivity and proper antibiotic therapy. Results typically come back within 24 to 48 hours.

### Acid Fast

The acid fast test is a rapid and effective method for determining tuberculosis. The process is to perform a gram stain followed by an acid wash. If the acid wash does not weaken the cell wall sufficiently enough to remove the color dye, the organism is considered an acid-fast bacterium.

## Topic 8: Coagulation Testing

Coagulation testing helps diagnose the cause of unexplained bleeding or inappropriate blood clots. It is also used to monitor patients on blood thinners. It is measured by puncturing the skin and seeing how long it takes to stop bleeding.

### Prothrombin Time (PT)

PT measures how much time it takes for your blood plasma to clot. The normal value is 12 to 15 seconds. It is indicated for patients on the blood thinner Warfarin, which is also known by the brand name Coumadin.

### International Normalized Ratio (INR)

INR is the ratio of a patient's PT and the normal mean PT. The normal value is 0.8 to 1.1. If INR is too high, the patient may be in danger of bleeding out. If INR is too low, the patient's blood clots very fast.

### Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (APTT)

The normal value for APTT is 23 to 32 seconds. It is indicated for patients on Heparin.

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Four parts of patient history**: Background, Past Medical, Family Medical, Occupational/Environmental
- **Chart review pieces**: Admission Note, Progress Notes, Physician Orders, Clinical Notes, DNR/DNI/CMO
- **Four lab categories**: CBC, BMP, Microbiology, Coagulation
- **WBC normal**: 4,000–11,000 — Leukocytosis (↑, >16,000 = antibiotics indicated), Leukopenia (↓, immunosuppressed)
- **Eosinophils ↑** = asthma / allergy
- **Monocytes ↑** = tuberculosis
- **RBC normal**: 4–6 mill/mm³ — Anemia (↓, hypoxia risk), Polycythemia (↑, COPD / high altitude, EPO-driven, thick blood)
- **Hgb**: 1.34 mL O₂ per gram — Male 13.5–15.5, Female 12.5–14.5 g/dL
- **Hct** (hydration): Male 42–52%, Female 37–48%
- **Platelets**: Thrombocytosis >400k (clots), Thrombocytopenia <150k (bleeds)
- **Na⁺ 135–145** (extracellular cation), **K⁺ 3.5–5.0** (intracellular cation), **Cl⁻ 98–106** (extracellular anion), **TCO₂ 22–29**, **Glucose 70–105**, **BUN 7–20**, **Creatinine 0.7–1.3**
- **BUN/Creatinine**: ↑ = kidney, ↓ = liver
- **Gram positive** (Staph, Strep, Diplo, Pneumo) → Penicillins
- **Gram negative** (Pseudomonas, H. influenzae, E. coli) → Streptomycin / Gentamycin / Colimycin
- **Acid Fast** = TB
- **PT / Warfarin (Coumadin)** — 12–15 sec
- **INR** — 0.8–1.1
- **APTT / Heparin** — 23–32 sec
