# Cardiac Muscle, Conduction System, and Cardiac Cycle — Complete Study Guide

## Cardiovascular Anatomy & Physiology

---

## Topic 1: Cardiac Muscle Histology

Cardiac muscle fibers are different from skeletal muscle fibers. They're shorter in length, less circular in cross-section, and they branch — giving them that characteristic stair-step appearance. A typical cardiac muscle fiber is about 50 to 100 micrometers long and about 14 micrometers in diameter.

The key feature here is gap junctions. These are protein channels that connect adjacent cardiac muscle cells, allowing ions to pass directly from one cell to the next. This allows the entire myocardium to contract as a single, coordinated unit.

---

## Topic 2: The Action Potential — Key Terms

Let's define some terms before diving into the action potential itself.

Polarized means the inside of the cell is more negative than the outside. Depolarization is the movement of charged particles across the cell membrane that makes the inside positive. Repolarization is the movement of ions that restores the negative charge inside the cell.

The action potential is caused by the interaction of three ions: potassium or K+, calcium or Ca2+, and sodium or Na+.

In the polarized or resting state, the inside of the cell is negatively charged with potassium inside. The outside is positively charged with sodium. The resting membrane potential — RMP — is the electrical charge difference across the fibers. It sits at about -90 millivolts.

The Na+/K+ pump establishes the concentration gradients: high Na+ outside the cell, high K+ inside. Both ions diffuse along their gradients — K+ diffuses out, Na+ diffuses in. But for every one Na+ that diffuses in, about 50 to 75 K+ ions diffuse out. This creates a deficiency of positive cations inside the cell, establishing the resting membrane potential.

## Topic 3: The Five Phases of the Action Potential

Phase 0 is depolarization. It starts when the threshold of -70 millivolts is reached. Na+ moves rapidly into the cell through voltage-gated fast Na+ channels, causing the spike on the action potential graph. Ca2+ moves in slowly through Ca2+ channels — this causes the heart to contract. K+ is leaving the cell.

Phase 1 is early repolarization. Na+ channels partially close, slowing Na+ inflow. Voltage-gated K+ channels open. The decrease in positive ions creates a negative deflection.

Phase 2 is the plateau phase. This is a sustained contraction. Ca2+ slowly enters through Ca2+ channels, trying to push the graph positive. K+ continues to slowly leave through K+ channels, trying to push the graph negative. These opposing forces create the plateau.

Phase 3 is final repolarization. K+ flows out rapidly through K+ channels. Na+ and Ca2+ channels close, stopping entry of those ions.

Phase 4 is the resting or polarized state. The resting membrane potential returns to -90 millivolts. Voltage-sensitive ion channels return to their pre-depolarized state. Excess Na+ that entered during depolarization is pumped back out, and the K+ that left during repolarization is pumped back in. Additional pumps remove the extra Ca2+ from inside the cell.

---

## Topic 4: Refractory Periods

The absolute refractory period — ARP — is when the cell will not respond to any further stimulation. It corresponds to the time needed for Na+ and Ca2+ channels to reopen. It includes phases 0, 1, 2, and part of phase 3.

The relative refractory period — RRP — is when some cells have repolarized to threshold and can respond to a stronger-than-normal stimulus.

The effective refractory period — ERP — includes the ARP and the first half of the RRP. Effective here means a conducted action potential cannot be generated because there's not enough inward current.

The supranormal period — SNP — extends from the end of phase 3 to the beginning of phase 4. A weaker-than-normal stimulus can cause depolarization during this period. This is when dysrhythmias can develop because the cell is more excitable than normal. It can cause R on T phenomenon.

## Topic 5: Conduction System Terminology

Automaticity is the ability of SA node cells to generate an action potential without being stimulated. Excitability is the ability to reach threshold potential and respond to a stimulus — also called irritability. Conductivity is the ability to receive an impulse and conduct it to an adjoining cell. Contractility is the ability of myocardial cells to shorten and cause contraction in response to electrical stimulus.

## Topic 6: The Cardiac Conduction System

The conduction system is made of autorhythmic fibers that are self-excitable. They repeatedly generate action potentials that trigger heart contractions. They act as pacemakers and form the conduction pathway that ensures coordinated contraction of all four chambers.

The sinoatrial node — the SA node — is located in the right atrial wall just inferior and lateral to the opening of the superior vena cava. It doesn't have a stable resting potential — it repeatedly depolarizes to threshold spontaneously. This is called the pacemaker potential. Its intrinsic rate is 60 to 100 beats per minute, making it the lead pacemaker.

From the SA node, the impulse travels through Bachmann's Bundle to the left atrium, causing both atria to contract simultaneously. It also travels through the anterior, middle, and posterior internodal tracts to the AV node.

The atrioventricular node — the AV node — is located in the interatrial septum just anterior to the opening of the coronary sinus. Its job is to delay the impulse. This allows the atria to fully empty blood into the ventricles, increasing stroke volume. Its intrinsic rate is 40 to 60 beats per minute.

The AV node has three functional regions. The atrionodal or AN region is the transitional zone. The nodal or N region is the midportion. The nodal-His or NH region is the lower region where fibers merge with the Bundle of His.

The AV bundle or Bundle of His is the only site where action potentials can conduct from the atria to the ventricles. It inserts into the fibrous skeleton and conducts impulses to the right and left bundle branches.

The right and left bundle branches extend through the interventricular septum toward the apex.

The Purkinje fibers rapidly conduct the action potential from the apex upward to the ventricular myocardium. This ensures that contraction begins at the apex and moves upward, squeezing blood toward the great vessels. Their intrinsic rate is 20 to 40 beats per minute.

## Topic 7: The Cardiac Cycle

One cardiac cycle includes the systolic and diastolic phases of both the atria and ventricles. At a heart rate of 75 beats per minute, one cycle lasts 0.8 seconds.

Let's define some key terms. EDV is end-diastolic volume — about 130 milliliters. SV is stroke volume — about 70 milliliters. ESV is end-systolic volume — about 60 milliliters. Stroke volume equals EDV minus ESV.

Atrial systole lasts about 0.1 seconds. SA node depolarization causes atrial depolarization, marked by the P wave on the ECG. The atria contract, forcing blood through the open AV valves into the ventricles. Atrial systole contributes a final 25 milliliters to the ventricular volume. At the end, each ventricle has about 130 milliliters of blood — that's the EDV.

Ventricular systole lasts about 0.3 seconds. AV node depolarization causes ventricular depolarization, marked by the QRS complex. The ventricles contract, pressure rises, and the AV valves close. For about 0.05 seconds, all four valves are closed — this is isovolumetric contraction. The muscle fibers are contracting but not shortening, and ventricular blood volume stays the same.

When left ventricular pressure rises above aortic pressure — about 80 mmHg — and right ventricular pressure rises above pulmonary trunk pressure — about 20 mmHg — the SL valves open. Ventricular ejection begins. The left ventricle reaches about 120 mmHg and the right ventricle reaches about 25 to 30 mmHg. Each ventricle ejects about 70 milliliters — that's the stroke volume — into the aorta and pulmonary trunk. The 60 milliliters remaining is the ESV. The T wave marks ventricular repolarization.

Ventricular diastole lasts about 0.4 seconds. The ventricles relax and pressure falls. Blood in the aorta and pulmonary trunk starts flowing backward, closing the SL valves. The aortic valve closes at about 100 mmHg. The dicrotic notch represents the rebound of blood off the closed aortic valve cusps.

There's a brief period when all four valves are closed again — isovolumetric relaxation. When ventricular pressure drops below atrial pressure, the AV valves open and ventricular filling starts. The majority of blood that has been filling the atria rushes in rapidly. The ventricles end up about three-quarters full by the end of the relaxation period.

## Topic 8: Cardiac Output

Cardiac output is the volume of blood ejected from the ventricle per minute. The formula is simple: CO equals stroke volume times heart rate.

In a typical resting adult male with a stroke volume of 70 milliliters per beat and a heart rate of 75 beats per minute, cardiac output is 5,250 milliliters per minute, or 5.25 liters per minute. The average male has about 5 liters of blood, so during rest the entire blood volume flows through each minute.

Cardiac reserve is the difference between maximum cardiac output and resting cardiac output. The average person has a cardiac reserve of four to five times the resting value. Top endurance athletes may have seven to eight times. People with severe heart disease may have little to no cardiac reserve.

## Topic 9: Regulation of Stroke Volume

Three factors regulate stroke volume.

Preload is the degree of stretch on the heart before contracting — at the end of diastole. The Frank-Starling law of the heart says the greater the stretch, the larger the volume, and the stronger the contraction. Preload and EDV are proportional. Two key factors determine EDV: the duration of ventricular diastole and the venous return. Increased venous return means increased EDV.

Contractility is the forcefulness of contraction of individual ventricular muscle fibers. Positive inotropic agents increase contractility by promoting calcium inflow during the action potential. Negative inotropic agents — like calcium channel blockers — decrease contractility.

Afterload is the pressure that must be exceeded before ejection can occur. The right ventricle overcomes pulmonary artery pressure — about 20 mmHg — to open the pulmonic valve. The left ventricle overcomes aortic pressure — about 80 mmHg — to open the aortic valve. An increase in afterload causes a decrease in stroke volume — more blood remains in the ventricles at the end of systole. Hypertension and atherosclerosis are processes that increase afterload.
