# Anatomy of the Heart — Complete Study Guide

## Cardiovascular Anatomy & Physiology

---

## Topic 1: Heart Description and Location

Let's start with the basics. The heart is a hollow, muscular organ. Its job is to pump blood — supplying tissues with nutrients and removing metabolic wastes. It's the central pump of the cardiovascular system, which also includes the blood and blood vessels.

The heart lies in the mediastinum. That's the medial cavity of the thorax, sitting between the lungs. It's slightly tilted, with about two-thirds lying to the left of the sternum and one-third to the right.

---

## Topic 2: Directional Terminology

Let's cover the directional terms you need to know.

The base is the upper portion of the heart, formed by the left atrium and a small amount of the right atrium. It sits approximately at the level of the 2nd rib within the midclavicular line, in front of the esophagus and descending aorta.

The apex is the lower portion, formed by the tip of the left ventricle. It lies above the diaphragm between the 5th and 6th rib within the midclavicular line.

The right border faces the right lung, extending from the inferior surface to the base. The left border faces the left lung, extending from the base to the apex. The inferior surface sits between the apex and the right border, resting mostly on the diaphragm. The anterior surface is deep to the sternum and the ribs — it's the front of the heart.

---

## Topic 3: Size and Shape

The heart is about 5 inches long, 3.5 inches wide, and 2.5 inches deep. Weight differs between sexes — females average about 8 ounces, males about 10 ounces.

Size and shape are influenced by age, body weight and build, how frequently you exercise, and whether heart disease is present.

---

## Topic 4: The Pericardium

The pericardium is a double-walled sac that encloses the heart. It protects the heart, reduces friction, and secures it in place.

The outer layer is the fibrous pericardium. It's made of tough, inelastic, dense irregular connective tissue. It anchors the heart in the mediastinum, prevents overstretching, and prevents movement when you change body position.

Inside that is the serous pericardium — a thinner, double-walled sac. The parietal pericardium is the outer layer, fused to the fibrous pericardium. The visceral pericardium — also called the epicardium — is the inner layer that adheres to the surface of the heart.

Between the visceral and parietal layers is the pericardial cavity. It contains about 20 milliliters of serous fluid — slippery secretions produced by pericardial cells. This fluid reduces friction as the heart beats.

---

## Topic 5: Layers of the Heart Wall

The heart wall has three layers.

The epicardium is the outermost layer. It contains blood capillaries, lymph capillaries, nerve fibers, and fat — the fat is especially thick around the ventricles. The main coronary arteries lie on its surface, making it the first area to receive blood from the coronary arteries.

The myocardium is the middle layer — cardiac muscle tissue. It's striated like skeletal muscle but involuntary like smooth muscle. The cells have a branching, stair-step pattern with gap junctions for communication. This is the thickest layer — about 95% of the heart wall. Its thickness varies by chamber: the ventricles are thicker than the atria, and the left ventricle is the thickest of all. The myocardium is responsible for the pumping action.

The endocardium is the innermost layer — a thin layer of endothelium over connective tissue. It lines the inner chambers, valves, chordae tendineae, and papillary muscles. It minimizes surface friction.

---

## Topic 6: Heart Chambers

The heart has four chambers: two atria up top, two ventricles down below.

The atria are the upper chambers that receive blood from the body. They have thin walls. They have wrinkled pouch-like structures called auricles that allow for a small increase in blood volume. The interatrial septum is the wall between the two atria.

The right atrium receives deoxygenated blood. Its walls are 2 to 3 millimeters thick with an average pressure of 0 to 8 mmHg. Blood enters from three sources: the superior vena cava bringing blood from the head and upper extremities, the inferior vena cava bringing blood from the lower half of the body, and the coronary sinus draining the coronary vessels.

The left atrium receives oxygenated blood from the lungs via the pulmonary veins. Its walls are also 2 to 3 millimeters thick with an average pressure of 1 to 10 mmHg.

The ventricles are the lower chambers. They're thick-walled. The right ventricle pumps blood to the lungs. The left ventricle pumps blood to the body.

Let's talk about some important internal features. Trabeculae carneae are ridges or raised bundles of cardiac muscle fibers that house some of the conduction system. Papillary muscles are cone-shaped extensions of the trabeculae carneae that connect to the chordae tendineae. The chordae tendineae are string-like connective tissue attached to the AV valves and papillary muscles.

The right ventricle is 4 to 5 millimeters thick. Its pressure ranges from 0 to 8 mmHg in diastole and 15 to 30 mmHg in systole. It pumps deoxygenated blood to the lungs through the pulmonary artery.

The left ventricle pumps blood to the body through the aorta. It rotates forward while contracting, causing the point of maximal impulse — the PMI — which is where you feel the LV most strongly. It's 13 to 15 millimeters thick — the thickest part of the heart since it has to overcome the pressure of the entire body. Its pressure ranges from 3 to 12 mmHg in diastole and 100 to 140 mmHg in systole.

---

## Topic 7: External and Internal Separation

Externally, the heart has grooves called sulci. These contain coronary arteries and fat and mark the boundaries between chambers. The coronary sulcus runs horizontally and separates the atria from the ventricles. The anterior and posterior interventricular sulci run vertically and separate the right and left ventricles.

Internally, the interatrial septum separates the right and left atria. It contains the fossa ovalis — an oval depression marking where the foramen ovale was located in fetal circulation. The interventricular septum separates the right and left ventricles.

---

## Topic 8: The Fibrous Skeleton

The fibrous skeleton is made of dense rings of connective tissue. It surrounds the heart valves, fuses all four valves together, and provides structural support. It prevents overstretching of the valves. It's also the insertion point for the Bundle of His. And importantly, it acts as an electrical insulator — it prevents the electrical signal from passing prematurely from the atria to the ventricles.
