# Heart Valves and Circulation — Complete Study Guide

## Cardiovascular Anatomy & Physiology

---

## Topic 1: Valve Description

Heart valves open and close in response to pressure changes as the heart contracts and relaxes. Their job is to ensure one-way flow of blood and prevent backflow, which is also called regurgitation.

There are two categories of valves. The atrioventricular valves — or AV valves — include the tricuspid valve on the right and the bicuspid or mitral valve on the left. The semilunar valves — or SL valves — include the pulmonary valve on the right and the aortic valve on the left.

---

## Topic 2: Atrioventricular Valves

The AV valves are anchored by the fibrous skeleton. They sit between an atrium and a ventricle and consist of cusps, chordae tendineae, and papillary muscles.

Here's how they work. When the ventricles are relaxed — that's diastole — the papillary muscles are relaxed and the chordae tendineae are slack. Blood flows from the contracting atria, which is higher pressure, into the relaxed ventricles, which is lower pressure.

When the ventricles contract — systole — ventricular pressure rises. Blood tries to move from higher pressure to lower pressure, pushing the AV valves upward until their edges meet and close. The papillary muscles contract, pulling the chordae tendineae to keep the valves from prolapsing.

The tricuspid valve sits between the right atrium and right ventricle and prevents backflow into the right atrium. The mitral or bicuspid valve sits between the left atrium and left ventricle and prevents backflow into the left atrium.

---

## Topic 3: Semilunar Valves

The SL valves are also anchored by the fibrous skeleton. They sit between a ventricle and the pulmonary artery or aorta. Each consists of three crescent moon-shaped cusps.

When the ventricles relax, blood starts to flow back toward the heart. The cusps fill with blood and the SL valves close. When the ventricles contract, pressure increases and the SL valves open.

The aortic valve prevents backflow into the left ventricle. The pulmonic valve prevents backflow into the right ventricle.

---

## Topic 4: Circulation of Blood

The heart pumps blood through two closed circuits: systemic circulation and pulmonary circulation.

Systemic circulation involves the left side of the heart. It receives oxygenated blood from the lungs. The route goes heart to aorta to arteries to arterioles to capillaries to venules to veins to the superior and inferior vena cava and back to the heart. Oxygenated blood goes out to the body, deoxygenated blood returns. This is a high-pressure system.

Pulmonary circulation involves the right side of the heart. It receives deoxygenated blood from the systemic circulation. The route goes heart to pulmonary trunk to pulmonary arteries to the lungs to pulmonary capillaries to pulmonary veins and back to the heart. Deoxygenated blood goes to the lungs, oxygenated blood returns. This is a low-pressure system.

---

## Topic 5: Major Arterial Routes

Let's go through the major arteries carrying blood away from the heart.

The aortic arch is an elastic artery. The carotid arteries — right and left — feed oxygenated blood to the brain. The subclavian arteries feed the upper limbs. The brachial arteries run along the medial side of the biceps brachii. The radial arteries supply the posterior compartment of the forearm. The ulnar arteries supply the anterior compartment of the forearm. The iliac arteries feed the lower limbs. The femoral arteries run inferior to the inguinal ligament. The popliteal arteries sit posterior to the knee. The dorsalis pedis runs along the top of the foot.

---

## Topic 6: Major Venous Routes

The inferior vena cava is the largest vein in the body — about 3.5 centimeters in diameter. It returns deoxygenated blood to the right atrium from the lower body.

The superior vena cava is about 7.5 centimeters long and 2 centimeters in diameter. It returns deoxygenated blood from the upper body.

The jugular veins drain deoxygenated blood from the brain, face, and neck. The superficial veins include the cephalic and basilic veins of the upper limbs.

---

## Topic 7: Coronary Circulation

The myocardium has its own network of blood vessels — the coronary arteries and coronary veins.

The left coronary artery has two main branches. The anterior interventricular branch, also called the left anterior descending or LAD, sits in the anterior interventricular sulcus and supplies both ventricles. The circumflex branch sits in the coronary sulcus and supplies the left ventricle and left atrium.

The right coronary artery also has two main branches. The posterior interventricular branch sits in the posterior interventricular sulcus and supplies both ventricles. The marginal branch sits in the coronary sulcus along the right margin and supplies the right ventricle.

The coronary veins include the great cardiac vein in the anterior interventricular sulcus, draining both ventricles and the left atrium. The middle cardiac vein in the posterior interventricular sulcus drains both ventricles. The small cardiac vein in the coronary sulcus drains the right atrium and right ventricle. The anterior cardiac veins run along the right margin and drain the right ventricle.

All coronary veins drain into the coronary sinus, which empties into the right atrium.
