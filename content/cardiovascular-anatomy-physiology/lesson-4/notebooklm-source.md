# Structure and Function of Blood Vessels — Complete Study Guide

## Cardiovascular Anatomy & Physiology

---

## Topic 1: Five Types of Blood Vessels

Let's start with the five types of blood vessels and what each one does.

Arteries carry blood away from the heart to other organs. Arterioles are divisions of larger arteries that lead into capillary beds. Capillaries are the smallest vessels — they're where the exchange of substances between blood and tissues happens. Venules are where capillary beds reunite, beginning the return flow to the heart. Veins convey blood back to the heart.

---

## Topic 2: Structure of a Blood Vessel Wall

The wall of a blood vessel has three layers called tunics.

The tunica interna — also called the tunica intima — forms the inner lining with direct contact with the blood. Blood flows through the lumen, which is the interior opening. The innermost layer is the endothelium. Endothelial cells secrete fluids that mediate the contractile state of the vessel and assist with capillary permeability. The basement membrane provides physical support. Collagen fibers give tensile strength and allow stretch and recoil. The internal elastic lamina is a thin sheet of elastic fibers with window-like openings that facilitate diffusion.

The tunica media is the middle layer and shows the greatest variation among different vessel types. It's mainly smooth muscle and elastic fibers. Its primary role is to regulate the diameter of the lumen. Vasoconstriction is a decrease in lumen diameter. Vasodilation is an increase in lumen diameter. The tunica media also plays an important role in vascular spasm after a vessel is damaged — this helps limit blood loss.

The tunica externa is the outer covering. It contains nerves and tiny blood vessels called the vasa vasorum that supply the vessel wall itself with oxygen and nutrients. It also helps anchor the vessel in place.

## Topic 3: Arteries

There are two types of arteries.

Elastic arteries are the largest in the body — from the garden-hose-sized aorta down to finger-sized branches. They have the largest diameter but relatively thin walls compared to their size. They get their name from their ability to transfer energy generated by the heart's pump. They have a well-defined internal and external elastic lamina and a thicker tunica media dominated by elastic fibers called elastic lamellae. When blood is ejected into them, their walls stretch and temporarily store mechanical energy. Then the stretched fibers recoil, continuing the flow of blood to the rest of the body.

Muscular arteries contain more smooth muscle than elastic tissue. They're capable of greater vasoconstriction and vasodilation to adjust blood flow. They're known as distributing vessels because they distribute blood to the rest of the body. They're important for maintaining vascular tone, vessel pressures, and efficient blood flow.

Anastomoses are unions of branches of two or more arteries supplying the same body region. They allow collateral circulation if a vessel becomes blocked or damaged.

## Topic 4: Arterioles, Capillaries, and Capillary Beds

Arterioles literally mean small arteries. They regulate blood flow into the capillary networks of tissue. They regulate resistance — decreasing the internal diameter increases friction, resistance, and pressure.

Capillaries are the smallest blood vessels. The word comes from capillus, meaning little hair. They're the exchange vessels. Their network forms an enormous surface area that's critical for diffusion. They lack a tunica media and tunica externa.

During periods of low use, capillary beds constrict and restrict blood flow to the local tissue. When metabolic demand increases, the capillary beds dilate, increasing blood flow and delivery of needed substances. Precapillary sphincters control this — they constrict and relax to allow blood flow into specific capillary beds.

---

## Topic 5: Venules and Veins

Venules and veins have thin walls that don't readily maintain their shape. Venules drain capillary blood and begin the return flow to the heart. Postcapillary venules receive blood from capillaries and are extremely porous — they're significant sites of nutrient exchange.

Muscular venules are the most distensible elements of the vascular system. They expand and serve as an excellent reservoir for accumulating large volumes of blood.

Veins have relatively thin walls compared to their diameter. The tunica externa is the thickest layer in veins, made of collagen and elastic fibers. Blood pressure in veins is much lower than in arteries.

Veins connect like rungs on a ladder, creating anastomoses between deep veins and superficial veins. The skeletal-muscle pump helps squeeze blood back to the heart, and small valves prevent blood from flowing backward.

---

## Topic 6: Blood Distribution

At rest, the largest portion of your blood volume is in your systemic veins and venules — about 64 percent. Systemic arteries and arterioles hold about 13 percent. Systemic capillaries hold about 7 percent. Pulmonary blood vessels hold about 9 percent. The heart holds about 7 percent.

Systemic veins and venules act as a blood reserve. During hemorrhage or exercise, vasoconstriction of these vessels allows blood to be redistributed to maintain survivable blood pressure or to reach working skeletal muscle.
