# Capillary Exchange, Hemodynamics, and BP Control — Complete Study Guide

## Cardiovascular Anatomy & Physiology

---

## Topic 1: Capillary Exchange

There are three basic mechanisms controlling the exchange of substances between blood and interstitial fluid.

First is diffusion — simple diffusion is the most important method. Many substances like oxygen, carbon dioxide, glucose, amino acids, and hormones enter and leave capillaries by simple diffusion. For example, pO2 is higher in the blood than in the tissues, so it diffuses out. CO2 does the opposite.

Second is transcytosis. Substances in the blood plasma become enclosed by tiny pinocyte vesicles and move across endothelial cells. This is how large, lipid-insoluble molecules that can't cross capillary walls any other way get through.

Third is bulk flow. This is a passive process where large numbers of ions, molecules, or particles in a fluid move together in the same direction. Substances move at rates far greater than diffusion alone. They move from areas of higher pressure to lower pressure.

---

## Topic 2: Starling's Law of the Capillaries

The volume of fluid and solutes reabsorbed is normally almost as large as the volume filtered — that near equilibrium is known as Starling's Law. It governs bulk flow.

Four pressures are involved.

Blood hydrostatic pressure — BHP — is the water pressure pushing out from the blood vessel to the interstitial space. Interstitial fluid hydrostatic pressure — IFHP — pushes fluid from the interstitial space into the blood vessel. Blood colloid osmotic pressure — BCOP — is the osmotic pressure of solutes that pulls water into the capillary from the interstitial space. Interstitial fluid osmotic pressure — IFOP — pulls water out of the blood vessel.

BHP and IFOP are pressures that promote filtration. BCOP and IFHP are pressures that promote reabsorption.

Net filtration pressure, or NFP, is calculated as BHP plus IFOP minus BCOP plus IFHP. It determines whether the volume of blood and interstitial fluid stays the same or changes, and it indicates the direction of fluid movement.

BHP pushes fluid out of the capillaries into interstitial fluid. IFHP pushes fluid from interstitial spaces back into capillaries — but it's usually very small and often negligible.

BCOP is caused by the colloidal suspension of large proteins in the plasma. These proteins — like albumin — are too large to pass through fenestrations or gaps between endothelial cells. BCOP pulls fluid from interstitial spaces into capillaries. Colloids are various proteins and lipids, and albumin is the main plasma protein responsible for colloid osmotic pressure.

IFOP pulls fluid out of the capillaries into the interstitial fluid. Proteins that leak from the plasma into interstitial fluid don't accumulate there because they pass into the lymph in lymphatic capillaries and eventually return to the blood.

## Topic 3: Hemodynamics — Blood Flow and Pressure

Blood flow is the volume of blood that flows through any tissue in a given period, measured in milliliters per minute. Total blood flow is cardiac output — CO equals SV times HR.

Blood flows from regions of higher pressure to regions of lower pressure. The greater the pressure difference, the greater the blood flow. Contraction of the ventricles generates blood pressure.

Blood pressure is determined by cardiac output, blood volume, and vascular resistance. BP is highest in the aorta and large systemic arteries — normally about 110 over 70. Systole is ventricular contraction; diastole is ventricular relaxation. BP decreases as it gets closer to the systemic arterioles and disappears in the capillaries, reaching zero by the right ventricle.

Mean arterial pressure — MAP — is the average blood pressure in the arteries. It's calculated as diastolic BP plus one-third of the difference between systolic and diastolic BP.

Normal adult blood volume is about 5 liters. Compensation for blood loss can occur, but anything greater than 10 percent loss will drop blood pressure.

## Topic 4: Vascular Resistance

Vascular resistance is the opposition to blood flow due to friction between the blood and the walls of blood vessels. It's based on three factors.

First, the size of the vessel lumen. The smaller the lumen, the greater the resistance. Vasodilation increases the radius, decreases resistance, and decreases pressure. Vasoconstriction decreases the radius, increases resistance, and increases pressure.

Second, blood viscosity. This depends mostly on the ratio of blood cells to plasma volume and, to a smaller extent, on protein concentration like albumin. Higher viscosity means greater resistance. Polycythemia and dehydration increase viscosity. Hemorrhage decreases it.

Third, total blood vessel length. Resistance is directly proportional to length. Longer vessels mean greater resistance. Obese people often have hypertension because additional blood vessels in adipose tissue increase total blood vessel length — about 400 miles of additional vessels develop for each 2.2 pounds of fat.

Systemic vascular resistance — SVR — is the total peripheral resistance caused by vessels throughout the cardiovascular system. The diameter of the vasculature regulates total system pressure, done primarily by the arterioles and controlled by the vasomotor center.

## Topic 5: Venous Return

Venous return is the volume of blood returning to the heart through the systemic veins. The pressure difference between the venules at about 16 mmHg and the right ventricle at about 0 mmHg drives this flow.

Two pumps besides the heart promote venous return.

The skeletal muscle pump works like this. While standing at rest, both the proximal and distal venous valves are open and blood flows toward the heart. Contraction of leg muscles compresses the vein, pushing blood through the proximal valve in a milking action. As blood is compressed toward the distal valve, the leaflets capture it and close, preventing backflow toward the foot. When the muscle relaxes, pressure falls in the compressed section. Blood flows through the distal valve from the foot into the decompressed vein. After equilibrium, both valves open again.

The respiratory pump works during inhalation. The diaphragm moves downward, decreasing thoracic pressure and increasing abdominal pressure. Abdominal veins compress, thoracic veins decompress, and blood is forced toward the heart. On exhalation, pressures reverse, but valves prevent backflow.

## Topic 6: Control of Blood Pressure

Several interconnected negative feedback systems control blood pressure by adjusting heart rate, stroke volume, systemic vascular resistance, and blood volume.

The cardiovascular center — or CV center — in the medulla oblongata regulates heart rate and stroke volume. It also controls neural, hormonal, and local negative feedback systems.

Three types of sensory receptors provide input. Proprioceptors monitor joint and muscle movements and account for the rapid increase in heart rate at the start of exercise. Baroreceptors are pressure-sensitive receptors located in the aorta, internal carotid arteries, and larger arteries of the neck and chest. Chemoreceptors monitor the concentration of various chemicals in the blood.

Sympathetic impulses reach the heart via the cardiac accelerator nerves. Increased sympathetic stimulation increases heart rate, force of contraction, and cardiac output. Parasympathetic stimulation comes from the vagus nerves and decreases heart rate.

Vasomotor tone refers to the continuous sympathetic stimulation of smooth muscle around vessels, maintaining a general state of vasoconstriction.

---

## Topic 7: Baroreceptor and Chemoreceptor Reflexes

Baroreceptors in the carotid sinus initiate the carotid sinus reflex, which regulates blood pressure to the brain. Baroreceptors in the aortic arch and descending aorta help regulate systemic blood pressure.

When blood pressure falls, baroreceptors are stretched less and send nerve impulses at a slower rate to the CV center. The CV center decreases parasympathetic response and increases sympathetic stimulation via the cardiac accelerator nerves. This causes the heart to beat faster and more forcefully, increasing systemic vascular resistance and blood pressure.

When blood pressure rises, baroreceptors send impulses faster. The CV center increases parasympathetic and decreases sympathetic stimulation. This decreases heart rate and force of contraction, reducing cardiac output. Sympathetic impulses to vasomotor neurons also decrease, causing vasodilation and decreasing SVR and blood pressure.

Chemoreceptors are located close to the baroreceptors in the carotid bodies and aortic bodies. They detect changes in pO2, pCO2, and H+. Hypoxia is low oxygen availability. Acidosis is increased H+ concentration. Hypercapnia is excess pCO2. These chemicals stimulate sympathetic outflow to arterioles and veins, increasing cardiac output.

---

## Topic 8: Hormonal Regulation and Autoregulation

Antidiuretic hormone — ADH, also called vasopressin — causes vasoconstriction and helps the kidneys retain water, increasing blood pressure. Epinephrine and norepinephrine increase sympathetic stimulation, increasing heart rate and force of contraction, and cause vasoconstriction.

Autoregulation is the ability of a tissue to automatically adjust its blood flow to match its metabolic demands. Physically, warming causes vasodilation and cooling causes vasoconstriction. The myogenic response means vessels contract more forcefully when stretched and relax when less stretched. Chemically, vasodilating substances like histamines and nitric oxide and vasoconstricting substances like serotonin regulate local blood flow.

## Topic 9: Shock and Homeostasis

Shock is a failure of the cardiovascular system to deliver enough oxygen and nutrients to meet cellular metabolic needs. All forms are characterized by inadequate blood flow to body tissues.

Hypovolemic shock is due to decreased blood volume. Common causes are hemorrhage and fluid loss from sweating, diarrhea, or vomiting. Treatment is colloid or fluid replacement therapy.

Cardiogenic shock is due to poor heart function. It's usually caused by myocardial infarction — heart cell death. Other causes include poor perfusion of the heart, heart valve problems, excessive preload or afterload, impaired contractility, and arrhythmias.

Vascular shock is due to inappropriate vasodilation. Anaphylactic shock releases histamines causing vasodilation. Neurogenic shock occurs from head trauma affecting the cardiovascular center. Septic shock occurs during bacterial infections and causes widespread vasodilation.

Obstructive shock occurs when a vessel becomes blocked. The most common cause is a pulmonary embolism, where a blood clot lodges in vessels leading to the lungs, decreasing blood flow and impairing gas exchange.

The body's homeostatic response to low blood pressure from shock uses the same control mechanisms — increased sympathetic response, increased hormonal response causing vasoconstriction, and increased local vasodilators.
