# Pain Management, Vasopressors, Inotropes & Antiarrhythmic Agents — Complete Study Guide
## Respiratory Therapy Course — RESC 2320 (Slides 154–176)

---

## Topic 1: Pain

Pain in clinical settings can arise from pathological conditions including preexisting diseases, surgical procedures, trauma, and monitoring and therapeutic devices. Pain can lead to agitation.

Stress responses to pain include tachycardia, elevated myocardial oxygen consumption, pulmonary dysfunction, and hypercoagulability, also known as thrombophilia.

---

## Topic 2: Non-Pharmacologic Pain Relief

Non-pharmacologic pain relief is the elimination of irritating physical stimulation, or pain, without the use of medication.

Examples include re-positioning the patient in bed and stabilization of a fracture.

---

## Topic 3: Analgesia

Analgesia provides absence of sensibility to pain without the loss of consciousness. An important distinction to remember: analgesia and anesthesia are different. While both assist in pain relief, anesthesia results in a complete loss of physical sensation.

---

## Topic 4: NSAIDs (Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs)

NSAIDs are the most common prescribed and cost effective analgesics.

Examples include Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Salicylates (Aspirin), and Celebrex.

Their indication is to treat moderate pain. They are delivered through multiple routes including oral and IV.

---

## Topic 5: Tylenol (Acetaminophen)

Tylenol is commonly used for moderate pain. An important distinction is that Tylenol does not address inflammation, unlike NSAIDs.

---

## Topic 6: Opioids/Narcotics

Opioids and narcotics have rapid onset and provide analgesia and sedation.

The adverse actions include respiratory depression, hypotension, and CNS depression.

The examples to know are Morphine, Hydromorphone, Fentanyl, Meperidine, and Codeine. Codeine has lower respiratory depression compared to the others.

Administration routes include transdermal, IM, IV, and oral.

---

## Topic 7: Sedation

Sedation produces a restful state of mind and a calming effect. Its indication is to treat anxiety and tension. A critical point: sedation must be used if delivering a paralytic agent.

The hypnotic agents, which aid in inducing sleep, include Sodium Thiopental (brand name Pentothal), Etomidate (brand name Amidate) which is used for rapid sequence induction, conscious sedation, and TBI patients, and Haloperidol (brand name Haldol).

---

## Topic 8: Benzodiazepines

Benzodiazepines are tranquilizer agents. Their indications are anxiety, panic attacks, insomnia, and seizures.

The short acting tranquilizer agents to know are Midazolam (brand name Versed) which is the most commonly used, Lorazepam (brand name Ativan), Alprazolam (brand name Xanax), Diazepam (brand name Valium), and Propofol (brand name Diprivan). Propofol causes decreased level of consciousness and lack of memory for events, and is nicknamed Milk of amnesia.

---

## Topic 9: Adenosine

Adenosine is indicated to treat SVT that does not improve with vagal maneuvers such as carotid massage and Valsalva.

Its action is to decrease AV node conduction and slow impulse transmission.

---

## Topic 10: Amiodarone

Amiodarone is indicated for A Fib, VFib, and ventricular tachyarrhythmias.

Its action is to slow conduction and delay repolarization.

---

## Topic 11: Atropine Sulfate

Atropine Sulfate is indicated for sinus bradycardia, asystole, PEA, and organophosphate poisoning.

Its action is to increase heart rate and increase the force of atrial contractions.

---

## Topic 12: Dopamine (Inotrope)

Dopamine is indicated for hypotension with signs and symptoms of shock and as a second-line drug for symptomatic bradycardia.

Its action is to increase myocardial contractility, increase cardiac output, produce cerebral vasodilation, and cause peripheral arterial and venous vasoconstriction.

---

## Topic 13: Epinephrine (Inotrope)

Epinephrine is a vasopressor that optimizes cardiac output and blood pressure.

Its indications include cardiac arrest (VF, pulseless tachycardia, asystole, PEA), symptomatic bradycardia, severe hypotension, and anaphylaxis.

Its action is to stimulate adrenergic receptors, produce vasoconstriction, increase blood pressure, increase heart rate, and improve perfusion to the brain and heart.

---

## Topic 14: Anaphylaxis Treatment

Anaphylaxis is an allergic reaction which involves shock and airway obstruction.

The treatment regimen in order is: first, Epinephrine which is the 1st line drug therapy; second, antihistamines injection; third, corticosteroids injection; fourth, fluids; and fifth, Trendelenburg position.

---

## Topic 15: Lidocaine

Lidocaine is indicated to treat ventricular arrhythmias. It is an alternative antiarrhythmic to Amiodarone in cardiac arrest from VF/VT.

Its action is to increase the electrical stimulation threshold and depress ventricular electrical activity.

---

## Topic 16: Magnesium Sulfate

Magnesium Sulfate is indicated for management of Torsades de Pointe or hypomagnesemia.

Its action is to prolong conduction time caused by hypomagnesemia.

---

## Topic 17: Norepinephrine (Inotrope) — Levophed

Norepinephrine, brand name Levophed, is indicated for vasogenic shock (very low blood pressure), septic shock, and neurogenic shock.

Its action is alpha-adrenergic stimulation producing vasoconstriction, which increases blood pressure and increases blood flow.

---

## Topic 18: Oxygen

Oxygen is indicated when there is decreased PaO2. It provides all cellular energy and support for myocardial contraction.

Its action is to prevent cellular and tissue hypoxia.

---

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Pain stress responses**: tachycardia, elevated myocardial O2 consumption, pulmonary dysfunction, hypercoagulability (thrombophilia)
- **Non-pharmacologic pain relief**: re-positioning, fracture stabilization — no medication
- **Analgesia vs anesthesia**: analgesia = pain relief without loss of consciousness; anesthesia = complete loss of physical sensation
- **NSAIDs**: most common, cost effective; Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Aspirin, Celebrex; moderate pain; oral and IV
- **Tylenol**: moderate pain but does NOT address inflammation
- **Opioids/Narcotics**: rapid onset; respiratory depression, hypotension, CNS depression; Morphine, Hydromorphone, Fentanyl, Meperidine, Codeine (lower resp depression)
- **Sedation**: must be used if delivering paralytic; hypnotic agents: Pentothal, Etomidate (RSI, conscious sedation, TBI), Haldol
- **Benzodiazepines**: anxiety, panic attacks, insomnia, seizures; Midazolam (Versed) most common; Propofol (Diprivan) = "Milk of amnesia"
- **Adenosine**: SVT not improved by vagal maneuvers; decreases AV node conduction
- **Amiodarone**: A Fib, VFib, ventricular tachyarrhythmias; slows conduction, delays repolarization
- **Atropine Sulfate**: sinus bradycardia, asystole, PEA, organophosphate poisoning; increases HR and force of atrial contractions
- **Dopamine (inotrope)**: hypotension/shock, second-line for symptomatic bradycardia; increases contractility, CO, cerebral vasodilation
- **Epinephrine (inotrope)**: cardiac arrest (VF, pulseless tach, asystole, PEA), anaphylaxis; stimulates adrenergic receptors, vasoconstriction, increases BP/HR
- **Anaphylaxis treatment order**: Epinephrine (1st!) → antihistamines injection → corticosteroids injection → fluids → Trendelenburg
- **Lidocaine**: ventricular arrhythmias; alternative to Amiodarone in VF/VT; increases electrical stimulation threshold
- **Magnesium Sulfate**: Torsades de Pointe or hypomagnesemia; prolongs conduction time
- **Norepinephrine (Levophed)**: vasogenic/septic/neurogenic shock; alpha-adrenergic stimulation → vasoconstriction → increases BP
- **Oxygen**: decreased PaO2; prevents cellular and tissue hypoxia; supports myocardial contraction
