## Topic 1: Drug Calculation Terminology

There are three key terms you need to know for drug dose calculations.

A solute is the drug or other substance being dissolved, and it is measured in milligrams (mg).

A solvent is the medium in which the solute is being dissolved. This is typically sterile water or saline, and it is measured in milliliters (mL).

A solution is the solute dissolved in the solvent — the mix. It is represented by a percentage or a percent concentration.

## Topic 2: The Drug Calculation Formula

There is one formula that may be manipulated by division to isolate the unknown variable and solve for "x."

A critical rule to remember: do not convert the percentage given to you in the problem.

Here is a worked example: What volume of a 0.083% solution should be given in order to deliver 2.5mg of the drug?

Step 1: Fill in the known values. 2.5mg equals 0.083 times x times 10.

Step 2: Divide both sides by 10. 2.5 divided by 10 equals 0.083 times x. That gives us 0.25 equals 0.083 times x.

Step 3: Divide both sides by 0.083 in order to isolate and solve for x. 0.25 divided by 0.083 equals x.

The answer is x equals 3mL.

## Topic 3: Ratios

Some medications are labeled as a ratio of weight to volume, written as x colon y.

To solve for the percent concentration from a ratio, divide x by y and multiply by 100.

Here is a worked example: What is the concentration of a medication listed as 1:250?

Solution: 1 divided by 250 equals 0.004. Multiply by 100 and you get 0.4%.

Practice thinking through these: Which ratio represents a 0.13% solution? The answer would be 1:750, because 1 divided by 750 times 100 equals approximately 0.13%.

## Topic 4: Adrenergic Bronchodilators — Short-Acting B2 Agonists (SABAs)

Short-Acting B2 Agonists, known as SABAs, are the most frequently administered class of respiratory medications.

B2 receptors are the most abundant and active receptors in the lungs and are the primary source of control of airway diameter. SABAs are the most effective medications for rapid and direct bronchodilation and are the medication of choice for acute asthma exacerbation. They can be used in conjunction with steroid medications.

### Albuterol (Racemic Albuterol)

Albuterol is indicated as a rescue treatment for acute asthma. It is also used 15 to 30 minutes before exercise to prevent exercise-induced bronchospasm.

The action onset is approximately 5 minutes, with 80% effect in 15 minutes. The action duration is 4 to 8 hours.

Side effects include tremors, palpitations, tachycardia, and hypertension.

Albuterol is available in nebulized solution, extended-release tablets, syrup, and MDI.

An important detail about Albuterol is that it contains both S and R isomers. The S isomer is responsible for the tremors and heart palpitations commonly associated with giving albuterol. The R isomer is the one that relaxes bronchial smooth muscle.

### Levalbuterol (Xopenex)

Levalbuterol is another Beta 2 agonist. It is indicated as rescue treatment for acute asthma and can be used as an alternative to Albuterol.

The key advantage of Levalbuterol is that it has no S isomer, thus eliminating the side effects seen with Albuterol.

## Topic 5: Adrenergic Bronchodilators — Ultra Short Acting

### Racemic Epinephrine

Racemic Epinephrine is indicated for inflammation of the upper airway, post extubation stridor, epiglottitis, croup, bronchiolitis, and bleeding during bronchoscopy.

Its effects last less than 3 hours. It is administered nebulized with saline or directly instilled as seen with bronchoscopy.

### Epinephrine

Epinephrine lacks receptor specificity, thus causing a high prevalence of side effects.

Its indications include systemic vasoconstriction, and it is the primary treatment for anaphylaxis. It also serves as a cardiac stimulant and can be used for asthma exacerbation, but it is not for daily maintenance.

Administration routes include subcutaneous injection for cardiac uses, inhalation for asthma, and intramuscular injection via an Epipen.

---

## Topic 6: Adrenergic Bronchodilators — Long Acting (LABAs)

Long-Acting Beta Agonists are indicated for maintenance treatment of moderate to severe asthma in patients greater than 5 years old, COPD, nocturnal dyspnea and orthopnea, and management of chronic airway disease and reactivity.

Their duration is 12 hours, and they are commonly paired with anti-inflammatory agents or corticosteroids.

Examples include Salmeterol (brand name Serevent) and Formoterol (brand names Foradil and Perforomist). These are known as your "eterol" drugs.

---

## Topic 7: Anticholinergic Bronchodilators

Anticholinergic bronchodilators are indicated for the maintenance of COPD, emphysema, and chronic bronchitis. They are used secondary to or in conjunction with SABAs.

An important note: patients will be prescribed a rescue inhaler or SABA for emergency relief.

### Ipratropium Bromide (Atrovent) — Short Acting Muscarinic Antagonist (SAMA)

Ipratropium Bromide has a duration of up to 6 hours. Available doses include MDI at 17mcg per puff and nebulized solution at 2.5mL in 0.02%.

### Tiotropium Bromide (Spiriva) — Long Acting Muscarinic Antagonist (LAMA)

Tiotropium Bromide slows the rate of progression of COPD. It improves FEV1, which measures how much air a person can exhale in the first second of a forced breath to assess lung function and other obstructive diseases. It also improves the patient's quality of life.

Its duration is 24 hours, and it is available at 18mcg per puff.

---

## Topic 8: Combination Bronchodilators

Albuterol combined with Ipratropium Bromide is a SABA plus SAMA combination. It is indicated for COPD patients who require additional bronchodilation.

The brand names are Combivent and Duoneb. The duration is 4 to 6 hours.

## Topic 9: Monitoring Bronchodilator Effects — Peak Expiratory Flow

Peak expiratory flow measurements, known as PEF, are used for determining airflow obstruction and trending patterns. A peak flow meter measures maximum expiratory flow.

Therapy is indicated in patients whose peak flow measures less than 80% of their predicted PEF value.

### Setting Up a Peak Flow Meter

To establish the predicted PEF, refer to the instruction insert. Locate the section for the patient's sex, then find the patient's age and height. The predicted PEF is where age and height cross on the chart. Then attach the color strip per the instructions.

### Performing the Measurement

First, reference the device insert to determine the patient's predicted peak flow and set the predicted goal value on the device. Instruct the patient to inspire to a maximum deep breath, then blow out as strong as they can in a rapid burst effort into the peak flow meter device. Exhalation to residual volume is unnecessary.

The peak flow measurement is repeated a total of 3 times. You record the best of 3 efforts in the patient's chart.

### PEF Zones

The patient's "personal best" is established by daily measurements as an outpatient when their airflow pattern is stable and the patient is asymptomatic. Results are compared to the patient's personal best baseline when available.

The Green Zone is a PEF of 80% to 100% of personal best. This is normal and no action is required.

The Yellow Zone is a PEF of 60% to 80% of personal best. This requires self-administration of a bronchodilator plus possibly oral steroids.

The Red Zone is a PEF less than 60% of personal best. This requires self-administration of a bronchodilator, contacting the physician, and calling 9-1-1.

## Topic 10: Xanthines (Methylxanthines)

Xanthines, also known as methylxanthines, act like stimulants. They increase cardiac muscle contractility, increase respiratory muscle endurance, increase CNS alertness, and cause diuresis, similar to coffee.

### Theophylline

Theophylline is indicated for asthma as a sustained release formulation — the medication is formulated to release slowly over a period of time — serving as maintenance therapy for mild, persistent asthma.

For COPD, it is used as maintenance after anticholinergics and beta agonists fail to provide adequate control. Other bronchodilators are preferred by the GOLD Guidelines.

Toxic effects must be monitored, including nausea, arrhythmias, and seizures. Liver and kidney functions must also be monitored.

A critical point: Theophylline is contraindicated for exacerbations.

### Caffeine Citrate (Cafcit)

Caffeine Citrate is indicated for Apnea of Prematurity, known as AOP, in premature infants. It stimulates breathing and increases CNS alertness.

It is administered orally or by IV.

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Solute**: drug being dissolved (mg)
- **Solvent**: medium dissolving the drug (mL) — typically sterile water or saline
- **Solution**: solute + solvent (percentage)
- **Ratio to percent**: divide x by y, multiply by 100
- **Do not convert** the percentage in drug calculation problems
- **SABAs**: most frequently administered respiratory medications; medication of choice for acute asthma exacerbation
- **Albuterol**: onset ~5 min (80% in 15 min), duration 4–8 hrs; S isomer = side effects, R isomer = bronchial relaxation
- **Levalbuterol (Xopenex)**: no S isomer — eliminates albuterol side effects
- **Racemic Epinephrine**: upper airway inflammation, stridor, croup; effects < 3 hrs
- **Epinephrine**: primary treatment for anaphylaxis; not for daily maintenance
- **LABAs ("eterol" drugs)**: Salmeterol, Formoterol; 12-hour duration; paired with corticosteroids
- **Ipratropium (Atrovent) — SAMA**: up to 6 hours
- **Tiotropium (Spiriva) — LAMA**: 24 hours; improves FEV1
- **Combivent/Duoneb**: Albuterol + Ipratropium (SABA + SAMA); 4–6 hours
- **PEF**: repeat 3 times, record best of 3
- **Green Zone**: 80–100% personal best — normal
- **Yellow Zone**: 60–80% personal best — bronchodilator + possibly oral steroids
- **Red Zone**: < 60% personal best — bronchodilator + physician + 911
- **Theophylline**: maintenance only; contraindicated for exacerbations; monitor for nausea, arrhythmias, seizures
- **Caffeine Citrate (Cafcit)**: Apnea of Prematurity in premature infants
