## Topic 1: What is Pharmacology?

Pharmacology is the study of drugs to include their origin, properties, and interactions with living organisms.

Drugs are often referred to by two names: their generic name and their trade (brand) name. For example, Acetaminophen is the generic name and Tylenol is the brand name. Ibuprofen is the generic name, and Motrin or Advil are brand names.

## Topic 2: Patient and Medication Verification (5 Steps)

Before administering any medication, we must first be sure we have the right patient. There are five verification steps that must be followed every time.

Step one is to verify patient identity using 2 identifiers: the patient's full name and date of birth (DOB).

Step two is to verify the medication prescribed and double check if any substitutions are or are not allowed. For example, if your patient was prescribed albuterol and the patient states that albuterol makes their heart race, but the physician did not list any approved substitutions, you need to contact the physician before making any changes.

Step three is to verify the dosage of the medication. The dosage of the drug can vary depending on the condition being treated and the way it is administered to the patient.

Step four is to verify correct treatment frequency. You need to know how many times this drug can be given within a 24-hour period and whether you are giving it during the correct prescribed time.

Step five is to verify the treatment route. For example, if your order states to give your patient a dry powder inhaler (a device that crushes the pill and is released when the user inhales deeply through the mouthpiece), but you go to your patient's room and see that they are intubated, that route will not work and you need to contact the physician.

## Topic 3: The Three Phases of Drug Action

There are three phases of drug action: Administration, Pharmacokinetics, and Pharmacodynamics.

Administration refers to how the drug is made available to the body.

Pharmacokinetics refers to the actions the body performs with the drug. The key acronym here is ADME: A stands for Absorption, D stands for Distribution, M stands for Metabolism, and E stands for Elimination.

Pharmacodynamics refers to the mechanisms by which medications produce effects in the body. Under pharmacodynamics, there are two key types of medications: Agonistic medications stimulate receptors, and Antagonistic medications block receptors.

## Topic 4: Routes of Administration

There are four main routes of administration: Oral, Parenteral (injectable), Inhalation, and Topical.

### Oral Route
The oral route is preferable for patients who cannot tolerate or may not respond to inhaled medications. It is also used for noncompliant patients and children. The oral route has a slower absorption time and is metabolized systemically, which means it has greater side effects.

### Inhalation Route
The inhalation route produces less side effects since it bypasses GI absorption and limits hepatic metabolism. It requires smaller doses and has a faster onset of action for respiratory medications. The smaller particle size of the drug results in easier delivery into the lungs.

### Parenteral Route
The parenteral route provides quicker onset of action systemically. Intravenous (IV) is the route of preference for systemically acting drugs in emergency conditions, such as fluids, cardiac medications, and vasopressors. It does carry more systemic side effects. Intramuscular (IM) and Subcutaneous (SQ) routes are slower to absorb and distribute, resulting in greater onset of action time.

In certain cases, giving drugs through an IV may be considered a bolus injection, which is used in emergencies, pain management, and anesthesia induction. Once the concentrated dose is pushed, it is followed by a flush of saline to ensure complete medication delivery.

### Intraosseous (IO)
Intraosseous administration involves giving medications directly into the marrow of large bones. It is only used when IV access cannot be established or other routes are not available. The concerns with IO include necrosis, infection, and osteomyelitis.

### Down the Endotracheal Tube (Emergency Delivery)
In an emergency, specific medications can be delivered down the endotracheal tube (ETT) if IV or IO access is unavailable. The acronym to remember which medications can be given this way is NAVEL: N stands for Naloxone (also called Narcan), A stands for Atropine, V stands for Vasopressin, E stands for Epinephrine, and L stands for Lidocaine. The dose given down the endotracheal tube is 2 to 2.5 times the normal IV dose, followed by a 10mL saline flush.

## Topic 5: Lock and Key Theory

The Lock and Key Theory explains how drugs, hormones, chemical substances, and neurotransmitters can exert or act upon specific receptor sites. The neurotransmitter is the "key" that holds the message. The receptor is the "lock" that will receive the message.

## Topic 6: Sympathetic Nervous System — The Adrenergic Pathway

The Adrenergic Pathway falls under the sympathetic nervous system. When we talk about drugs that impact receptors under the sympathetic nervous system, we classify them as either Adrenergic (which stimulate receptors responding to norepinephrine and epinephrine) or Anti-adrenergic (which block receptors from responding to norepinephrine and epinephrine).

### Alpha(1) Receptors
Alpha-1 receptors are located in the heart, lungs, and vasculature. When stimulated, they cause vasoconstriction (which increases blood pressure) and an increased heart rate. An example of drugs that stimulate alpha-1 receptors are vasopressors.

### Alpha Anti-Adrenergic Medications
Alpha anti-adrenergic medications reduce arteriolar resistance and produce vasodilation, which lowers blood pressure. These drugs are referred to as your "zosin" drugs: Prazosin, doxazosin, and terazosin. They help with blood pressure.

### Beta 1 Receptors
Beta-1 receptors are located in the heart and kidneys. When stimulated, they cause constriction and contraction, thus increasing heart rate and increasing myocardial contractility. An example is Dobutamine, which increases heart rate and contractility.

### Beta 2 Receptors
Beta-2 receptors are located in the lungs, pancreas, liver, intestines, and arteriolar smooth muscle. When stimulated, they cause relaxation: vasodilation (which decreases blood pressure) and bronchodilation (which opens up the airways). Examples include Albuterol and levalbuterol.

### Beta Anti-Adrenergic Medications (Beta Blockers)
Beta blockers block the effects of epinephrine and norepinephrine. The result is reduced heart rate and blood pressure. They are the primary treatment for hypertension. These are your "lol" drugs: atenolol, metoprolol, and labetolol.

## Topic 7: Parasympathetic Nervous System — The Cholinergic Pathway

Under the parasympathetic nervous system (PNS), we follow the Cholinergic Pathway. The receptors under this pathway are muscarinic receptors.

Cholinergic medications stimulate receptors to respond to acetylcholine. Anti-cholinergic medications block receptors from responding to acetylcholine.

### Muscarinic Receptors
Muscarinic receptors are located in the airways. M1 and M3 receptors, when stimulated, cause constriction of the airways. Anti-cholinergic medications block stimulation of M1 and M3, which results in bronchodilation.

## Topic 8: Tips and Tricks for Receptor Classification

When trying to figure out what a drug does, ask yourself three questions:

First, where is my receptor located?

Second, what number is the receptor? The key rule to remember is: 1 and 3 contract, 2 relaxes.

Third, am I stimulating the receptor or blocking the receptor? In other words, is it an agonist or an antagonist?

Here is a worked example: A B2 Adrenergic Medication. The location of beta-2 receptors is the lungs, smooth arteriolar muscles, liver, pancreas, and intestines. The number 2 means relaxation. It is an agonist, meaning it stimulates the receptor. The result is relaxation of the lungs, also known as bronchodilation.

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Pharmacology**: Study of drugs including their origin, properties, and interactions with living organisms
- **Patient verification**: 2 identifiers — full name and DOB
- **Three phases**: Administration, Pharmacokinetics, Pharmacodynamics
- **ADME**: Absorption, Distribution, Metabolism, Elimination
- **Agonistic**: Stimulate receptors
- **Antagonistic**: Block receptors
- **NAVEL**: Naloxone, Atropine, Vasopressin, Epinephrine, Lidocaine (ETT medications at 2–2.5x IV dose + 10mL flush)
- **Lock and Key**: Neurotransmitter = key, Receptor = lock
- **"Zosin" drugs**: Alpha anti-adrenergic (Prazosin, doxazosin, terazosin) — lower BP
- **"Lol" drugs**: Beta blockers (atenolol, metoprolol, labetolol) — lower HR and BP
- **Receptor number rule**: 1 and 3 contract; 2 relaxes
- **Alpha-1**: Heart, lungs, vasculature — vasoconstriction, increased HR
- **Beta-1**: Heart, kidneys — increased HR, increased myocardial contractility
- **Beta-2**: Lungs, pancreas, liver, intestines, arteriolar smooth muscle — vasodilation, bronchodilation
- **Muscarinic M1/M3**: Airways — constriction when stimulated, bronchodilation when blocked (anti-cholinergic)
