# Corticosteroids, Anti-Infectives, Vasodilators & Critical Care Agents — Complete Study Guide
## Respiratory Therapy Course — RESC 2320 (Slides 89–end)

## Topic 1: Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids produce anti-inflammatory action in the lungs by inhibiting cytokines and pro-inflammatory cells. Their primary indication is maintenance treatment for asthma.

Corticosteroids can be administered orally, IV, or by inhalation. Inhalation is the preferred route for respiratory treatment. They are contraindicated for acute exacerbations. Most are long lasting and given BID. Corticosteroids are commonly combined with LABAs in a single treatment.

The inhalation route makes corticosteroids the first line maintenance and control therapy for chronic asthma and COPD. They are also prescribed if the patient is using a SABA more than a few days per week, or if the patient is waking up at night with SOB.

The inhaled corticosteroids you need to know are: Beclomethasone (brand name Qvar), Flunisolide (brand name Aerospan MDI), Fluticasone (brand names Flovent MDI and Flovent Diskus), Budesonide (brand names Pulmicort and Pulmicort Respules), Mometasone (brand name Asmanex Twisthaler), and Ciclesonide (brand name Alvesco).

Side effects of corticosteroids include decreased efficacy of hypertensive and diabetic medications, osteoporosis, cataracts, elevated BP, drug interaction, bleeding risk, and potential growth retardation in pre-pubescents.

A particularly important side effect is fungal infections. Candida Albicans is a yeast infection also known as Thrush. Aspergillus Niger is another fungal infection associated with corticosteroid use. To prevent fungal infections, utilize a holding chamber and rinse the mouth post treatment.

Corticosteroids are also available in combination with LABAs. Fluticasone combined with Salmeterol is sold as Advair in both DPI and MDI forms. Mometasone combined with Formoterol is sold as Dulera MDI. Budesonide combined with Formoterol is sold as Symbicort MDI.

## Topic 2: Nonsteroidal Anti-Asthma Agents

The primary indication for nonsteroidal anti-asthma agents is prophylactic management of mild persistent asthma. They are used in conjunction with anti-inflammatory therapy. An important distinction: these are controller medications, not symptom relievers or for acute exacerbations.

### Asthma Inflammation Triggers

There are two types of asthma inflammation triggers. Extrinsic or Atopic triggers are environmental — these are allergic hypersensitivity reactions to antigens such as pollen, dust, smoke, automobile exhaust, or animal dander. Intrinsic triggers are non-allergic sensitivity — internal factors within the body that can initiate an inflammatory response, even in the absence of external threats like infection or injury.

### Inflammatory Trigger Cascade

The inflammatory trigger cascade works as follows. First, an extrinsic trigger enters the body through allergen sensitivity. Second, release of mediators target airway tissue — these are immune cells that promote inflammation. Third, inflammatory cells are activated in the airways, including leukotrienes, histamine, prostaglandins, and phosphodiesterase.

## Topic 3: Mast Cell Stabilizers

Cromolyn Sodium is a mast cell stabilizer. Its action is to encapsulate the cell membrane making breach of the membrane more difficult, ultimately neutralizing release of inflammatory mediators including histamine, prostaglandins, and leukotrienes.

The result is decreased severity and episodes in asthma and COPD exacerbation.

---

## Topic 4: Anti-Leukotrienes

There are three anti-leukotriene medications to know.

Zafirlukast (brand name Accolate) is indicated for prophylaxis and long-term treatment of asthma in patients 5 years and older. The dose is twice daily with a LABA.

Montelukast (brand name Singulair) is indicated for prophylaxis and long-term treatment of asthma in patients older than 12 months. The dose is once daily.

Zileuton (brand name Zyflo) is indicated for prophylaxis and long-term treatment of asthma in patients 12 years and older. The dose is 600 mg tablet QID, or two extended-release tablets BID. An important point about Zileuton is liver damage susceptibility — liver enzymes must be checked regularly.

---

## Topic 5: Monoclonal Antibody (Anti-IgE)

Omalizumab (brand name Xolair) is a monoclonal antibody classified as an Anti-IgE agent.

Its indications are moderate or greater perennial allergic asthma in patients older than 12 years who are not controlled with inhaled steroids.

It is administered as a subcutaneous injection every 2 to 4 weeks. Its action is to prevent IgE (immunoglobulin E) from attaching to mast cells — in other words, it blocks IgE.

---

## Topic 6: Phosphodiesterase Enzyme (PDE4) Inhibitor

Roflumilast is a PDE4 inhibitor. Its indication is to inhibit the inflammatory response associated with COPD exacerbations.

Its action is to increase cAMP, which produces long-acting inhibition of the PDE4 enzyme. By increasing cAMP, we suppress pro-inflammatory pathways.

It is administered orally.

## Topic 7: Treatments for Pneumonia

There are two main classes of antibiotics used to treat pneumonia.

Quinolones treat Gram positive and Gram negative bacteria. They are a more broad-spectrum type of antibiotic. The quinolones to know are Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin, and Gemifloxacin.

Macrolides treat Gram positive bacteria and limited Gram negative bacteria. The macrolides to know are Azithromycin, Clarithromycin, and Erythromycin.

## Topic 8: Anti-Infective Inhaled Aerosol Agents

The anti-infective inhaled aerosol agents to know are Pentamidine (Nebupent), Tobramycin (Tobi), Gentamicin, Aztreonam (Cayston), Amphotericin B, Zanamivir (Relenza), and Ribavirin (Virazole).

### Pentamidine (Nebupent)

Pentamidine is indicated for prophylactic therapy for Pneumocystis Carinii Pneumonia, known as PCP, which is common in AIDS infected patients.

Its negative effects include bronchospasm, shortness of breath, and bronchial irritation. Bronchodilator pre-treatment may reduce symptoms.

It is administered as a nebulized solution. You must utilize negative airflow rooms with HEPA filtration. The Respirgard II nebulizer is used to protect the care environment. Providers must wear an N-95 mask and full PPE.

### Ribavirin (Virazole)

Ribavirin is indicated for infants with severe Respiratory Syncytial Virus, known as RSV.

It is administered using a Small Particle Aerosol Generator, known as SPAG-2.

Precautions include utilizing negative airflow rooms with HEPA filtration. There are exposure concerns to providers with negative side effects involving the GI system, CNS, and hematologic system.

### Tobramycin (Tobi)

Tobramycin is indicated for Chronic Pseudomonas Aeruginosa, which is a Gram negative organism. It is a common treatment for CF patients.

The dose for adults and children older than 6 years is 300 mg in 5 mL, given twice daily. It is not mixed with other medications and is given as the last medication of each therapy visit.

It is administered as a nebulized solution. Use negative airflow rooms with HEPA filtration.

The inhaled negative effects include teratogenic effects, which means fetal abnormalities risk during pregnancy, voice alteration and tinnitus, ototoxicity which is hearing loss with prolonged use, and nephrotoxicity which is impaired renal function.

### Aztreonam (Cayston)

Aztreonam is indicated for Chronic Pseudomonas Aeruginosa. It is not recommended for children younger than 7 years of age. It may cause bronchospasm and decrease in FEV1. Pretreatment with a bronchodilator is recommended.

### Gentamicin

Gentamicin is indicated for Pseudomonas Aeruginosa. It is common with CF.

### Amphotericin B

Amphotericin B is indicated for pulmonary fungal infections in transplant patients. The specific infections include Aspergillosis and Candida Albicans.

### Zanamivir (Relenza)

Zanamivir is indicated for influenza in adults and children older than 5 years of age within the first 2 days of infection. It is administered as a DPI using a Diskhaler. The dose is two inhalations of 5 mg each, every 12 hours (BID) for 5 days.

The negative effects include bronchospasm. It is not recommended for patients with underlying airway disease.

## Topic 9: Pneumovax — Pneumococcal Vaccine

Pneumovax protects against influenza bacteria and Streptococcus Pneumoniae.

Immunization is given annually to health care providers, children, people with risk of aspiration, school and work communities, and individuals predisposed to environmental risk.

Contraindications include allergy sensitivity, which is rare.

---

## Topic 10: Cold and Cough Agents — Antihistamines

Antihistamines are secretion modifiers — medications that inhibit mucus production and/or modify mucus rheology or consistency.

Their indication is to reduce the volume of secretions associated with upper airway diseases and allergies. They produce a strong, long acting, drying effect.

The medications to know are Allegra, Zyrtec, and Claritin, which are non-sedating. Benadryl (Diphenhydramine) has a sedating effect.

---

## Topic 11: Inhaled Nitric Oxide (INOmax)

Inhaled Nitric Oxide, sold as INOmax, is a pulmonary vasodilator.

Its primary FDA-approved indication is Persistent Pulmonary Hypertension in Neonates, known as PPHN, in neonates older than 34 weeks. It decreases the need for ECMO.

Off label benefits that are NOT FDA approved include acute pulmonary hypertension in adults, ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome), post lung transplant perfusion, reducing pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR), and reducing pulmonary artery pressure (PAP).

Treatment is normally for 14 days or less. Delivery modalities include inline mechanical ventilator life support, which is the primary mode, and high flow NC using Vapotherm for neonates and adults. The recommended dose is less than 20 ppm.

The risks of INOmax include methemoglobin, which is a ferric (Fe3+) hemoglobin molecule that cannot bind with oxygen. Slow weaning is required to prevent rebound.

---

## Topic 12: Prostacyclin

The prostacyclin agents to know are Iloprost (brand name Ventavis), Treprostinil (brand name Tyvaso), and Epoprostenol Sodium (brand name Flolan).

---

## Topic 13: Skeletal Muscle Relaxants — Neuromuscular Blocking Agents (NMBAs)

NMBAs work to paralyze and relax skeletal muscles. They affect skeletal muscle only.

Indications for NMBAs include facilitating intubation by paralyzing the vocal cords, immobilizing patients for surgery, enhancing ventilator synchrony, reducing intracranial pressure (ICP), and reducing O2 consumption.

---

## Topic 14: Depolarizing Agents

Succinylcholine is the only agent in the depolarizing category. It works by occupying acetylcholine receptors.

Its action produces paralysis in 60 to 90 seconds. The effect persists for 10 to 15 minutes. It is ideal for patients requiring intubation.

A critical point: there is no reversal agent for Succinylcholine.

Adverse effects include dry eyes, tachycardia, hypotension, malignant hyperthermia, muscle pain and muscle weakness, and hyperkalemia. Hyperkalemia occurs due to the efflux of potassium from muscle cells. The effects of severe hyperkalemia include arrhythmias and cardiac arrest.

---

## Topic 15: Non-Depolarizing Agents

Non-depolarizing agents work by blocking acetylcholine receptors. They have a longer duration than the depolarizing agent, approximately 30 minutes. They are the preferred agents for paralysis of mechanically ventilated patients. Onset varies and is dosage dependent, typically greater than 5 minutes.

The non-depolarizing agents to know are Rocuronium, Vecuronium, and Pancuronium.

The reversal agent for non-depolarizing agents is Neostigmine.

Adverse effects include inadequate ventilation due to paralysis of the diaphragm and intercostal muscles; cardiovascular effects where the vagolytic effect triggers tachycardia and an increase in mean arterial pressure (MAP), with Pancuronium having the greatest potential for cardiovascular side effects; and histamine release from mast cells which triggers hypotension.

---

## Topic 16: Urine Output

Normal urine output is 30 to 60 mL per hour. Anuria means no urine output and is a sign of renal failure. Oliguria means urine output less than 30 to 60 mL per hour. Polyuria means urine output greater than 60 mL per hour.

---

## Topic 17: Diuretics

Diuretics are indicated for IV resuscitation fluid overload, Congestive Heart Failure, hypertension, pulmonary edema, cerebral edema (TBI), and renal dysfunction.

Contraindications include hypovolemia, pregnancy, and women breast feeding.

Diuretic effects include stimulating urine production, eliminating excess extracellular fluid volume, and reducing blood pressure.

Side effects include hypovolemia and acid-base abnormalities.

The common diuretics to know by category are: Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (Acetazolamide, Methazolamide, and Dichlorphenamide), Osmotic diuretics (Mannitol, Glycerol, and Urea), Loop diuretics (Furosemide, also known as Lasix, which is the most commonly used diuretic for CHF), Thiazide diuretics (Chlorothiazide and Hydrochlorothiazide), and Potassium Sparing diuretics (Amiloride and Spironolactone).

---

## Topic 18: Fluid Resuscitation

Fluid resuscitation is indicated for hypotension, hypovolemia, postural hypotension, and shock.

Treatments include fluids (intravenous and oral rehydration, blood products, and colloids), chemical agents (vasopressor agents such as norepinephrine and dopamine), and placing the patient in the Trendelenburg position.

---

## Topic 19: Hypotension and Hypovolemia

Hypotension is defined as blood pressure less than 90/65 mmHg. An important point: hypotension is NOT synonymous with shock. Hypotension is caused by dehydration (hypovolemia) and is reversed by rehydration.

Hypovolemia means decreased blood volume. Causes include initiation of diuretic treatment or increased dose, reduced dietary sodium intake, diarrhea or excessive sweating, and ingesting drugs that impair diuretic administration.

Indications of hypovolemia include sinus tachycardia, decreased CO and SV, dizziness, extreme thirst, excessive mouth dryness, decrease or dark urine output, and constipation.

---

## Topic 20: Postural Hypotension

Postural hypotension is dizziness upon standing or abruptly sitting up, caused by reduced cerebral blood flow. It occurs in healthy individuals with mild hypovolemia. Syncope may occur if it is more serious (fainting). It can also be caused by medication side effects or interactions.

The verification method is to measure blood pressure in the supine and sitting positions or standing up.

---

## Topic 21: Shock and Resuscitation Fluids

Shock is defined as inadequate delivery of O2 and nutrients to vital organs relative to their metabolic demand. There are two categories: hypo-dynamic (which includes cardiogenic and hypovolemic shock) and hyper-dynamic (which includes vascular, septic, and anaphylactic shock).

### Colloid — Volume Expanders

Colloids are large molecule solutions incapable of crossing the healthy semipermeable capillary membrane owing to the molecular weight and size of the molecules. Examples include Human Albumin (4 to 5%) in saline and Hydroxyethyl Starch (HES).

### Crystalloid — Carrier and Expander

Crystalloids are small molecule solutions. The flow crosses the semipermeable membrane allowing transfer from the bloodstream into cells and body tissues. Normal saline (0.9%) is the most common crystalloid.
