> 1. **Physics of ultrasound** — ultrasound uses HIGH-FREQUENCY SOUND WAVES of 20,000 Hz OR MORE transmitted through a medium via a TRANSDUCER. The beam can be directed and focused. Three key terms: INTERFACE is a surface formed when two media of different densities come together; REFRACTION is the bending of sound from a straight path as it goes from one medium to another; REFLECTION is sound that bounces back from an interface. Reflection depends on the DEGREE OF DIFFERENCE between the two media and on the ANGLE of the interface — the closer to 90 DEGREES, the greater the sound reflected back.
>
> 2. **Echocardiography overview** — the echocardiogram remains the MOST COMMONLY USED and COMPREHENSIVE cardiac imaging method. It is NON-INVASIVE and INEXPENSIVE. Two primary methods: STANDARD TRANSTHORACIC ECHOCARDIOGRAPHY (TTE) and TRANSESOPHAGEAL ECHOCARDIOGRAPHY (TEE). Sound waves are emitted from PIEZOELECTRIC CRYSTALS housed in the TRANSDUCER. The transducer SENDS AND RECEIVES the ultrasound. Reflected waves produce the image on screen.
>
> 3. **2-D echocardiography** — two-dimensional imaging uses MULTIPLE emitting and receiving elements, occurring RAPIDLY to generate MOVING IMAGES. Excellent for BASIC CARDIAC MORPHOLOGY and FUNCTIONAL ASSESSMENT.
>
> 4. **Four standard transducer positions** — PARASTERNAL (beside the sternum, 3rd–4th left intercostal space), APICAL (at the apex, located by palpating the PMI), SUBCOSTAL (below the ribs, upper abdomen), and SUPRASTERNAL (in the suprasternal notch above the sternum). Rotations and angulations of the transducer increase visualization of specific anatomy.
>
> 5. **Patient positions** — PARASTERNAL and APICAL views: patient in the LEFT LATERAL DECUBITUS position. SUBCOSTAL and SUPRASTERNAL NOTCH views: patient in the SUPINE position.
>
> 6. **Parasternal long axis view** — represents a SAGITTAL OR CORONAL section of the heart; BISECTS the heart from base to apex. The ultrasound beam is parallel with a line joining the RIGHT SHOULDER to the LEFT FLANK. Structures visible: AORTIC ROOT, AORTIC VALVE LEAFLETS, MITRAL VALVE LEAFLETS, and CHORDAL/PAPILLARY MUSCLE ATTACHMENTS. To obtain the RIGHT VENTRICULAR INFLOW TRACT long-axis view, tilt the transducer INFEROMEDIALLY (pointing down) and rotate slightly clockwise — visualizes the RV and RA; good for identifying TRICUSPID VALVE REGURGITATION.
>
> 7. **Parasternal short axis view** — PERPENDICULAR to the long axis view (like slicing bread). Obtained by rotating the transducer CLOCKWISE from the long axis position; the notch faces SUPERIORLY toward the RIGHT SUPRACLAVICULAR FOSSA. Four levels from base to apex: AORTIC VALVE level (aortic leaflets and RVOT); MITRAL VALVE level (also called "FISH LIPS" for the appearance of the open mitral valve — the BASE/BASAL level); PAPILLARY MUSCLE level (MID level); APICAL walls.
>
> 8. **Apical views** — start by palpating the POINT OF MAXIMAL IMPULSE (PMI) to locate the apex. Images are displayed as if viewing from the APEX UP through to the BASE. Patient remains in the LEFT LATERAL DECUBITUS position. THREE views: FOUR-CHAMBER VIEW — visualizes ALL FOUR CHAMBERS simultaneously; greatest benefit is identifying RELATIVE VENTRICULAR SIZES; APEX is CLOSEST to the transducer, ATRIA are FURTHEST away. LONG-AXIS (FIVE-CHAMBER) VIEW — slight CLOCKWISE rotation adds the AORTIC ROOT and AORTIC VALVE to identify the AORTIC OUTFLOW TRACT. TWO-CHAMBER VIEW — further clockwise rotation. ALL THREE APICAL VIEWS are ESSENTIAL for analysis of REGIONAL MYOCARDIAL CONTRACTILITY — particularly for stress echocardiography.
>
> 9. **Subcostal position** — used when the standard ultrasonic window is OBLITERATED due to HYPERINFLATED LUNGS from COPD or EMPHYSEMA. Transducer placed in the MIDLINE or slightly to the patient's RIGHT; groove pointed toward the PATIENT'S SPINE. Tilting inferiorly with the transducer head toward the patient's right visualizes LIVER PARENCHYMA, HEPATIC VESSELS, and INFERIOR VENA CAVA. Combined with COLOR DOPPLER: helps identify REGURGITATION and RIGHT HEART DYSFUNCTION.
>
> 10. **Suprasternal view** — transducer head positioned in the SUPRASTERNAL NOTCH. PRIMARY USE: examine the GREAT VESSELS. Long-axis view shows the ascending aorta and arch. Short-axis view obtained by rotating clockwise so the notch faces POSTERIORLY toward the PATIENT'S TRACHEA. Structures: ASCENDING AORTA, AORTIC ARCH, BRACHIOCEPHALIC VESSELS, and DESCENDING THORACIC AORTA. May be UNCOMFORTABLE on the patient's throat.
>
> 11. **M-Mode** — records DETAILED MOTION of cardiac structures. VERTICAL AXIS = DISTANCE (DEPTH). HORIZONTAL AXIS = TIME. Can only be measured through 2-DIMENSIONAL imaging. Used for: measurement of DIMENSIONS; detection of SUBTLE MOTION ABNORMALITIES; detection of VALVULAR DYSFUNCTION (primarily MITRAL and AORTIC valves); assessment of VENTRICULAR DIMENSIONS.
>
> 12. **Doppler effect and Doppler echocardiography** — the Doppler effect: INCREASE in sound frequency as the source moves TOWARD the observer; DECREASE as the source moves AWAY. In the circulatory system the moving target is RED BLOOD CELLS. Doppler echocardiography measures BLOOD FLOW VELOCITIES in the heart and great vessels. Blood cells moving TOWARD the transducer have a HIGHER frequency; blood cells moving AWAY have a LOWER frequency.
>
> 13. **Color flow imaging and BART** — color flow imaging is based on PULSED WAVE DOPPLER principles (single transducer crystal fires and receives a sound wave). The machine automatically assigns colors to velocities and directions: BART mnemonic — BLUE = AWAY (from transducer), RED = TOWARDS (transducer). GREEN = TURBULENT FLOW.
>
> 14. **Contrast studies (bubble study)** — contrast echocardiography is primarily used to identify INTRACARDIAC SHUNTS; also known as a BUBBLE STUDY. AGITATED SALINE produces small BUBBLES visible on ultrasound. Primarily used to identify ATRIAL SHUNTS through the PATENT FORAMEN OVALE (PFO), which is a COMMON FINDING. If agitated saline bubbles enter the RIGHT ATRIUM and then appear in the LEFT ATRIUM shortly after, this indicates an ATRIAL SEPTAL DEFECT.
>
> 15. **Stress echocardiography** — most common indication is evaluation of MYOCARDIAL ISCHEMIA or CORONARY ARTERY DISEASE (CAD). Done PRE AND POST STRESS. Some echo machines allow SIDE-BY-SIDE COMPARISON of pre and post recordings. Stress modalities: TREADMILL EXERCISE TEST; UPRIGHT OR SUPINE BICYCLE; PHARMACOLOGIC AGENT — example: DOBUTAMINE. Exercise-induced REGIONAL WALL MOTION ABNORMALITIES can be detected A FEW MINUTES AFTER EXERCISE — they are due to ISCHEMIA causing ABNORMAL CONTRACTILITY.
>
> 16. **TEE — procedure** — SEMI-INVASIVE procedure. Uses a MODIFIED GASTROESOPHAGEAL ENDOSCOPY PROBE with an ULTRASOUND TRANSDUCER at the tip. Patient in LEFT LATERAL DECUBITUS position. Patient is LIGHTLY SEDATED and given NUMBING AGENTS (e.g., lidocaine) before probe insertion. FASTING IS REQUIRED — higher likelihood of PULMONARY ASPIRATION. Careful guidance needed to avoid TRACHEAL INTUBATION during esophageal passage.
>
> 17. **TEE — indications** — PRIMARY: SOURCE OF EMBOLISM (from atrial fibrillation) and ATRIAL FIBRILLATION. Others: SUSPECTED ENDOCARDITIS, VALVULAR DISEASES, EVALUATION OF UPPER HEART CHAMBERS (ATRIA), EVALUATION OF AORTA, CONGENITAL HEART DISEASE (CHD), INTRACARDIAC MASS.
>
> 18. **TEE — hazards** — ESOPHAGEAL PERFORATION (hole in the esophagus); BLEEDING; INCREASE IN METHEMOGLOBIN LEVELS from numbing agents (lidocaine, etc.); INCREASED RISK OF PULMONARY ASPIRATION; INCREASED HEART RATE and HEART RHYTHM PROBLEMS; DIFFICULTY BREATHING.

## Topic 1: Physics of Ultrasound

An **echocardiogram (Echo)** is a type of ultrasound that creates a **real-time image of the cardiovascular system**.

Ultrasound uses **high-frequency sound waves, 20,000 Hz or more**, through a medium which are sent and received through a **transducer**. Ultrasound can be directed as a beam and focused.

As ultrasound passes through a medium, it obeys the laws of reflection and refraction.

Key terms:

- **Interface** — a surface formed when two media of different densities come together.
- **Refraction** — bending of sound from a straight path as it goes from one media to another.
- **Reflection** — sound that bounces back from an interface.

Reflection depends upon:

1. The degree of difference between the two media.
2. The closer to 90° the angle of the interface is to the transducer, the greater the sound that will be reflected.

---

## Topic 2: Echocardiography Overview

Echocardiography remains the **most commonly used and comprehensive cardiac imaging method**.

- **Non-invasive**
- **Inexpensive**

Two primary methods:

1. **Standard transthoracic echocardiography (TTE)**
2. **Transesophageal echocardiography (TEE)**

Echocardiography is based on standard ultrasound principles using high-frequency sound waves emitted from **piezoelectric crystals** housed in a **transducer**. Sound waves are emitted from the transducer and interact with internal structures, then reflected back to the transducer to produce an image.

The **transducer** consists of piezoelectric elements — many small, carefully arranged elements that are interconnected electronically. Each element is coupled to electrodes that transmit current to the crystals and record the voltage generated by the returning signals. The transducer **sends and receives** the ultrasound.

---

## Topic 3: 2-D (Two-Dimensional) Echocardiography

Two-dimensional imaging uses multiple emitting and receiving elements located in the transducer. It occurs rapidly to generate moving images and displays them on a screen for interpretation. Excellent for **basic cardiac morphology and functional assessment**.

Echo examinations start with transthoracic two-dimensional scanning from four standard **transducer positions**:

1. **Parasternal** — beside the sternum (3rd–4th left intercostal space)
2. **Apical** — at the apex of the heart (located via PMI)
3. **Subcostal** — below the ribs, upper abdomen
4. **Suprasternal** — in the suprasternal notch above the sternum

Rotation and positioning of the transducer can increase or help identify specific anatomy of the heart.

---

## Topic 4: Patient Positions for Echo

| Views | Patient Position |
|-------|-----------------|
| Parasternal and Apical | Left lateral decubitus |
| Subcostal and Suprasternal notch | Supine |

From each transducer position, cross-sectional (tomographic) views of the heart may be obtained by rotating or angling the transducer.

---

## Topic 5: Parasternal Long Axis View

The parasternal long-axis view represents a **sagittal or coronal section** of the heart, bisecting it from the base to the apex.

- The transducer is placed at the **3rd or 4th intercostal space**; the ultrasound beam is **parallel with a line joining the right shoulder to the left flank**
- Structures visible: **aortic root**, **aortic valve leaflets**, **mitral valve leaflets**, and **chordal/papillary muscle attachments**

To obtain the **RV inflow long-axis view**: tilt the transducer **inferomedially** (pointing down) and rotate slightly clockwise → visualizes the **RV and RA**; good for identifying **tricuspid valve regurgitation**.

---

## Topic 6: Parasternal Short Axis View

The short-axis view is **perpendicular to the long axis view** — like slicing bread.

Obtained by rotating the transducer **clockwise** from the long axis; the transducer notch is pointed **superiorly toward the right supraclavicular fossa**.

Short-axis levels (obtained by tilting the transducer superiorly or inferiorly):

| Level | Structures Seen |
|-------|----------------|
| Aortic valve level | Aortic valve leaflets, right ventricular outflow tract |
| Mitral valve level (Base/Basal) | Mitral valve — **"Fish Lips"** appearance |
| Papillary muscle level (Mid) | Papillary muscles, LV walls |
| Apical level | Apical walls of the LV |

**"Fish Lips"** — the appearance of the open mitral valve leaflets in the parasternal short axis view at the basal level.

---

## Topic 7: Apical Views

The **point of maximal impulse (PMI)** is located by palpation and used as the starting point for apical views. Images are displayed as if viewing from the **apex up through to the base**. Patient remains in the **left lateral decubitus position**.

**Apical Four-Chamber View:**
- Visualizes all **four chambers simultaneously**
- Greatest benefit: identifying the **relative sizes of the ventricles**
- Apex is closest to the transducer; atria are furthest away

**Apical Long-Axis (Five-Chamber) View:**
- Slight **clockwise rotation** adds the **aortic root and aortic valve**
- Identifies the **aortic outflow tract**

**Apical Two-Chamber View:**
- Further clockwise rotation

All three apical views are **essential for analysis of regional myocardial contractility** — particularly beneficial for stress echocardiography.

---

## Topic 8: Subcostal Position

In patients with **COPD or emphysema**, hyperinflated lungs obliterate the standard ultrasonic window. The subcostal position is used to avoid imaging interference.

- Transducer placed in the **midline or slightly to the patient's right**, with the groove pointed toward the patient's **spine**
- Tilting inferiorly with the transducer head toward the patient's right visualizes:
  - **Liver parenchyma**
  - **Hepatic vessels**
  - **Inferior vena cava (IVC)**
- Combined with **color Doppler** → helps identify regurgitation and **right heart dysfunction**

---

## Topic 9: Suprasternal View

Transducer head is positioned in the **suprasternal notch** for visualization of the aortic arch.

- **Primary use:** examine the **great vessels**
- Long-axis view: ascending aorta and arch
- Short-axis view: rotate clockwise so the notch faces **posteriorly toward the patient's trachea**
- May be **uncomfortable** on the patient's throat

Structures visualized:

- Ascending aorta
- Aortic arch
- Brachiocephalic vessels
- Descending thoracic aorta

---

## Topic 10: M-Mode

M-Mode (motion mode) is used for the **measurement of dimensions** and is essential for the display of **subtle motion abnormalities** of specific cardiac structures.

- Records **detailed motions** of cardiac structures
- **Vertical axis** = distance (depth)
- **Horizontal axis** = time
- Can only be measured through 2-dimensional imaging

Uses:

- Detection of **valvular dysfunction** — primarily mitral and aortic
- Assessment of changes in **ventricular dimensions**

---

## Topic 11: Doppler Effect and Doppler Echocardiography

The **Doppler effect** — the increase in sound frequency as a sound source moves toward the observer and the decrease in frequency as the source moves away.

In the circulatory system, the moving target is **red blood cells**.

Doppler echocardiography measures **blood flow velocities** in the heart and great vessels.

- Blood cells moving **toward** the transducer → **higher** frequency
- Blood cells moving **away** from the transducer → **lower** frequency

---

## Topic 12: Color Flow Imaging and BART

Color flow imaging is based on **pulsed wave Doppler** principles (single transducer crystal fires and receives a sound wave).

The echo machine automatically assigns colors to different velocities and directions of blood flow:

**BART** mnemonic:
- **B**lue = **A**way from the transducer
- **R**ed = **T**owards the transducer
- **Green** = **Turbulent** flow

---

## Topic 13: Contrast Studies (Bubble Study)

**Contrast echocardiography** — also called a **bubble study** — is primarily used to identify **intracardiac shunts**.

- **Agitated saline** produces small bubbles visible on ultrasound
- Primarily used to identify atrial shunts through the **patent foramen ovale (PFO)**, which is a common finding
- If bubbles enter the right atrium and then appear in the **left atrium** shortly after, this indicates an **atrial septal defect (ASD)**

---

## Topic 14: Stress Echocardiography

The most common indication is evaluation of **myocardial ischemia** or **coronary artery disease (CAD)**.

Done **pre and post stress**. Some echo machines allow **side-by-side comparison** of pre and post stress recordings.

Stress modalities:

| Modality | Description |
|----------|-------------|
| Treadmill exercise test | Standard exercise protocol |
| Upright or supine bicycle | Allows imaging during exercise |
| Pharmacologic agent | **Dobutamine** is the most common agent |

Exercise-induced **regional wall motion abnormalities** can be detected **a few minutes after exercise**. These abnormalities are due to **ischemia** causing **abnormal contractility**.

---

## Topic 15: Transesophageal Echocardiogram (TEE) — Procedure

TEE is a **semi-invasive** procedure using a **modified gastroesophageal endoscopy probe** with an ultrasound transducer at the tip, placed into the esophagus.

Procedure:

- Patient is in the **left lateral decubitus** position
- Patient is **lightly sedated** and given **topical numbing agents** (e.g., lidocaine) before insertion
- **Fasting is required** — higher likelihood of pulmonary aspiration
- Careful guidance is needed to avoid **tracheal intubation** during esophageal passage

---

## Topic 16: TEE — Indications

| Category | Indication |
|----------|------------|
| **Primary** | Source of embolism (from atrial fibrillation) |
| **Primary** | Atrial fibrillation |
| Other | Suspected endocarditis |
| Other | Valvular diseases |
| Other | Evaluation of the upper heart chambers (atria) |
| Other | Evaluation of aorta |
| Other | Congenital heart disease (CHD) |
| Other | Intracardiac mass |

---

## Topic 17: TEE — Hazards

| Hazard | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Esophageal perforation** | Hole in the esophagus |
| Bleeding | From esophageal trauma |
| Elevated methemoglobin | From topical numbing agents (lidocaine, etc.) |
| Pulmonary aspiration | Increased risk — fasting required |
| Heart rate/rhythm changes | Arrhythmias during probe passage |
| Difficulty breathing | From sedation or probe position |

## Key Terms and Mnemonics to Remember

- **Ultrasound frequency:** 20,000 Hz OR MORE.
- **Interface** = two media of different densities meeting. **Refraction** = bending of sound path. **Reflection** = sound bouncing back from interface.
- **90° rule:** closer to 90° = greater reflection.
- **TTE** = non-invasive, from chest wall. **TEE** = semi-invasive, probe in esophagus.
- **4 transducer positions:** Parasternal · Apical · Subcostal · Suprasternal.
- **Patient positions:** LEFT LATERAL DECUBITUS (parasternal + apical) · SUPINE (subcostal + suprasternal).
- **Parasternal long axis:** beam parallel to line from right shoulder to left flank; views AV, MV, LV.
- **RV inflow long axis:** tilt inferomedially + clockwise; views RV, RA; good for TR.
- **Parasternal short axis:** perpendicular to long axis (rotate clockwise); 4 levels — aortic, mitral (FISH LIPS), papillary, apical.
- **"FISH LIPS"** = open mitral valve in short axis, basal level.
- **PMI** = starting landmark for APICAL views.
- **Apical 4-chamber** = all four chambers; best for ventricular size comparison.
- **Apical 5-chamber (long axis)** = adds aortic outflow tract.
- **All 3 apical views** = essential for regional myocardial contractility (stress echo).
- **Subcostal** = used when COPD/emphysema obliterates ultrasonic window; shows liver, hepatic vessels, IVC.
- **Suprasternal** = great vessels (ascending aorta, arch, brachiocephalic, descending aorta).
- **M-Mode:** vertical = depth · horizontal = time · used for dimensions and subtle valve motion.
- **BART:** BLUE = AWAY · RED = TOWARD · GREEN = TURBULENT.
- **Bubble study:** agitated saline → PFO/ASD detection; bubbles in LA after RA = ASD.
- **Stress echo:** most common indication = ischemia/CAD; pharmacologic agent = DOBUTAMINE; wall motion abnormalities appear minutes after exercise.
- **TEE:** left lateral decubitus · sedation + numbing agent · fasting required · primary indication = source of embolism (AFib).
- **TEE most serious hazard:** ESOPHAGEAL PERFORATION.
