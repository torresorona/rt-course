> 1. **Importance of cardiopulmonary rehabilitation** — exercise training was introduced AFTER THE 1950s, undoing the restricted physical activity that had been prescribed after cardiac surgeries. Key benefits: 15% REDUCTION in mortality and recurrent MYOCARDIAL INFARCTIONS in patients who exercise. Patients also receive counseling to improve PSYCHOLOGICAL WELL-BEING, REDUCED CIGARETTE SMOKING, and increased adherence to MEDICATIONS AND DIET.
>
> 2. **VO2 max** — the MAXIMAL AMOUNT OF OXYGEN that an individual can transport during exercise before being limited by FATIGUE OR DYSPNEA. An EASILY REPRODUCIBLE TEST that allows the healthcare provider to indirectly measure MAXIMAL CARDIAC PUMP CAPACITY. VO2 is DIRECTLY RELATED TO Q (CARDIAC OUTPUT) AND SV (STROKE VOLUME).
>
> 3. **Exercise and VO2 max** — exercise has a DIRECT LINK to an individual's VO2 max. During cardiac rehabilitation, regular exercise training INCREASES VO2 MAX and also increases VO2 SUBMAXIMAL, which DECREASES DYSPNEA AND FATIGUE during everyday activities.
>
> 4. **Cardiac rehabilitation phases** — divided into THREE PHASES based on the patient's clinical status. Activities include walking, jogging, and basic light weights and resistance training for about 20 MINUTES, 3 TIMES A WEEK, throughout the different phases. PHASE I: INPATIENT programs started SOON AFTER THE ACUTE CARDIAC EVENT OR INTERVENTION; usually performed by PHYSICAL THERAPY in the hospital. PHASE II: PHYSICIAN-SUPERVISED OUTPATIENT programs after discharge; patients exercise THREE TIMES WEEKLY for 3–4 MONTHS with follow-ups and ECG MONITORING. PHASE III: NON-ECG MONITORED MAINTENANCE programs; usually performed at HEALTH CLUBS OR GYMS.
>
> 5. **Tilt-table testing — purpose** — purpose is to identify patients who have a VASODEPRESSOR OR CARDIO-INHIBITORY RESPONSE as a cause of SYNCOPE (passing out when standing too quickly due to a heart condition or vasovagal response). ORTHOSTATIC HYPOTENSION is one of the PRIMARY CAUSES of syncope while standing up and can be determined by tilt-table testing.
>
> 6. **Tilt-table testing — procedure** — patient is placed on a tilt table in the SUPINE POSITION. The table is tilted upright to a maximum of 60–80 DEGREES for 20–45 MINUTES. If there is NO REACTION: INTRAVENOUS ISOPROTERENOL administered as a BOLUS may provoke syncope; causes a VASODEPRESSOR RESPONSE (DECREASE IN HEART RATE AND BLOOD PRESSURE).
>
> 7. **Hemodynamic catheters — overview** — three common indwelling catheters: ARTERIAL LINE (A-LINE), CENTRAL VENOUS PRESSURE (CVP) CATHETER, and PULMONARY ARTERY PRESSURE (PAP) CATHETER also known as SWAN-GANZ CATHETER. Patients requiring these typically have ADVANCED CARDIAC DISEASE, ADVANCED LUNG DISEASE, or MASSIVE TRAUMA.
>
> 8. **Arterial lines (A-lines)** — used for: obtaining ARTERIAL BLOOD GASES (ABGs); CONTINUOUSLY MONITORING BLOOD PRESSURE. Usually placed in the RADIAL OR FEMORAL ARTERY.
>
> 9. **CVP catheter** — measures the pressure as blood fills the RIGHT SIDE OF THE HEART from the central veins (superior/inferior vena cava). ASSESSES RIGHT-SIDED HEART FUNCTION. Access begins in large central vessels — usually the JUGULAR VEIN OR SUBCLAVIAN VEIN (under the clavicle). Also called a CENTRAL LINE. Tip ends in the RIGHT ATRIUM. Can also DELIVER DRUGS AND FLUIDS (similar to an IV). Three types: PICC (PERIPHERALLY INSERTED CENTRAL CATHETER) — placed in a VEIN IN THE ARM; TUNNELED CATHETER — surgically placed in a vein in the CHEST OR NECK and passed UNDER THE SKIN; IMPLANTED PORT — similar to tunneled but placed ENTIRELY UNDER THE SKIN.
>
> 10. **Swan-Ganz (PAP) catheter** — also called a BALLOON-TIPPED FLOW-DIRECTED CATHETER. Sits within the PULMONARY ARTERY. Can measure MORE THAN ONE PRESSURE (CVP/PAP/PCWP). Port colors: BLUE = CVP/RAP; YELLOW = PAP; RED = PCWP. Helpful in identifying: SHOCK (cardiogenic, hypovolemic, septic), LEFT VENTRICULAR FAILURE, MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION (MI), PULMONARY VASCULAR DISEASE, PULMONARY EDEMA, ARDS.
>
> 11. **Strain-gauge pressure transducer** — converts pressures measured by catheters into INTERPRETABLE NUMBERS on the vitals monitor. Physical STRAIN ON THE DIAPHRAGM changes the amount of ELECTRICITY flowing through the wires; by measuring changes in ELECTRICAL FLOW, there is an INDIRECT MEASUREMENT OF PRESSURE.
>
> 12. **MAP — normal values and evaluation** — measured via A-LINE. Normal: SYSTOLIC 90–140 mmHg; DIASTOLIC 60–90 mmHg; MAP 80–100 mmHg. HYPERTENSION: ABP > 140/90 mmHg OR MAP > 100 mmHg — causes: PAIN, ANXIETY, FLUID OVERLOAD. HYPOTENSION: ABP < 90/60 mmHg OR MAP < 80 mmHg — causes: PUMP FAILURE (CHF), FLUID LOSS (HEMORRHAGE/HYPOVOLEMIA).
>
> 13. **CVP** — measures RIGHT ATRIAL PRELOAD — the pressure as blood fills the right side of the heart. PRELOAD is the pressure that stretches the chamber walls as blood fills. Measured by CVP catheter or Swan-Ganz catheter.
>
> 14. **PCWP** — helpful in discriminating between CARDIOGENIC and NON-CARDIOGENIC PULMONARY EDEMA (e.g., ARDS). DANGEROUS — requires INFLATING A BALLOON within the pulmonary artery; if not deflated, causes TURBULENT FLOW and INCREASED LIKELIHOOD OF CLOTTING.
>
> 15. **Cardiac output (CO)** — CO = SV × HR (STROKE VOLUME × HEART RATE). Stroke volume is the VOLUME OF BLOOD PUMPED OUT OF THE LEFT VENTRICLE per beat. CO is measured in L/MIN. Stroke volume can be measured with an ECHOCARDIOGRAM.
>
> 16. **Thermodilution method for CO** — Swan-Ganz catheters calculate CO via thermodilution. A solution of 10 mL DEXTROSE (D5W) OR SALINE is injected; travels through the NORMAL PATHWAY OF THE HEART; because it is COOLER THAN BLOOD, a TEMPERATURE DIFFERENCE is recorded; this difference is used to calculate CO.
>
> 17. **Cardiac index (CI)** — CI = CO ÷ BSA (CARDIAC OUTPUT ÷ BODY SURFACE AREA). Used to determine if the heart is supplying the body with ENOUGH OXYGEN AND NUTRIENTS. A MORE ACCURATE INDICATOR of cardiac function than CO alone.
>
> 18. **SVR and PVR formulas** — SVR = [(MAP − CVP) ÷ CO] × 80. PVR = [(mean PAP − PCWP) ÷ CO] × 80. The factor 80 is a CORRECTION FACTOR that converts mmHg into DYNES·SEC/CM⁵ — the standard unit for vascular resistance. SVR measures resistance THROUGH THE BODY (systemic); PVR measures resistance THROUGH THE LUNGS (pulmonary).
>

## Topic 1: Importance of Cardiopulmonary Rehabilitation

Exercise training was introduced **after the 1950s**, undoing the restricted physical activity previously prescribed after cardiac surgeries.

Benefits of cardiac rehabilitation:
- **15% reduction** in mortality and recurrent myocardial infarctions
- Counseling to improve **psychological well-being**
- **Reduced cigarette smoking**
- Increased adherence to **medications and diet**

---

## Topic 2: VO2 Max

**VO2 max** — the maximal amount of oxygen an individual can transport during exercise before being limited by **fatigue or dyspnea**.

- An **easily reproducible test** for indirectly measuring **maximal cardiac pump capacity**
- VO2 is **directly related to Q (cardiac output) and SV (stroke volume)**

**Exercise and VO2 max:**
- Exercise has a **direct link** to VO2 max
- Regular exercise training **increases VO2 max**
- Also increases **VO2 submaximal** → **decreases dyspnea and fatigue** during everyday activities

---

## Topic 3: Cardiac Rehabilitation Phases

Three phases based on clinical status. Activities: walking, jogging, light weights/resistance training — approximately **20 minutes, 3 times per week**.

- **Phase I** — INPATIENT; started soon after the acute cardiac event or intervention; usually by **physical therapy** in the hospital
- **Phase II** — PHYSICIAN-SUPERVISED OUTPATIENT after discharge; **3 times weekly for 3–4 months** with follow-ups and **ECG monitoring**
- **Phase III** — NON-ECG MONITORED MAINTENANCE; usually at **health clubs or gyms**

---

## Topic 4: Tilt-Table Testing

**Purpose:** identify patients who have a **vasodepressor or cardio-inhibitory response** as a cause of **syncope** (passing out when standing too quickly).

**Orthostatic hypotension** is one of the primary causes of syncope while standing — can be determined by tilt-table testing.

**Procedure:**
1. Patient placed on tilt table in the **supine position**
2. Table tilted upright to maximum **60–80 degrees** for **20–45 minutes**
3. If **no reaction**: **IV isoproterenol** administered as a bolus to provoke syncope
   - Causes a **vasodepressor response** (decrease in heart rate and blood pressure)

---

## Topic 5: Hemodynamic Catheters

Three common indwelling catheters used for hemodynamic monitoring:
1. **Arterial Line (A-line)**
2. **Central Venous Pressure (CVP) Catheter**
3. **Pulmonary Artery Pressure (PAP) Catheter** — also known as Swan-Ganz Catheter

Patients requiring these catheters typically have **advanced cardiac disease, advanced lung disease, or massive trauma**.

---

## Topic 6: Arterial Lines (A-Lines)

Used for:
- Obtaining **arterial blood gases (ABGs)**
- **Continuously monitoring blood pressure**

Usually placed in the **radial or femoral artery**.

---

## Topic 7: CVP Catheter

Measures the pressure as blood fills the **right side of the heart** from the central veins (superior/inferior vena cava). Assesses **right-sided heart function**.

- Access begins in large central vessels — usually the **jugular vein or subclavian vein** (under the clavicle)
- Also called a **Central Line**
- Tip ends in the **right atrium**
- Can also **deliver drugs and fluids** (similar to an IV)

Three types:
- **PICC** — placed in a **vein in the arm**
- **Tunneled catheter** — surgically placed in a vein in the **chest or neck**, passed **under the skin**
- **Implanted port** — similar to tunneled, but placed **entirely under the skin**

---

## Topic 8: Swan-Ganz (PAP) Catheter

Also called a **balloon-tipped flow-directed catheter**. Sits within the **pulmonary artery** and can measure **more than one pressure** (CVP/PAP/PCWP).

Port colors:
- **Blue** = CVP / RAP (Right Atrial Pressure)
- **Yellow** = PAP (Pulmonary Arterial Pressure)
- **Red** = PCWP (Pulmonary Capillary Wedge Pressure)

Helpful for identifying: shock (cardiogenic, hypovolemic, septic), left ventricular failure, MI, pulmonary vascular disease, pulmonary edema, ARDS.

---

## Topic 9: Strain-Gauge Pressure Transducer

Converts pressures measured by catheters into **interpretable numbers** on the vitals monitor.

- Physical **strain on the diaphragm** changes the amount of **electricity** flowing through the wires
- By measuring changes in **electrical flow**, there is an **indirect measurement of pressure**

---

## Topic 10: MAP — Normal Values and Evaluation

Measured via A-line.

| Measurement | Normal Range |
|------------|-------------|
| Systolic arterial BP | 90–140 mmHg |
| Diastolic arterial BP | 60–90 mmHg |
| MAP | 80–100 mmHg |

**Hypertension:** ABP > 140/90 mmHg or MAP > 100 mmHg — causes: pain, anxiety, fluid overload.

**Hypotension:** ABP < 90/60 mmHg or MAP < 80 mmHg — causes: pump failure (CHF), fluid loss (hemorrhage/hypovolemia).

---

## Topic 11: CVP and PCWP

**CVP — Central Venous Pressure:**
- Measures **right atrial preload** — pressure as blood fills the right side of the heart
- **Preload** = pressure stretching the chamber walls as blood fills
- Measured by CVP catheter or Swan-Ganz catheter

**PCWP — Pulmonary Capillary Wedge Pressure:**
- Helpful in discriminating **cardiogenic vs. non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema** (e.g., ARDS)
- Requires **inflating a balloon** within the pulmonary artery
- **Dangerous** if not deflated — causes turbulent flow and **increased likelihood of clotting**

---

## Topic 12: Cardiac Output (CO)

**CO = SV × HR** (Stroke Volume × Heart Rate)

- Stroke volume = volume of blood pumped out of the **left ventricle** per beat
- CO measured in **L/min**
- Stroke volume can be measured with an **echocardiogram**

**Thermodilution method:**
- 10 mL of **dextrose (D5W) or saline** injected through the Swan-Ganz catheter
- Solution travels through the **normal pathway of the heart**
- Because it is **cooler than blood**, a **temperature difference** is recorded
- Temperature difference is used to **calculate CO**

---

## Topic 13: Cardiac Index (CI) and Vascular Resistance

**CI = CO ÷ BSA** (Cardiac Output ÷ Body Surface Area)

- Used to determine if the heart is supplying the body with **enough oxygen and nutrients**
- A **more accurate indicator** of cardiac function than CO alone

**SVR and PVR:**

- SVR = [(MAP − CVP) ÷ CO] × 80
- PVR = [(mean PAP − PCWP) ÷ CO] × 80

The factor **80** converts mmHg into **dynes·sec/cm⁵** — the standard unit for vascular resistance.
