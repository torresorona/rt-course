> 1. **Cardiac catheterization overview** — cardiac catheterization is a procedure that examines the heart by passing a catheter through an artery — USUALLY THE FEMORAL ARTERY — to examine structures of the heart. It is considered primarily a DIAGNOSTIC STUDY, though therapeutic interventions may also be performed. The MOST COMMON INDICATION is identification of CAD and assessment of its EXTENT and SEVERITY. Different catheters are selected based on the disease process expected; each is introduced via a GUIDEWIRE that remains in place for rapid exchange.
>
> 2. **Equipment** — five main categories: FLUOROSCOPY (real-time radiographic imaging), PHYSIOLOGIC MONITORING (ECG, invasive and noninvasive blood pressure), STERILE TABLE SUPPLIES (catheters, sheath insertion device, sterile drapes, heparinized saline), IMAGING FOR VASCULAR ACCESS (ultrasound), and EMERGENCY CART AND DEFIBRILLATOR.
>
> 3. **Judkins technique — femoral access** — the patient is placed on a flat table with a FLUOROSCOPE capable of a 360° VIEW. JUDKINS TECHNIQUE: percutaneous entry into the FEMORAL ARTERY. Steps: palpate for the INGUINAL LIGAMENT; measure 1 TO 3 CM (ONE OR TWO FINGERS) BELOW the inguinal ligament; make a small NICK in the skin above the vessel; introduce the catheter via guidewire. FEMORAL ARTERIAL ACCESS is the MOST COMMON route.
>
> 4. **Coronary angiography — overview** — the GOLD STANDARD for evaluating the anatomy of the coronary artery tree and diagnosing CAD. Normally a SAME-DAY PROCEDURE. The MOST WIDELY USED INVASIVE PROCEDURE in cardiovascular medicine. Can be done through FEMORAL or RADIAL arteries: FEMORAL has a SLIGHTLY LONGER RECOVERY PERIOD; RADIAL REDUCES TOTAL RECOVERY TIME but limits catheter size (only SMALLER CATHETERS). Procedure: DIRECT INJECTION of RADIOPAQUE CONTRAST into the coronary arteries; contrast enhances x-ray absorption; catheter guided UP THE AORTA to the coronary artery; contrast injected; images captured on HIGH-RESOLUTION DIGITAL RADIOGRAPHIC systems.
>
> 5. **Coronary angiography — indications** — primary: STABLE ISCHEMIC CAD, UNSTABLE ANGINA/NSTEMI, POST-REVASCULARIZATION ISCHEMIA, POST-STEMI. Others: initial diagnostic test for stable ischemic heart disease; survived sudden cardiac death or lethal arrhythmia; CHF patients with worsening symptoms (LV DYSFUNCTION > 50%); exercise testing showing ST-SEGMENT CHANGES, ANGINA, or WALL DYSFUNCTION on non-invasive echo.
>
> 6. **Coronary angiography — contraindications** — there are NO ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATIONS to coronary angiography. Relative risk factors requiring risk/benefit analysis include: ACTIVE BLEEDING, ABNORMAL COAGULOPATHY (ELEVATED INR), FEVER, and others.
>
> 7. **Catheter types** — JUDKINS RIGHT (JR) is shaped to access the RIGHT CORONARY ARTERY. JUDKINS LEFT (JL) is shaped to access the LEFT CORONARY ARTERY. PIGTAIL allows LARGER VOLUMES OF CONTRAST and is placed in the LEFT VENTRICLE to evaluate WALL MOTION and CARDIAC OUTPUT. Judkins catheters between 5F AND 6F are standard for diagnostic arteriography.
>
> 8. **Patient preparation and contrast agents** — conscious sedation: FENTANYL and MIDAZOLAM (VERSED). HEPARIN anticoagulation. NITROGLYCERIN may be used to dilate vessels in active coronary event patients. Contrast agents: injected to enhance x-ray absorption, producing sharp contrast with surrounding tissue; typically contain IODINE, which MAY CAUSE AN ALLERGIC REACTION in some patients.
>
> 9. **Assessing the lesion** — the composition, distribution, and location of atherosclerotic plaque are UNIQUE TO EACH PATIENT. Every patient has a unique coronary tree, which can complicate stenting or atherectomy.
>
> 10. **Cardiac electrophysiology (EPS)** — INVASIVE ELECTROPHYSIOLOGY STUDIES use a multi-lead catheter with electrodes to RECORD OR STIMULATE cardiac electrical activity. Performed by an ELECTROPHYSIOLOGIST. Has both DIAGNOSTIC AND THERAPEUTIC functions. Effective at INITIATING VT AND SVT when these have occurred spontaneously. A diagnostic EPS can: measure specific electrical dysfunctions (e.g., THIRD-DEGREE AV BLOCK), INITIATE ARRHYTHMIAS (SVT, tachycardia), and help determine if a patient requires a PACEMAKER OR CARDIOVERSION DEVICE.
>
> 11. **Cardiac mapping** — records ELECTRICAL POTENTIALS from the heart and displays them spatially as ACTIVATION TIMES AND ELECTRICAL AMPLITUDES of myocardial tissue. Displayed as a COLOR CHART. Effective at identifying FOCI causing SVT, WOLFF-PARKINSON-WHITE (WPW) syndrome, and SCAR TISSUE.
>
> 12. **Percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI)** — relieves symptoms and reduces risk of mortality and subsequent MIs. MORTALITY RATE < 1%. Complications: risk of RESTENOSIS of a stent, THROMBOSIS, and PERFORATION of the coronary artery.
>
> 13. **Balloon angioplasty** — expands the coronary lumen by STRETCHING AND TEARING the atherosclerotic plaque and vessel wall. Does NOT REMOVE atherosclerosis — it DISPLACES it to the SIDES of the vessel wall. RESTENOSIS IS COMMON. Rare: arterial rebound and vessel swelling causing occlusion WORSE than initial stenosis.
>
> 14. **Stents** — used in 90% OF PCI PROCEDURES WORLDWIDE; the PREDOMINANT FORM OF PCI. Tube-shaped devices that maintain VESSEL INTEGRITY AND PATENCY. Deployed on a BALLOON CATHETER and expanded within the stenosis. Restenosis in only ~1% of cases. Some stents are DRUG-IMPREGNATED to prevent thrombosis and plaque development.
>
> 15. **Coronary atherectomy** — REMOVAL (rather than displacement) of obstructing atherosclerotic plaque by GRINDING it. Allows a LARGER FINAL MINIMAL LUMEN DIAMETER than balloon angioplasty alone. Most commonly used device: ROTABLATOR ROTATIONAL ATHERECTOMY SYSTEM. Complication: DISTAL BLOCKAGES as plaque is broken away.
>
> 16. **Ablation** — DESTROYS myocardial tissue by delivering energy through a catheter electrode placed on the myocardium. Two types: RADIOFREQUENCY (RF) — generates HEAT through the catheter tip; CRYO — destroys tissue through FREEZING. Used for: FOCI causing SVT/arrhythmias; BRIDGING TISSUE between scar tissue sustaining REENTRANT ARRHYTHMIAS.
>
> 17. **Radionuclide studies** — also called RADIONUCLIDE ANGIOGRAPHY or BLOOD POOL IMAGING. Provides imaging to detect EJECTION FRACTIONS and CHAMBER VOLUMES. A RADIONUCLIDE (ISOTOPE) is a radioactive chemical that emits radiation (usually GAMMA) detectable by a GAMMA CAMERA. Isotopes have an EXCESS NUMBER OF PROTONS OR NEUTRONS giving them excess nuclear energy. Most common isotope: TECHNETIUM-99 (99mTc). Gamma camera can ROTATE AROUND THE PATIENT producing Anterior-Posterior, Left Anterior Oblique, and Lateral views. Data recorded synchronized with the R WAVE of the ECG. Can identify: SIZE OF HEART CHAMBERS AND GREAT VESSELS, REGIONAL WALL MOTION, GLOBAL FUNCTION, VENTRICULAR WALL THICKNESS, PERICARDIAL EFFUSION, PERICARDIAL FAT PAD, PARACARDIAC MASS.
>
> 18. **Synchronized cardioversion** — shock timed to the R WAVE to convert a fast, irregular rhythm to a regular rhythm. Synchronizing function noted as a 'TIC' MARK ON THE R WAVE. Delivered at a PREDETERMINED OPTIMAL MOMENT. Indications: HEMODYNAMICALLY SIGNIFICANT SVT (narrow complex), ATRIAL FIBRILLATION, ATRIAL FLUTTER, VENTRICULAR TACHYCARDIA WITH PULSE. PULSELESS VT AND VF are treated with UNSYNCHRONIZED SHOCKS — called DEFIBRILLATION, NOT cardioversion.
>
> 19. **Cardioversion procedure** — preparation: verify indication; explain to awake patient; remove clothing from upper body; REMOVE NITROGLYCERIN PASTE OR PATCHES and wipe residue; remove excessive hair; DO NOT apply alcohol, tincture of benzoin, or antiperspirant; print ECG strip; ensure suction and emergency medications available; give OXYGEN and start IV. Delivering the shock: press SYNC control; select lead with OPTIMUM QRS AMPLITUDE and no artifact; confirm SENSE MARKERS APPEAR ON THE R WAVE (NOT the T wave); if misplaced, ADJUST ECG SIZE OR SELECT ANOTHER LEAD; administer SEDATION per protocol; confirm SYNC MODE; select energy level; charge defibrillator; place pads; call "CLEAR!"; hold both discharge buttons until shock delivers; SLIGHT DELAY while machine detects next R wave; reassess rhythm and patient; confirm SYNC MODE is still active before additional shocks. If rhythm deteriorates to pulseless VT/VF: TURN SYNC OFF and DEFIBRILLATE.
>
> 20. **Pacemakers and ICDs** — PACEMAKERS deliver LOW-VOLTAGE pacing pulses for SYMPTOMATIC BRADYCARDIA or ANTI-TACHYCARDIA PACING (reentrant tachycardias). ICDs deliver HIGH-VOLTAGE shock pulses for atrial fibrillation and ventricular fibrillation. PRIMARY PACEMAKER INDICATION: relieve or prevent SYMPTOMATIC BRADYCARDIA (also for documented asymptomatic bradycardia at higher risk of becoming symptomatic). ICD INDICATION: prevention of SUDDEN DEATH FROM VT/VF; patients who have had SUSTAINED VT/VF.
>
> 21. **Cardiac electrical stimulation types** — STANDARD PACING: low voltage for bradycardia; a stimulus that successfully stimulates local myocardium is said to have CAPTURED it. ANTITACHYCARDIA PACING (ATP): higher voltage; delivered during the RELATIVE REFRACTORY PERIOD. DEFIBRILLATION: highest energy to convert VF/VT.
>
> 22. **Pacemaker hardware** — leads have common cable structures inserted into a HEADER. BIPOLAR LEADS are used more frequently — better sensing → better detection. Generator: CLEAR PLASTIC HEADER (where leads attach); TITANIUM CASING ("can") housing BATTERY, RAM/ROM MEMORY, MICROPROCESSOR — essentially a TINY COMPUTER in the patient's chest.
>
> 23. **Sensing, detection, and oversensing** — SENSING: the instant the device DETECTS ATRIAL OR VENTRICULAR DEPOLARIZATION. DETECTION: the SOFTWARE ALGORITHM that deciphers sensing data to CLASSIFY THE RHYTHM and determine appropriate treatment. OVERSENSING: device detects a signal that is NOT a true depolarization → may cause INAPPROPRIATE PACING OR SHOCK when no abnormal rhythm exists.
>
> 24. **Pacemaker interrogation checklist** — 10 items: 1. DEVICE AND MANUFACTURER; 2. CURRENT BATTERY VOLTAGE (note ERI voltage); 3. LAST FULL ENERGY CHARGE TIME for ICDs — NORMAL IS < 15 SECONDS; 4. LEADS — a) IMPEDANCE: normal 300–1,000 Ω (except high-impedance leads), b) SENSING: normal A > 1 mV, V > 5 mV, c) THRESHOLD: atrium 1V @ 0.4ms or less, ventricle 1–2V @ 0.4ms or less; 5. PERCENT OF TIME PACED AND SENSED in A and V; 6. UNDERLYING RHYTHM (test by decreasing pacing rate or temporarily suspending pacing); 7. MODE SWITCH EPISODES (pacemakers); 8. TACHY EVENTS (ICDs); 9. SENSING THRESHOLD SETTING (normal < 1/2 sensing capability); 10. PACING OUTPUT (should be set at 2× PACING THRESHOLD for voltage, 3× THRESHOLD for pulse duration). Noninvasive tools: REMOTE MANAGEMENT and SHORT-RANGE TELEMETRY WITH AN INDUCTIVE WAND.
>
> 25. **Precautions for device implantation** — damage to cardiac vasculature, CARDIAC PERFORATION, device infection (MRSA), LEAD DISPLACEMENT, STROKE.

## Topic 1: Cardiac Catheterization Overview

**Cardiac catheterization** is a procedure to examine the heart by passing a catheter through an artery — usually the **femoral artery** — to examine structures of the heart.

- Considered primarily a **diagnostic study**, though therapeutic interventions may be performed
- The most common indication is **identification of CAD and assessment of its extent and severity**
- Different catheters are selected based on the **type of disease process** expected (guided by echocardiography or CMRI)
- Each catheter is introduced via a **guidewire** that remains in place for rapid exchange

---

## Topic 2: Equipment

Five categories of equipment:

1. **Fluoroscopy (C-Arm)** — real-time radiographic imaging system; provides 360° view of the heart projected on a screen
2. **Physiologic monitoring** — ECG, invasive and noninvasive blood pressure
3. **Sterile table supplies** — catheters, sheath insertion device, sterile drapes and towels, heparinized saline
4. **Imaging for vascular access** — ultrasound
5. **Emergency cart and defibrillator**

---

## Topic 3: Judkins Technique (Femoral Access)

**Judkins technique** — percutaneous entry into the femoral artery:

1. Palpate for the **inguinal ligament**
2. Measure **1–3 cm (one or two fingers) below** the inguinal ligament
3. Make a small **nick in the skin** above the vessel
4. Puncture the femoral artery and introduce the catheter via guidewire

**Femoral arterial access is the most common route** for cardiac catheterization.

---

## Topic 4: Coronary Angiography

The **gold standard** for evaluating coronary artery anatomy and diagnosing CAD. Normally a **same-day procedure** and the most widely used **invasive procedure** in cardiovascular medicine.

Access routes:
- **Femoral** — slightly longer recovery period
- **Radial** — shorter recovery; limits catheter to smaller sizes

Procedure steps:
1. Direct injection of **radiopaque contrast** into the coronary arteries
2. Catheter guided **up the aorta** to the coronary artery ostium
3. Contrast injected; images captured on **high-resolution digital** systems

---

## Topic 5: Coronary Angiography — Indications

Primary indications:
- Stable ischemic CAD
- Unstable angina / NSTEMI
- Post-revascularization ischemia
- Post-STEMI

Other indications:
- Initial diagnostic test for stable ischemic heart disease
- Survived sudden cardiac death or lethal arrhythmia
- CHF with worsening symptoms (LV dysfunction > 50%)
- Exercise testing showing ST-segment changes, angina, or wall dysfunction on non-invasive echo

---

## Topic 6: Coronary Angiography — Contraindications and Catheter Types

**There are NO ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATIONS** to coronary angiography. The risk/benefit ratio must be considered for patients with active bleeding, elevated INR, fever, and others.

**Catheter types:**

- **Judkins Right (JR)** — shaped to access the **right coronary artery**
- **Judkins Left (JL)** — shaped to access the **left coronary artery**
- **Pigtail** — allows larger volumes of contrast; placed in the **left ventricle** to evaluate wall motion and cardiac output
- Standard size for diagnostic arteriography: **5F to 6F**

---

## Topic 7: Patient Preparation and Contrast Agents

**Conscious sedation:**
- Fentanyl
- Midazolam (Versed)

**Heparin** — anticoagulation

**Nitroglycerin** — used to dilate vessels in active coronary event patients

**Contrast agents:**
- Enhance x-ray absorption; produce sharp contrast with surrounding tissue
- Typically contain **iodine** — which may cause an **allergic reaction** in some patients

---

## Topic 8: Assessing the Lesion and Cardiac Electrophysiology

The composition, distribution, and location of atherosclerotic plaque are **unique to each patient**. Because every patient has a unique coronary tree, the lesion's location may complicate stenting or atherectomy.

**Invasive Electrophysiology Studies (EPS):**
- Use a multi-lead catheter with electrodes to **record or stimulate cardiac electrical activity**
- Performed by an **electrophysiologist** (a subspecialty cardiologist)
- Both **diagnostic and therapeutic** function
- Effective at **initiating VT and SVT** when these have occurred spontaneously

Diagnostic EPS can:
- Measure specific electrical dysfunctions (e.g., **third-degree AV block**)
- Initiate arrhythmias (SVT, tachycardia) for characterization
- Help determine if a patient requires a **pacemaker or cardioversion device**

---

## Topic 9: Cardiac Mapping

Records **electrical potentials** from the heart, depicted spatially as **activation times and electrical amplitudes** of myocardial tissue.

- A computer algorithm detects the electrical potential of the myocardium and how quickly muscle tissue is stimulated
- Displayed as a **color chart**
- Effective at identifying **foci causing SVT**, **Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW)**, and **scar tissue**

---

## Topic 10: Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI)

PCI provides relief of ischemic CAD symptoms and reduces risk of mortality and subsequent MIs.

- **Mortality rate < 1%**
- Complications: **restenosis** of a stent; **thrombosis**; **perforation** of the coronary artery

---

## Topic 11: Balloon Angioplasty

Expands the coronary lumen by **stretching and tearing** the atherosclerotic plaque and vessel wall.

- Does **not remove** atherosclerosis — it **displaces** it to the sides of the vessel wall
- **Restenosis is common**
- Rare: arterial rebound causing occlusion **worse than initial stenosis**

---

## Topic 12: Stents

- Used in **90% of PCI procedures worldwide** — the **predominant form of PCI**
- Tube-shaped devices that maintain **vessel integrity and patency**
- Deployed on a **balloon catheter** and expanded within the stenosis
- Restenosis in only **~1%** of cases
- Some stents are **drug-impregnated** to prevent thrombosis and plaque development

---

## Topic 13: Coronary Atherectomy and Ablation

**Atherectomy:**
- **Removes (grinds)** obstructing atherosclerotic plaque (vs. displacing it)
- Allows a **larger final minimal lumen diameter** than balloon angioplasty alone
- Most common device: **rotablator rotational atherectomy system**
- Complication: **distal blockages** as plaque is broken away

**Ablation:**
- **Destroys myocardial tissue** via energy delivered through a catheter electrode
- **Radiofrequency (RF)** — generates **heat** through the catheter tip
- **Cryo** — destroys tissue through **freezing**
- Used for: **foci causing SVT/arrhythmias**; **bridging tissue** between scar tissue sustaining **reentrant arrhythmias**

---

## Topic 14: Radionuclide Studies

Also called **radionuclide angiography** or **blood pool imaging**.

- Provides imaging to detect **ejection fractions** and **chamber volumes**
- **Radionuclide (isotope)** — radioactive chemical that emits radiation (usually **gamma**) detected by a **gamma camera**
- Isotopes have an **excess number of protons or neutrons**, giving them excess nuclear energy
- Most common isotope: **Technetium-99 (99mTc)**

**Radionuclide Ventriculography:**
- Gamma camera can **rotate around the patient**: Anterior-Posterior, Left Anterior Oblique, Lateral views
- Data synchronized with the **R wave** of the ECG

**What it can identify:**
- Size of heart chambers and great vessels
- Regional wall motion
- Global function (qualitative assessment)
- Ventricular wall thickness
- Pericardial effusion
- Pericardial fat pad
- Paracardiac mass

A **reduced EF** results in **blood pooling** within a ventricle, which is the basis of the term "blood pool imaging."

---

## Topic 15: Synchronized Cardioversion

Delivers a shock **timed to the R wave** to convert a fast, irregular rhythm to a regular rhythm.

- Synchronizing function is noted as a **'Tic' mark on the R wave**
- Delivered at a **predetermined optimal moment** (milliseconds after R wave detection)

**Indications:**
- Hemodynamically significant SVT (narrow complex)
- Atrial fibrillation
- Atrial flutter
- Ventricular tachycardia **with pulse**

> **Pulseless VT and VF** are treated with **unsynchronized shocks** — this is **defibrillation**, not cardioversion.

---

## Topic 16: Cardioversion Procedure

**Preparation:**
- Verify indication; explain procedure to awake patient
- Remove clothing from upper body
- Remove nitroglycerin paste/patches; wipe away residue
- Remove (shave) excessive hair from electrode sites
- Do **not** apply alcohol, tincture of benzoin, or antiperspirant
- Print ECG strip to document rhythm
- Ensure suction and emergency medications available; give oxygen; start IV

**Delivering the shock:**
1. Ensure defibrillator is on; apply defib gel (paddles) or adhesive pads
2. Press **"Sync"** control
3. Select lead with **optimum QRS amplitude** and no artifact
4. Confirm **sense markers appear on the R wave** — NOT on the T wave
5. If misplaced, **adjust ECG size or select another lead**
6. Administer **sedation** per protocol
7. Confirm **Sync mode**; select energy level; charge defibrillator
8. Place paddles/pads; call **"Clear!"** — everyone clear of patient and equipment; oxygen NOT flowing over chest
9. Press and **hold both discharge buttons** until shock is delivered
10. **Slight delay** occurs while the machine detects the next R wave
11. Reassess rhythm and patient; confirm **Sync mode still active** before additional shocks

> If rhythm deteriorates to **pulseless VT or VF**: **turn Sync OFF** and **defibrillate**.

---

## Topic 17: Pacemakers and ICDs

| Device | Voltage | Primary Use |
|--------|---------|-------------|
| **Pacemaker** | Low | Symptomatic bradycardia; anti-tachycardia pacing |
| **ICD** | High | VF/VT; sudden death prevention |

**Indications:**
- **Pacemaker:** relieve or prevent symptomatic bradycardia; also for documented asymptomatic bradycardia at higher risk of becoming symptomatic
- **ICD:** prevention of sudden death from VT/VF; patients who have had sustained VT/VF

**Types of cardiac electrical stimulation:**
- **Standard pacing** — low voltage for bradycardia; a stimulus that successfully stimulates local myocardium is said to have **captured** it
- **Antitachycardia pacing (ATP)** — higher voltage; delivered during the **relative refractory period**
- **Defibrillation** — highest energy to convert VF/VT

---

## Topic 18: Pacemaker Hardware

**Leads:**
- Have common cable structures inserted into a **"Header"**
- **Bipolar leads** used more frequently — better sensing → better detection

**Generator:**
- **Clear plastic header** where leads attach
- **Titanium casing ("can")** houses all electronics:
  - Battery
  - RAM/ROM memory
  - Microprocessor
  - Essentially a **tiny computer** in the patient's chest

---

## Topic 19: Sensing, Detection, and Oversensing

| Function | Definition |
|----------|-----------|
| **Sensing** | The instant the device detects atrial or ventricular depolarization has occurred |
| **Detection** | The software algorithm that deciphers sensing data to classify the rhythm and determine appropriate treatment |
| **Oversensing** | The device detects a signal that is NOT a true depolarization → may cause INAPPROPRIATE pacing or shock delivery |

---

## Topic 20: Pacemaker Interrogation Checklist

A complete standard interrogation includes 10 items:

1. Device and manufacturer
2. Current battery voltage (note ERI voltage)
3. Last full energy charge time (ICDs) — **normal < 15 seconds**
4. Leads:
   - Impedance — **normal 300–1,000 Ω** (except high-impedance leads)
   - Sensing — **normal A > 1 mV; V > 5 mV**
   - Threshold — **atrium: 1V @ 0.4 ms or less; ventricle: 1–2V @ 0.4 ms or less**
5. Percent of time paced and sensed in A and V
6. Underlying rhythm — test by decreasing pacing rate or temporarily suspending pacing
7. Mode switch episodes (pacemakers) — e.g., "none," "3," "frequent," "in progress"
8. Tachy events (ICDs)
9. Sensing threshold setting — **normal < 1/2 sensing capability**
10. Pacing output — **should be set at 2× pacing threshold for voltage; 3× threshold for pulse duration**

**Noninvasive interrogation tools:**
- Remote management of the pacemaker
- Short-range telemetry with an **inductive wand**

---

## Topic 21: Precautions for Device Implantation

- Damage to cardiac vasculature during placement
- **Cardiac perforation**
- Device infection — **MRSA**
- **Lead displacement**
- **Stroke**
